Research-Driven Development

Build software you understand.

RDD is a methodology for working deeply on complex software problems. It wraps BDD and TDD in a research layer that changes what you build — not just how you build it.

The premise: when you investigate a problem before committing to a solution, you discover that the problem isn't what you assumed. Stakeholders you hadn't considered reshape the requirements. Assumptions you'd never questioned turn out to be wrong. The system you build after research is a different system than the one you would have built without it — and it's better, because it reflects investigated reality rather than the first plausible story you told yourself.

RDD is slow on purpose. It's deep work. Understanding what you're doing is valuable on its own — not just as a hedge against maintenance costs, but because deep engagement with a problem changes how you think about it. Research reshapes the problem statement. Synthesis surfaces connections you didn't expect. The act of explaining what you've learned reveals what you haven't.

The Pipeline

RDD moves through phases. Each phase produces an artifact and ends at an epistemic gate — a moment where you must generate something (an explanation, a prediction, an articulation) rather than merely approve an output. These gates are where learning happens.

RESEARCH → PRODUCT → MODEL → DECIDE → ARCHITECT → BUILD → SYNTHESIS
  • Research — Investigate the problem space. Read the literature, run spikes, search for prior art. Produce a citation-audited essay. The research often reframes the problem in ways that change what you'd build.
  • Product Discovery — Surface who this is for, what they need, and what you're assuming. Invert every major assumption before it becomes architecture.
  • Domain Model — Name things precisely. Extract the vocabulary of concepts, actions, relationships, and invariants that the system must respect.
  • Decide — Make architectural decisions and write refutable behavior scenarios. Each decision traces to research; each scenario is testable.
  • Architect — Decompose the system into modules with provenance chains linking every design choice back to research and user needs.
  • Build — Turn scenarios into working software through BDD and TDD. The architecture prevents surprises; the scenarios prevent ambiguity.
  • Synthesis — Extract publishable insight from the work. The essay you write is the final act of discovery — writing toward a conclusion you can't yet state.

Core Principles

  1. Research before architecture. The system you build after investigating a problem is different from the one you'd build on assumptions. RDD is grounded in what research finds, not what you assumed going in.
  2. Understanding requires generation, not review. Every gate requires you to produce something — an explanation, prediction, or articulation — not merely assess an output. That's where learning happens.
  3. Assumptions are inverted before they harden. Every major product assumption gets systematically questioned — "what if this were wrong?" — before it's encoded in architecture.
  4. Provenance links design to research. Every module, every responsibility allocation, every architectural decision traces back to a research finding or a user need. When someone asks "why is it this way?", you can follow the chain.
  5. Understanding compounds. Each epistemic act builds on the last. Your response at the research gate enriches domain modeling; your modeling insights sharpen architectural decisions. The quality effect is cumulative.
  6. Epistemic cost stays lightweight. Each gate takes minutes, not hours. The methodology earns its keep or gets out of the way.
  7. The scaffolding fades. RDD is a deep work tool you reach for when a problem warrants it and put away when the knowledge has been absorbed. The project's native documentation is the permanent home; RDD artifacts are the working space.

The Test

After completing an RDD cycle, you should be able to explain the system's design rationale, key decisions, domain vocabulary, and the user needs those decisions serve — without assistance. Not because you memorized the output, but because you shaped the process and the result.

If you can do this, the methodology worked. If you can't, it failed — regardless of whether the software runs.

How RDD Differs

From agile: Agile optimizes for business delivery and discovers user value incidentally. RDD optimizes for understanding through research and discovers business value as a consequence of user alignment.

From BDD/TDD alone: BDD and TDD verify that the system does what was specified. RDD questions whether the specification reflects reality. The research and product discovery phases change what gets specified; BDD/TDD in the build phase ensures it gets built correctly.

From vibe coding: Vibe coding treats AI output as the product. RDD treats output as starting material for human understanding. The gap between generation and explanation is where learning happens.
