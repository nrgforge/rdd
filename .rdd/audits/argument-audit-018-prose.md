### P1 — Must Fix

**Issue 1.1 — "Single largest S-Small divergence point" is stated as a direct matrix finding; the Loop D matrix does not support "single largest" quantitatively**

- **Location:** §2, paragraph 2 ("RESEARCH is the methodology's single largest S-Small divergence point"); §7, paragraph 1 (repeated)
- **Claim:** The CRESS phase-by-phase audit establishes that RESEARCH is "the methodology's *single largest* S-Small divergence point."
- **Evidence gap:** The Loop D matrix scores S-Small across eight phases. RESEARCH receives WEAK. DISCOVER and MODEL also receive WEAK on S-Small ("accumulates over cycles," "grows monotonically"). The matrix assigns WEAK to three phases, not one. "Single largest" implies either a unique score or a magnitude comparison; neither is available in a four-level ordinal scale where multiple phases share the same level. The principled-vs-accidental classification table does clarify that RESEARCH's divergence is "Accidental" while MODEL's is "Principled" — but this is a qualitative classification, not a magnitude comparison that licenses "largest." The essay makes this claim twice and each instance presents it as a direct finding of the matrix rather than a synthesis judgment.
- **Recommendation:** Revise to "RESEARCH is the methodology's most consequential *accidental* S-Small divergence point" or "the only phase whose S-Small divergence is accidental and unremediated." This preserves the argumentative load while being accurate to what the matrix shows. The distinction between accidental and principled divergence (which the matrix does support) is doing the real work here; lean on that rather than on an unsupported magnitude claim.

### P2 — Should Fix

**Issue 2.1 — The 80–85% audit-fidelity figure is the auditor's qualitative assessment of the Spike S1 comparison; the essay presents it with more structural precision than the source supports**

- **Location:** §4, paragraph 3 ("preserves approximately 80–85% of argument-audit fidelity")
- **Claim:** Outline form preserves approximately 80–85% of argument-audit fidelity relative to prose.
- **Evidence gap:** The essay does attribute this correctly as a "qualitative assessment" in the same sentence, and §8 repeats the scope qualification. However, the number is generated by the Spike S1 auditor as a gestalt judgment, not by a defined scoring rubric. The research log (ADR-087 §4 standing caveat) explicitly notes: "The 80–85% figure is the auditor's qualitative assessment based on issue-by-issue comparison of one essay's two forms. It is not a statistical measurement over many essays." The figure is presented in the essay more cleanly than the source warrants: the parenthetical "(9 of 8 prose issues transferred at equivalent strength to the outline audit)" in §4 implies a 9/8 or 112% ratio, which would suggest the outline is *better* than prose on an issue-count basis — the opposite of the "loss" the 80–85% is meant to convey. The two figures (80–85% fidelity and 9/10 issue count) are measuring different things, and their juxtaposition without disambiguation risks reader confusion about what "fidelity" is being measured.
- **Recommendation:** The §8 scope-of-claim paragraph adequately calibrates the figure. The §4 presentation should make explicit that the 80–85% assesses *qualitative argument coverage* (specifically the calibration-and-confidence-gradient dimension), not issue-count. Distinguish the two measures: the issue count comparison (one additional chain mapped, two new framing findings surfaced, one prose issue resolved) is a *gain* story for outline form; the 80–85% figure is a *specific loss* story for differential-confidence calibration. Both can be true simultaneously, but the essay's current presentation conflates them.

**Issue 2.2 — Loop B's "audited/unaudited" finding rests on an absence of distinction across twelve sources, not on positive evidence that attestation has been tested and failed**

- **Location:** §1, paragraph 3 ("A critical finding from Loop B reframes the cycle's central question: the AI-prose critique does not distinguish audited from unaudited AI prose"); §8 scope-of-claim paragraph
- **Claim:** The AI-prose critique applies categorically, regardless of audit-attestation.
- **Evidence gap:** This is correctly characterized in §8 as "resting on the absence of the distinction in twelve sources surveyed, not on positive evidence that audit-attestation has been tested and failed." The essay both states this (in §8) and builds load-bearing architecture on the finding (in §1 and §7, where it grounds the "cannot be primarily justified as critique-response" claim). The §1 presentation treats the absence-of-distinction as equivalent in force to a positive empirical finding. The disclosure/attestation literature (Cheong et al., Gallegos et al.) does provide some positive evidence — attestation-as-label does not resolve the critique — but the essay itself notes that these studies measure "disclosure" as a sentence, not as a full citation/argument-audit attachment (Loop B limitations section makes this explicit). The scope of available evidence is therefore: no study examines audited AI prose as a distinct category; this is not the same as evidence that audit is ineffective.
- **Recommendation:** Retain the finding, but soften the framing in §1 from "critical finding" (which implies positive evidence) to something like "structural gap in the literature: no source distinguishes audited from unaudited AI prose, which means the critique applies as written to both." This is an honest and sufficiently strong statement of the evidence without overstating the inference. The §8 calibration is correct; pull some of that language forward to §1.

**Issue 2.3 — The Liu et al. (2026) ARA 72.4%→93.7% figure is drawn from a single unreviewed preprint and the essay does not flag the figure's benchmark-dependence**

- **Location:** §3, paragraph 3 ("QA accuracy for downstream AI agents improved from 72.4% to 93.7% versus prose papers")
- **Claim:** ARA infrastructure improves downstream AI agent QA accuracy from 72.4% to 93.7%.
- **Evidence gap:** The essay does flag the source as "a single April 2026 preprint, not peer-reviewed" in §8. However, it does not flag the benchmark-dependence in §3 where the figure appears and does argumentative work. The Loop C lit-review is explicit: "its performance claims should be treated as preliminary" (Loop C limitations, item 6). The essay's §3 presentation ("the strongest empirical signal in the literature surveyed for outline-form artifacts serving downstream AI agents specifically") is accurate, but the strength of the signal — a very large accuracy improvement — could anchor reader intuition well before the §8 calibration appears. The figure is also not directly analogous to RDD's case: ARA uses a fully typed, machine-traversable four-layer structure with infrastructure that does not exist in RDD's stack, whereas the outline-with-inline-citations hypothesis is a much more modest structural move.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief scope note at the point of first citation in §3: something like "on a particular benchmark set in a single unreviewed preprint." The §8 calibration is sufficient but appears six paragraphs after the figure is introduced. The §3 parenthetical "the scope qualifications" sentence at the end of the paragraph is doing this work but arrives after the figure has already been absorbed.

**Issue 2.4 — The "audit-extended prose cannot separate rhetorical force from logical force" claim is presented as a Loop C finding; it is more accurately the essay's synthesis from Loop C than a direct finding of the reviewed literature**

- **Location:** §3, paragraph 5 ("The critical Loop C finding against the strongest within-form competitor — audit-extended prose — is the claim that 'audit-extended prose cannot separate rhetorical force from logical force, which means framing adoption can occur within a well-headed section without detection'"); §7, paragraph 3 (same claim repeated as "the structural load of the comparative case against staying with prose")
- **Claim:** The Loop C literature establishes that audit-extended prose cannot separate rhetorical force from logical force.
- **Evidence gap:** The Loop C lit-review (Category 8: Audit-Extended Prose) makes a related but narrower claim: "What it cannot do: separate the rhetorical envelope from the logical structure in a way that allows the logical structure to be audited independently of prose quality. A well-written prose section under a mandatory heading can still embed unwarranted inferences in fluent prose." The "framing adoption can occur within a well-headed section without detection" part is the essay's inference from that observation, combined with the RDD-context qualifier (AI agent whose prose discipline is the variable in question). The research log's Loop D notes confirm this is "the load-bearing argument for the outline move" but frames it as the essay's synthesis. Presenting this inference as "the critical Loop C finding" attributes more directness to the source material than it warrants. Additionally, §8 explicitly notes: "Loop C's 'audit-extended prose cannot separate rhetorical from logical force' is theoretical (Docherty & Smith and the cycle's synthesis) rather than empirically measured for AI-agent-produced prose specifically." That accurate qualification appears only in §8.
- **Recommendation:** Rephrase §3 and §7 to distinguish the Loop C observation (rhetorical and logical force are co-present in prose and not independently separable by structural headings) from the RDD-context inference (therefore framing adoption can occur within headed sections in an AI-agent context without detection). The inference is sound but it is the essay's inference, not a direct finding. The current phrasing overstates Loop C's provenance.

### P3 — Consider

**Issue 3.1 — The Spike S1 comparison table arithmetic has an internal inconsistency: "9 of 8 prose issues transferred"**

- **Location:** §4, paragraph 2 ("Nine of the eight prose issues transferred at equivalent strength to the outline audit")
- **Claim:** Nine of eight issues transferred at equivalent strength.
- **Evidence gap:** The prose audit found 8 total issues (1 P1, 4 P2, 3 P3). The claim that "nine of eight" transferred is mathematically awkward — it implies more issues transferred than existed. The intended meaning is that all eight transferred, plus one additional finding was surfaced by the outline form, and one prose finding was *resolved* by the outline form rather than merely transferred. The research log's Spike S1 comparison table uses a different formulation ("9 of 8") that is itself confusingly worded. The prose form of this sentence inherits the confusion.
- **Recommendation:** Revise to: "All eight prose issues transferred to the outline audit at equivalent or higher strength; the tenth issue identified by the outline audit was a new framing finding with no prose equivalent; and one prose issue (the Decker citation bibliographic phantom) was resolved by the outline form rather than transferred." The current phrasing is a minor readability problem but creates real confusion for a reader trying to follow the audit logic.

**Issue 3.2 — §5's "One loss in auditability counterbalances the gains" understates the net finding before correcting it**

- **Location:** §5, paragraph 3 ("One loss in auditability counterbalances the gains")
- **Claim:** The citation-auditor identified gains and one counterbalancing loss.
- **Evidence gap:** The citation-audit-018-outline.md's net assessment is explicitly: "Outline form increases citation auditability overall. The gains in claim-source separation and scope-qualification visibility outweigh the loss in quote-boundary clarity." The auditor's own language is directional — gains outweigh the loss — not "counterbalanced." The essay's phrasing misrepresents the auditor's net judgment before the verbatim quotation corrects it in the same sentence. The citation-audit-018-prose-r3.md (the most recent re-audit) flags this exact issue: "the essay's preceding sentence says 'One loss in auditability counterbalances the gains' — this is a paraphrase that understates the net direction." The R3 audit notes it is "not a citation accuracy issue" because the following verbatim quote corrects the impression, but as an argument-fidelity matter the mischaracterization is present.
- **Recommendation:** Revise to: "One loss in auditability partially offsets the gains without reversing the net direction" or simply omit the characterizing sentence and lead with the verbatim quote. The verbatim quote does the work cleanly.

**Issue 3.3 — §6 does not note that the corpus consumption check (R2) relies on a grep-based method that could miss implicit content reuse**

- **Location:** §6, paragraphs 2–3
- **Claim:** The R2 method characterizes downstream consumption of essays by examining 16-essay corpus and downstream phase artifacts.
- **Evidence gap:** The research log describes R2's method as "`grep -rEn 'Essay [0-9]+|essays/[0-9]+' docs/`" — a pattern-match on explicit essay references. Implicit content reuse (where downstream artifacts use a framework concept or synthesis from an essay without citing it by essay number) would not be detected by this method. The 96 ADR references and the three consumption-mode taxonomy are findings from explicit citation patterns. The essay presents the R2 findings as if they characterize the full consumption relationship, but the method can only surface explicit references. This is a scope limitation that affects the claim that "downstream phases consume essays substantively (mode 3 is non-trivial — 96 ADR references is substantial)." The 96-reference figure may undercount mode 3 (implicit reuse) or miscount mode 2 (section-keyed warrants that carry substantive content but are counted as "medium density" rather than high). The §8 scope qualification notes the corpus scope but not the method scope.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief method-scope note in §6: the grep-based method surfaces explicit essay-numbered references; implicit content reuse (unlabeled concept or framework adoption) is not captured. This does not change the finding's direction but prevents overclaiming the method's coverage.

## Section 2: Framing Audit

The framing audit makes the negative space of content selection visible. This essay's central argument is that prose form is more susceptible to framing-adoption than outline form. The framing audit is therefore especially consequential for this cycle: if the essay itself has adopted a framing without surfacing alternatives, that would be an instance of the phenomenon it describes.

### Question 1: What alternative framings did the evidence support?

**Alternative framing A: The outline move is primarily a practical usability improvement for downstream agents, not a susceptibility-surface argument**

The Q4 corpus consumption check (R2) found 96 ADR references to essay content and established that downstream phases consume structured content (claims, frameworks, synthesis statements). The Liu et al. ARA 72.4%→93.7% finding is the strongest empirical signal in the surveyed literature. Both pieces of evidence point toward a downstream-consumption rationale that is essentially about *tool fitness* for an AI-agent audience, rather than about sycophancy-surface-minimization. The research log (Loop C Key Findings section) frames the ARA finding as "empirical motivation for moving away from prose toward structured claim-evidence bindings for downstream agent use." Under this alternative framing, the outline move is an agent-interface design decision, and the sycophancy-resistance and CRESS-alignment arguments are secondary benefits rather than primary justifications. The essay would look different under this framing: §6 would carry the argumentative load rather than §1–§3, and the critique-response reframing in §1 would be a background observation rather than a central pivot.

What would the reader need to believe for this framing to be right: that the primary value of a research artifact is its downstream consumption fitness, and that susceptibility arguments (sycophancy, AI-prose-critique) are heuristic rather than mechanistically established. The evidence supports this; the downstream-consumption evidence (R2, Liu et al.) is the most directly measurable.

**Alternative framing B: The outline move trades one susceptibility for another — visible calibration-loss is a susceptibility of its own**

The Spike S1 finding is that outline form loses rhetorical confidence calibration: "In prose, hedging language ('suggests' vs. 'establishes') signals differential confidence; in outline form all CLAIM bullets carry the same visual weight regardless of underlying confidence level." The Loop C audit-extended-prose category explicitly discusses this trade in the context of structured artifacts. Under this framing, the outline form does not straightforwardly *reduce* susceptibility — it redistributes it. Sycophantic framing adoption in the synthesizing step becomes more visible; sycophantic over-claiming at the individual claim node becomes *less* visible (all bullets look equally confident). The essay acknowledges the calibration loss as a "real fidelity concern" but treats it as a production-discipline problem. An alternative framing would treat calibration-loss as a structural susceptibility introduced by the form change, not merely a discipline problem solvable by tagging conventions.

What would the reader need to believe: that CLAIM-level confidence calibration is structurally harder to enforce in outline form, and that the production-discipline solution (explicit CONFIDENCE-LEVEL tags) is not reliably executed in practice. The Spike S1 source data is consistent with this reading; the essay's production-discipline framing is the more optimistic interpretation.

**Alternative framing C: The form question is less consequential than the production-discipline question, regardless of form**

The R1 task finding (research log) is that prose narrative in the corpus essays carries argument-structure content that can be preserved in outline form "with discipline." The Spike S1 auditor's net assessment is that "a disciplined outline form is argument-equivalent to prose." The key variable in both findings is discipline. Under this framing, the entire prose-vs-outline debate is secondary to establishing and enforcing production discipline, and the form change accomplishes little if the discipline is not in place. This framing would direct attention toward the outline-production checklist, the required-bullet taxonomy, and the audit-time check for synthesis-bullet presence — all mentioned in §8 but treated as implementation details rather than as the central question.

What would the reader need to believe: that AI agents produce disciplined outlines no more reliably than they produce well-structured prose, and that discipline enforcement is the bottleneck regardless of form.

### Question 2: What truths were available but not featured?

**Available truth A: The sycophancy literature does not directly test prose vs. structured output as a susceptibility variable**

The Loop B lit-review is explicit in its limitations section: "No 2025–2026 study directly tests prose vs. structured output as a susceptibility variable. The hypothesis that an outline is 'minimally susceptible' relative to prose is not directly supported or refuted by existing empirical work. It is theoretically motivated but empirically untested." The Loop C lit-review repeats this: "The sycophancy literature (Cheng et al. 2025; Kim et al. 2025) does not directly measure whether structured outlines vs. prose inputs produce different rates of framing adoption at the output side." The essay's central susceptibility argument is therefore built on a theoretical inference (less connective synthesis = fewer invisible premises) that no available study confirms. The essay mentions the absence of direct empirical support in §8's scope-of-claim paragraph, but does not surface it prominently in §1 where the susceptibility argument is introduced and the reframing is executed.

Why it may have been excluded: the essay's structure follows the research log's reframing narrative, in which Loop B's finding ("critique does not distinguish audited from unaudited") precedes and dominates the susceptibility argument. Surfacing the absence of direct empirical support for the susceptibility claim prominently in §1 would weaken the reframing's rhetorical momentum.

Would its inclusion change the argument: yes, materially. The "reduced surface area for sycophantic framing-adoption" justification — which the essay names first among the three surviving justifications — would need to be characterized as a theoretical inference rather than an evidenced finding. The argument remains plausible but loses the appearance of being evidence-grounded in the same way that the CRESS-alignment and downstream-consumption arguments are.

**Available truth B: Kim et al. (2025) introduces a complication that the essay does not address**

The Loop C lit-review discusses Kim et al. (2025), who find that "citation-based rebuttals trigger the highest rate of regressive sycophancy: models over-weight authoritative-sounding argument structure even when conclusions contradict ground truth." The essay includes Kim et al. in the References but does not cite this finding in the body text. The Loop C lit-review explicitly discusses its implications: "the strongest form of the 'stay with prose' argument comes from the sycophancy literature itself, paradoxically... a heavily structured document could itself increase a model's susceptibility by presenting an authoritative-looking framework that the model over-weights." The Loop C lit-review's counter-argument (this operates on the input side, not the output side) is available, but neither the complication nor the counter-argument appears in the essay body.

Why it may have been excluded: the Kim et al. finding complicates the essay's central argument. Including it requires navigating the input/output distinction (which the Loop C lit-review does competently), and its omission simplifies the argument significantly. The Kim et al. reference appears in the essay's References section (carried forward from the citation audit) but is orphaned — no in-text citation exists. The citation-audit-018-prose.md flagged this as P3 Issue 3.8.

Would its inclusion change the argument: the essay could handle Kim et al. using the Loop C counter-argument without conceding the central claim. But its omission means the essay presents the susceptibility argument without acknowledging the complication in the literature, which is a framing choice that favors the essay's central position.

**Available truth C: The DISCOVER phase also scores WEAK on S-Small, and its divergence is classified as "mixed" rather than clearly accidental**

The Loop D matrix assigns S-Small WEAK to DISCOVER ("accumulates over cycles") and MODEL ("grows monotonically"). The principled-vs-accidental table classifies DISCOVER's divergence as "Mixed" and MODEL's as "Principled." The essay's argument that RESEARCH's divergence is *uniquely* accidental depends on the contrast with these other WEAK-scoring phases being suppressed. DISCOVER's "mixed" classification means that not all phases with S-Small divergence have principled explanations — DISCOVER is an intermediate case that the essay does not discuss. Surfacing this would weaken the "single largest accidental divergence" framing but not eliminate it.

### Question 3: What would change if the dominant framing were inverted?

The essay's dominant framing: the outline move is justified by three positive considerations (sycophancy-surface-reduction, CRESS alignment, downstream-consumption fitness), all established by the research.

Inverted framing: the outline move is a hypothesis with theoretical motivation and weak empirical grounding, most defensible as a downstream-consumption tool change, and its susceptibility-reduction claims are unverified.

Under the inverted framing:

- The strongest claims become weaker: "reduced surface area for sycophantic framing-adoption" loses its grounding status and becomes a theoretical proposal. "CRESS S-Small alignment" loses some force because DISCOVER and MODEL also diverge, and scope-transfer from coding context to research artifact remains unvalidated.
- The weakest claims become stronger: the downstream-consumption evidence (R2 corpus check, Liu et al. ARA) is the most direct and measurable. Under the inverted framing, §6's downstream-consumption argument is the essay's strongest section.
- Evidence that becomes more salient: the Spike S1 calibration-loss finding, the absence of direct empirical evidence for prose-vs-outline susceptibility differences, and the Kim et al. complication.
- What the document would need to address if it took the inverted framing seriously: whether outline-production discipline is achievable in practice (not assumed), whether CRESS principles transfer to research artifacts without empirical validation of that transfer, and whether the sycophancy-reduction claim is a hypothesis to be tested or a finding to be relied upon.

### Framing Issues

**Framing P2-A — The susceptibility-surface argument (one of the three named justifications) lacks direct empirical grounding and this absence is not surfaced where the argument is made**

- **Location:** §1, paragraph 4 ("reduced surface area for sycophantic framing-adoption — less connective synthesis means fewer invisible premises"); §7, paragraph 1 (repeated)
- **Available truth:** Loop B and Loop C both explicitly note that no study directly tests prose vs. structured output as a susceptibility variable.
- **Why it matters:** The essay names sycophancy-surface-reduction *first* among the three surviving justifications, and in §7 lists it as part of "the joint force of three considerations." If this consideration is theoretical rather than evidenced, the three-consideration structure should acknowledge that one of the three has a different epistemic status than the other two.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief epistemic-status marker in §1 and §7: "a theoretically motivated but empirically untested reduction in sycophantic framing-adoption surface." This preserves the argument while being honest about its epistemic status.

**Framing P2-B — Kim et al. (2025) complication is available in the source material but absent from the essay body; the References carries an orphan citation**

- **Location:** References section (Kim et al. present but uncited in body); Loop C lit-review, Category 8, "strongest form of the 'stay with prose' argument"
- **Available truth:** Kim et al. (2025) find that highly structured, citation-based argument presentation triggers the highest regressive sycophancy rate in LLMs — the most structured form is the most susceptibility-inducing at the input side.
- **Why it matters:** The essay argues that structured outline form reduces susceptibility. The Kim et al. finding introduces a complication (on the input side) that the Loop C lit-review resolves via an input/output distinction. The essay could use both — the complication and the resolution — without changing its conclusion. Its current omission of the complication means the susceptibility argument is presented without the strongest available counterargument.
- **Recommendation:** Either add the Kim et al. input-side complication with the Loop C counter-argument in §1 or §3, or remove Kim et al. from the References if the finding is not being used. Carrying an orphan reference to a paper that contains a complication for the essay's central argument is a framing risk: a reader who looks up Kim et al. may find the complication without seeing the resolution.

**Framing P3-A — The CRESS scope-transfer question is raised and then effectively set aside without resolution**

- **Location:** §2, paragraph 1 ("A scope-transfer question therefore precedes its application to RDD's research artifacts: do the principles transfer when the 'context' being engineered is a methodology artifact rather than a coding prompt?")
- **What the essay does:** Poses the scope-transfer question, then proceeds to apply CRESS to RDD phases across four paragraphs and two sections (§2 and §7) without returning to whether the transfer is validated. The rhetorical move is to raise the question, confirm that Gorman invites skepticism, and then treat the transfer as established by the phase-by-phase alignment findings.
- **Why it matters:** The Loop A research log notes: "CRESS is scoped to coding-task context, not methodology artifacts. A scope-transfer question applies before applying CRESS to RDD phases." Loop D uses CRESS as a scoring framework across all nine phases, but the findings of that scoring are only as strong as the transfer is valid. The essay's argument chain 3 (CRESS identifies RESEARCH as the largest S-Small divergence) is fully dependent on the transfer being legitimate. A reader sympathetic to the scope-transfer objection could dismiss the entire CRESS thread, which would weaken the two-thread convergence argument in §7.
- **Recommendation:** Either add a brief affirmative case for why the transfer is appropriate (the CRESS principles express general properties of context fed to an LLM agent, which is what RDD phases produce; the scope-transfer is argued by analogy rather than established by Gorman directly) or acknowledge the transfer as an assumption the reader should evaluate. The scope-transfer question is too load-bearing to leave posed-and-unanswered.
