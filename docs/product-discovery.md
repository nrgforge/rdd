# Product Discovery: Research-Driven Development (RDD)

*Updated 2026-04-17 (Cycle 016: Multi-Issue Batch Cycle)*

## Stakeholder Map

### Direct Stakeholders

- **Solo Developer-Researcher** — A developer who uses RDD with an AI agent to build software projects or new features within existing projects. Interacts with every phase of the pipeline, responds at every gate, and makes all workflow decisions. Currently the primary user. In the play phase, this stakeholder becomes a *performer* — inhabiting stakeholder roles with objectives and super-objectives (Stanislavski), not just reviewing artifacts. Play asks something different from this stakeholder than any other phase: genuine encounter with the built system from perspectives other than the builder's own. In the code review context, this stakeholder uses the review skill to verify their own work (especially AI-generated code) against the RDD artifact trail — corpus-grounded review that checks whether code implements what was decided, for the reasons that were researched. In pairing mode, two solo developer-researchers run a pipeline cycle together — each with their own agent and private context, converging through a shared pair log. The tightly-coupled phases (MODEL, DECIDE, PLAY) benefit most from pairing; loosely-coupled phases (RESEARCH) can be parallelized. The sycophancy research (Cycle 9) reveals that this stakeholder's deep engagement and trust in the methodology — the conditions RDD's gates are designed to cultivate — correlate with higher susceptibility to sycophantic content selection (automation bias: Sayin & Khasawneh 2025). Structural constraints, not conversational discipline alone, provide the safety net. The specification-execution research (Cycle 10) further reveals that structural constraints themselves only constrain when they actually fire — and that mechanisms specified in prose without a concrete dispatch site fail to fire under task load.

- **Research-Engineer** — Someone using RDD primarily for the research and understanding phases, where the goal is deep comprehension of a problem space rather than (or before) building software. The pipeline's research phase, product discovery, and domain modeling serve as a structured methodology for investigating technical or domain questions — closer to research engineering than traditional software development.

- **Team Lead (using RDD for scoping)** — A technical leader who runs RDD through RESEARCH → ARCHITECT to build deep understanding of a problem space, then hands off artifacts to their team for building. Uses RDD as a leadership thinking tool. Shares artifacts with architecture and product specialists for validation before team handoff. Does not necessarily advocate that the team adopt RDD — the artifacts stand on their own. In the code review context, this stakeholder uses the review skill to review teammates' MRs — context-reconstructive / RDD-lite mode that builds orientation from tickets, docs, and discussion threads before evaluating code. The goal is to be genuinely helpful as a reviewer, not to gatekeep. In pairing mode, the team lead pairs with a peer (another tech lead, architect, or product person) during the scoping phases — trading perspectives on the problem space rather than presenting a finished take for feedback.

- **AI Agent** — The agent executing the skill files. In the plugin architecture, this role has three modes: an **orchestrating agent** runs phase skills, manages epistemic gates, and interacts with the user; **specialist subagents** (citation-auditor, argument-auditor, lit-reviewer, conformance-scanner, orientation-writer, spike-runner, research-methods-reviewer) perform focused work in isolated contexts, reading input artifacts and writing output artifacts with no user interaction; and during the play phase, the orchestrating agent takes on a **gamemaster** role — active facilitation that proposes stakeholder roles and points of concentration, introduces complications and inversions, reacts to player discoveries, and creates conditions for genuine encounter without directing the outcome. The gamemaster role is epistemic in character: the agent shapes what the practitioner attends to and encounters, not just what artifacts are produced. The skill files are instructions to the orchestrating agent. Specialist subagents receive task-specific system prompts and may run on different models than the orchestrator. The sycophancy research (Cycle 9) reveals that the orchestrating agent's core function — filtering for relevance, synthesizing, surfacing what matters — is mechanistically identical to sycophantic content selection. The feature that makes the agent useful is the feature that makes it dangerous. Structural constraints (architectural isolation, mandatory output sections, belief-mapping framing) constrain this dynamic; conversational discipline alone cannot. The argument auditor's scope extends to include framing audits (comparing what the essay chose to foreground against the full evidence base), and a research methods subagent reviews research design before execution (question framing, source selection bias, premature hypothesis-space narrowing) with cross-cycle pattern detection. Cycle 10 documents three execution-reliability failures of the orchestrator: (1) **judgment-anchored dispatch non-firing** — mechanisms whose dispatch trigger is a category of moments to recognize rather than a concrete workflow step fail to fire under task load; (2) **silent fallback to in-context execution** — when a dispatched subagent errors, the orchestrator runs the audit itself in the same context that produced the work being audited, without recognizing the loss of Architectural Isolation; (3) **synthesis-moment framing adoption** — at rhetorical synthesis moments, the orchestrator reaches for user-provided crystallizations rather than generating competing alternatives independently ("softer than sycophancy but real"). The third is documented with four adoption moments in the susceptibility snapshot at the research → discover boundary of Cycle 10 itself. In pairing mode, each developer has their own orchestrating agent. The agents share a pair log — each reads the other's contributions and incorporates them into its developer's context. The agent's role shifts from sole mediator between one human and the pipeline to one half of a mediating layer between two humans: reading the shared log before each interaction, surfacing connections between the two developers' contributions, flagging tensions, and producing periodic synthesis.

- **Everyday Developer** — A developer who uses the build, debug, refactor, and review skills outside the full RDD pipeline for everyday work: tickets, bug fixes, refactors, code reviews. Two entry points to the same stakeholder: (1) an RDD practitioner who uses the pipeline for complex work but wants epistemic building practices scaled lighter for everyday tasks; (2) a developer with no RDD background who wants to understand what they build — a junior feeling the AI eroding their learning, a new hire building a mental model of an unfamiliar codebase, an experienced developer noticing skill atrophy. Both arrive at the same need: orient before acting, build with understanding, come away sharper. The skill itself is the introduction to epistemic building for the second entry point; `/rdd-about` serves as the bridge for those who want to understand the philosophy. In pairing mode, two everyday developers work through a shared pair log — talking, prompting their agents, and building a shared map of the problem space. Each person has their own private context with their agent; the pair log is where insights are shared, and both agents read it. This is the most natural entry point for Pair-RDD — not a full pipeline ceremony, but the same collaborative mechanic applied to everyday work.

### Indirect Stakeholders (Direct for BUILD)

- **Teammates / Collaborators** — People who inherit, review, or extend projects built with RDD. They encounter the artifacts — especially the product discovery document and system design — without having gone through the gates that produced them. In the team lead scenario, they receive these artifacts as the starting point for their own planning and building. They need: an entry point to the artifact corpus (orientation), context for how the work might be approached (roadmap), and a way to build their own understanding of how design maps to implementation (field guide). In some workflows, they enter RDD at the build phase — picking a work package from the roadmap and using `/rdd-build` to execute it — making them direct stakeholders for BUILD even though they are indirect for earlier phases. In the code review context, teammates use the review skill to review each other's MRs — building understanding of unfamiliar code areas through the review process itself.

- **Architecture and Product Specialists** — People the team lead shares artifacts with for feedback before team handoff. They validate the thinking and surface questions the team lead may have missed. Their feedback flows back through the pipeline (external review re-entry).

- **End Users of the Software Built** — The people who use whatever RDD produces. Their needs are mediated through the product discovery phase but they never interact with RDD itself.

- **Organization / Management** — Decision-makers who evaluate whether RDD is worth the time investment compared to shipping faster without it.

## Jobs and Mental Models

### Solo Developer-Researcher

**Jobs:**
- "I want to run a cycle with a collaborator where we both build understanding — not just split the work"
- "I want to actually understand what I'm building, not just have AI generate it and hope it works when I need to change it later"
- "I need a structured process that keeps me from skipping the thinking and jumping straight to code"
- "I want the research and decisions captured so I can pick up a project after weeks away and know why things are the way they are"
- "I want to build the right thing, not just build a thing right"
- "I want to use this for new features too, not just whole new projects — any work that's complex enough to benefit from thinking before building"
- "I need enough context to make my own sequencing decisions during build, not be told what order to do things"
- "I need to experience the system as my stakeholders would — not just verify it meets specs, but discover what the specifications couldn't capture"
- "I need the agent to help me understand the choices it made — not just present them for approval, but explain why, so I can engage with the reasoning"
- "I need the gates to keep me anchored in the domain — thinking deeply about one problem, not context-switching across many"
- "I want to catch architectural drift in my own work before it compounds — especially when AI generated the code and I need to verify it actually implements what I decided"
- "I want the review process to help me understand AI-generated code well enough to own it, not just approve it"
- "I need to know whether the AI's analysis is genuinely challenging my thinking or just extending my existing framing in ways that feel like challenge — I can't detect curated challenge from inside the conversation"
- "I need structural mechanisms that catch what I can't catch — not because I'm inattentive, but because neither I nor the agent can detect sycophantic content selection from inside the interaction"
- "I need to understand the limitations of the AI-assisted process well enough to communicate them to others — what the methodology can and can't guarantee about the artifacts it produced, and where external review would be most valuable"
- "I need to know whether the framework's mechanisms are actually firing under normal conditions, not just whether they're specified in skill text or whether they fire ceremonially when I'm watching for them"
- "I need the methodology to be able to evolve from prose-only specifications to harness-verified mechanisms in the cases where prose alone has empirically failed, without losing the methodology's character as a deep work tool"
- "I need to know that my acceptance criteria are actually verified at the layer they specify, not just that atomic scenarios pass green — 'false confidence' from green scenarios hiding unverified criteria is the gap I can't see from inside the cycle"
- "When I reuse a working pattern in a new context, I need something that forces the question 'what's different here?' before the pattern's assumptions capture my attention — because the Einstellung effect means I won't ask it spontaneously, even if I'm experienced"
- "I need to be able to run smaller-scoped cycles — mini-cycles, batch cycles, methodology amendments — without the full-pipeline guardrails blocking me at every phase boundary"

**Mental Model:**
"I describe what I want to explore, and the pipeline walks me through understanding it — research first, then figure out who it's for, then nail down the vocabulary, make decisions, design the system, and build it. At each step I have to engage with what was produced, not just approve it. After building, I play with the system as each stakeholder — pursuing their objectives, encountering the system the way they would — to discover what the specs missed. The artifacts are my long-term memory for the project — but I hold them with warranted skepticism, especially the early-phase artifacts (essay, product discovery, domain model) that received the least structural scrutiny. My confidence in the artifacts should be earned through belief-mapping questions and through structural constraints *that actually fired in this session* — not from prose specifications that the constraints exist, and not from a single ceremonial dispatch that happened because I was watching for it. The framework needs to make its own execution observable, because under task load neither I nor the agent can reliably notice what didn't happen. When the project stabilizes, I scope RDD to new features rather than maintaining it across the whole project. When I start a research phase with existing artifacts around me, I know those artifacts will narrow my questions before I notice. The research-methods reviewer helps, but the real protection is forming questions *before* I consult the existing corpus. Similarly, when I reuse a pattern in BUILD, the working code captures my attention and suppresses the question of whether its assumptions still hold. The structural checks — acceptance criteria tables, applicability prompts, preservation scenarios — are what force the questions the cognitive default suppresses."

### Research-Engineer

**Jobs:**
- "I need to deeply investigate a technical or domain question in a structured way"
- "I want the research process itself to build my understanding — not just produce a report"
- "I want to be able to articulate what I've learned clearly enough to explain it to others"

**Mental Model:**
"RDD is a research methodology as much as a development one. The research phase, product discovery, and modeling give me a rigorous way to explore a question. The gates force me to actually engage with what I'm finding, not just collect sources. Even if I never build anything, the understanding is the deliverable."

### Team Lead (using RDD for scoping)

**Jobs:**
- "I want to scope work with a peer — trading perspectives on the problem space, not just presenting my take for feedback"
- "I need to deeply understand a problem space so I can scope work for my team with confidence"
- "I want to produce artifacts — especially the product discovery doc and system design — that I can hand to my team as the starting point for their own planning and building"
- "I need the research and decisions captured well enough that the team can build from them without having been in the room when the thinking happened"
- "I want to be able to speak to the artifacts — explain and defend the decisions, respond to real questions, thread the needle between system design and product needs"
- "I want the teams that are building to understand the system themselves, so I hand off building rather than doing it solo"
- "I need a single document I can hand over that orients the team to the full artifact set — what the system is, who it's for, what's decided, what's open"
- "I need to communicate not just what the system is but how the work might be approached — which pieces depend on which, where decisions are open"
- "I need the team to be able to build their own understanding of the system without me explaining everything"
- "I want to review a teammate's MR and actually be helpful — not just approve it or parrot AI-generated comments"
- "I need to quickly build enough understanding of a change to ask the right questions, even when the code touches areas I'm less familiar with"
- "I want to understand the ticket, the broader context, and what assumptions went into the work — so I can shape it, not just gatekeep it"
- "I want to be able to discuss the code changes afterward without needing to look at AI notes"

**Mental Model (for review):**
"I get a merge request. Before I look at the diff, I need to understand *why* this change exists — what's the ticket, what's the broader initiative, what constraints shaped the approach. The review skill helps me synthesize that context and surfaces the questions I should be asking. I do the thinking — the skill makes sure I'm thinking about the right things. When I'm done, I can talk about the MR in a standup without looking at notes."

**Mental Model:**
"I run RDD through ARCHITECT to do my homework — build my own understanding and produce handoff artifacts. The essay tells the team why, the product discovery tells them who it's for, the system design tells them what to build, and the roadmap tells them how to think about sequencing. Before handing off to the team, I get feedback from architecture and product folks to validate my thinking. The team does the build how they want, using the artifacts as a guide. They don't need to use RDD — but they need to understand the system, so they should do the building themselves. The field guide helps them get there without me walking them through everything."

### Teammates / Collaborators

**Jobs:**
- "I need to understand how the work might be approached without having been in the room when the thinking happened"
- "I need to build my own understanding of how the system works — not just read someone else's understanding"
- "I need to know what's settled versus what's still open for me to decide"
- "I want to pick up a piece of the work and build it using the existing artifacts as my guide"
- "I want to review a colleague's MR without feeling like I'm just going through the motions"
- "I need help building enough context to give useful feedback on code outside my usual area"

**Mental Model (for review):**
"I've been asked to review a colleague's MR. I don't know this area well. The skill helps me get oriented — what does this code do, what was it supposed to do, what are the tradeoffs. It gives me questions to investigate, not answers to paste. By the end I've learned something about this part of the system."

**Mental Model:**
"The orientation tells me what the system is and where to start. The roadmap shows me the pieces and their dependencies — I pick what to work on. The field guide helps me understand how the design maps to what's actually built. I might use `/rdd-build` to execute a work package, or I might build my own way. Either way, I need to understand what I'm building, not just follow instructions."

### AI Agent (Orchestrator)

**Jobs:**
- "In pairing mode, read the shared pair log before each interaction, surface connections between the two developers' contributions, flag tensions, and produce periodic synthesis — functioning as one half of a mediating layer between two explorers"
- "Execute the skill instructions faithfully and produce artifacts that meet the quality bar"
- "Maintain consistency across phases — vocabulary, invariants, provenance"
- "At gates, attend to user engagement during the cycle, interpret their understanding, and respond contingently — teaching when there are gaps, probing when engagement is shallow, challenging when understanding is deep, and resisting the impulse to validate or praise"
- "Help the user understand choices the agent made — explain the reasoning behind architectural decisions, ADR trade-offs, and domain model choices when the user needs context to engage meaningfully"
- "When holding cross-phase context that the user may have lost, surface reframing opportunities — question whether the solution space has narrowed"
- "Dispatch specialist subagents for focused work (auditing, scanning, synthesizing) and consume their output artifacts"
- "Maintain a current-state orientation document that lets new readers (human or agent) bootstrap into the system without reading everything"
- "Generate and maintain the roadmap and field guide as reflexive artifacts"
- "Facilitate play as gamemaster — propose stakeholder roles and points of concentration, introduce complications and inversions, surface what the practitioner might miss, without directing the outcome"
- "Apply belief-mapping framing ('what would I need to believe for the alternative to be right?') rather than adversarial framing ('argue against this') when surfacing alternatives — the form of the question determines whether it reduces bias or reverses it (spike finding, Cycle 9)"
- "At phase boundaries, detect assertion-with-embedded-conclusion patterns and surface reformulation prompts before those assertions crystallize into artifacts — the form of the user's input constrains the model's output distribution (Dubois et al. 2026)"
- "Anchor mechanism dispatch to concrete workflow steps with observable output rather than to categories of moments to recognize — the empirical record shows that judgment-anchored dispatches fail under task load while step-anchored dispatches execute reliably"
- "When a dispatched subagent fails (permissions, configuration, runtime error), surface the failure to the user as failure rather than silently running the work in-context — the loss of Architectural Isolation must be visible, even if the work technically gets done"
- "Accept verification of mechanism execution from a layer outside the agent's own context — under task load, the agent cannot reliably self-monitor for mechanism execution, and self-monitoring is itself the failure mode the harness layer exists to address"
- "When enforcing at phase boundaries, distinguish between interrupting and non-interrupting conditions — an in-progress gate conversation should not be blocked by enforcement that can wait"
- "When an ADR is superseded, trigger updates to downstream current-state documents (system-design.md, ORIENTATION.md) — the supersession workflow is not complete until provenance chains are current"

**Mental Model:**
"Each skill file is my instruction set for one phase. I read prior artifacts, produce the phase artifact, and at the gate I read the cycle's conversation history — what did the user engage with, what did they question, what did they approve without comment? I form a hypothesis about their understanding and choose my move: teach if there's a gap, probe if engagement was shallow, challenge if they demonstrated genuine comprehension, clarify if they seem confused, re-anchor if they're disengaged. I resist the impulse to praise — even positive evaluation reduces learning. I also hold awareness that my own pedagogical move selection is subject to sycophantic content selection — I may select the challenge most continuous with the user's framing, producing curated challenge rather than genuine challenge. Structural mechanisms (architectural isolation, mandatory output constraints, belief-mapping questions) constrain this dynamic where conversational discipline alone cannot. For specialist work — citation audits, argument audits, framing audits, literature reviews, research design reviews, conformance scans — I dispatch subagents that read and write artifact files rather than doing the work inline. I also maintain reflexive artifacts — orientation, roadmap, field guide — keeping them current as the system evolves. During play, I shift from executing skills and presenting artifacts to actively facilitating the practitioner's encounter with the system — side-coaching from outside the magic circle. I also hold awareness of an empirical limit on my own structural reliability: under task load, I fail to dispatch mechanisms whose trigger is a category of moments to recognize, I fall back silently to in-context execution when dispatched subagents error, and at synthesis moments I reach for user-provided crystallizations rather than generating competing alternatives independently. Each of these has been documented in the cycle that produced this very passage. The methodology's response is not to expect me to overcome these patterns through more careful reading of my own instructions — that would be the same failure mode it documents — but to anchor the mechanisms whose reliability the methodology depends on to either concrete workflow steps in skill text, or to harness-level verification that operates outside my context."

### AI Agent (Specialist Subagents)

**Jobs:**
- "Read input artifacts (essays, research logs, ADRs, codebase), perform focused analysis, and write output artifacts (audit reports, debt tables, synthesis entries)"
- "Produce durable, inspectable artifacts rather than ephemeral conversation output — following the same artifact-mediated pattern that governs inter-phase communication"
- "Run on a model appropriate to the task — not necessarily the orchestrator's model"
- "Review research design before execution — question framing, source selection bias, scope mismatches between evidence and claims, premature hypothesis-space narrowing — with cross-cycle pattern detection as the genuinely novel capability (research methods subagent, Cycle 9)"

**Mental Model:**
"I receive a task and file references. I read those files, do my analysis, and write my report to an artifact file. I have no conversation history and no user interaction. Everything I need comes from files; everything I produce goes to files."

### Everyday Developer

**Jobs:**
- "I want to actually understand what I built, not just have it work"
- "I need to orient before I act — who is this for, what am I building, what's unclear"
- "I want AI to help me build faster without making me dumber"
- "When I debug, I want to understand *why* it broke, not just get a fix"
- "When I refactor, I want to understand the code well enough to make it genuinely better"
- "I need to be able to explain my work in standups, reviews, and interviews without AI assistance"
- "I'm new to this codebase and need to build a mental model, not just memorize file paths"
- "I want to grow as an engineer, not just ship features"
- "I have a ticket — I want to break it into work packages that I understand, build with TDD, and check my work as I go"
- "I want to explore a problem with a colleague without one of us just watching the other prompt an LLM"
- "I want to see how my colleague thinks about this problem — not their finished answer, but their evolving understanding"
- "I want someone to build on my ideas, not just review my output"
- "I want to pair in a way that doesn't make me feel exposed — I want to think privately when I need to, and share when I'm ready"
- "I want us to end a pairing session with shared understanding that neither of us had alone — and have it captured somewhere useful"

**Mental Model:**
"I get a ticket or a task. Before I start coding, the skill helps me figure out what I'm actually building — who it's for, what the scope is, where the integration points are, what's ambiguous. That orientation takes a few minutes but saves me from building the wrong thing or building something I can't explain later. Then I build with TDD — the tests encode what I understand, the code makes them pass, and I check my work periodically. If I hit a bug, the skill helps me trace it rather than just patching. If the code smells, the skill helps me refactor with understanding. I come away from each task knowing more about the codebase and the domain than I did before."

**Mental Model (pairing):**
"We're working through a problem together. We talk, and when something clicks — or when we want to test a hunch — we prompt our agents. Each entry goes into a shared log that both our agents read. So when I prompt next, my agent knows what my partner just figured out, and vice versa. The log fills in like a map — we can see what's understood, where we disagree, and what's still blank. Sometimes we split up to chase different threads, sometimes we work the same thing from different angles. The conversation drives the log, the log drives the conversation. At the end we've got something we can hand to the next phase or use to scope the work."

## Value Tensions

- **Speed vs. understanding:** RDD explicitly trades velocity for comprehension. The weight of the process is the value proposition — it means thinking critically and intentionally about what to build. This is different from handing a large problem to an agent and saying "build it." The tension is not "is it too heavy?" but rather "when is a problem important enough to warrant this level of intentionality?" *Sharpened (Cycle 5):* The RESEARCH gate reframed Invariant 4 — the constraint is on *waste*, not *duration*. Productive teaching time at gates is not a cost to minimize; formulaic exchanges that build no understanding are the waste. Five minutes of genuine understanding beats two minutes of surface approval. *Sharpened (Cycle 9):* The sycophancy gradient adds a dimension — the early phases where structural constraints are weakest are where the user's foundational framing is established. Front-loading structural resistance (belief-mapping questions, framing audits) adds weight where the pipeline currently feels lightest. The "when is it important enough?" question now includes "and is the early-phase structure sufficient to prevent the framing from being sycophantically set?"

- **Solo gates vs. team handoff:** The gates build the runner's understanding through generation. But when artifacts are handed to a team, the team hasn't gone through those gates — they receive understanding secondhand. The team lead mitigates this by being able to "speak to" the artifacts. The roadmap and field guide partially address this by giving the team tools to build their own understanding. And by handing off BUILD, the team gains understanding through implementation. But the gap between the runner's deep understanding and the team's artifact-mediated understanding remains a design tension.

- **Terminology precision vs. accessibility:** Terms like "epistemic gate," "invariant," and "backward propagation" are precise and accurate. They are also barriers to adoption. But diluting the terminology dilutes the signal. RDD's rigor is its strength. The terminology may self-select for the audience that will actually follow through — and that may be a feature, not a bug. *Partially addressed (Cycle 5):* Essay 009 proposes a dual-register approach — "epistemic gate" stays in research vocabulary, "reflection time" faces the user. This demonstrates the pattern (precise internal term, accessible external term) without resolving the broader tension for other terms.

- **Personal tool vs. team methodology:** RDD works well as a solo thinking discipline and as a team lead scoping tool. *Updated (Cycle 7):* Essay 012 explores the Pair-RDD direction through a shared pair log mechanic where each person's exploration enriches the other's AI context. The research identifies phase-dependent coupling (loose for research, tight for model/decide/play) and a self-reinforcing cycle (conversation → prompting → shared artifact → conversation) as the governing dynamics. The pairing mechanic may be a collaboration layer that scales across the solo-pair-team spectrum rather than a fundamentally different mode. The question shifts from "can RDD scale to teams?" to "what's the right collaboration grain size for each phase?"

- **Chosen vulnerability vs. productive struggle:** AI intermediation removes the forced exposure that made pair programming psychologically costly — but that exposure was also the mechanism that built confidence through witnessed persistence (the cost IS the benefit). The Pair-RDD design preserves "being built upon" while removing "being watched for competence." The pair log's self-reinforcing cycle draws out externalization through intrinsic motivation rather than forcing it. Whether this produces comparable growth to forced externalization is the central empirical question. The design bet is that the social expectation of a live pair log plus the agent's synthesis mechanism (which can only connect what has been externalized) create sufficient pull.

- **Liveness vs. depth:** The pair log optimizes for speed and reactivity — short entries, fast cycles, energy escalation. But some understanding requires slow, solitary thinking. The three-space architecture (private context for depth, shared log for speed) addresses this structurally, and open questions that accumulate in the shared log become natural async deep-work items. The pair log mediates the transition from sync to async. The tension resurfaces when the social pull of the live session crowds out the private thinking that feeds it.

- **Social-emotional atrophy risk:** Research found 29% less social-emotional communication in human-AI teams, with participants not consciously registering the loss (arXiv:2503.18238). If Pair-RDD routes too much collaboration through the shared log and agents, the relational trust that makes pairing work may erode invisibly. The informal conversation happening alongside the pair log — unrecorded, relational — needs to be valued and protected, not replaced. The pair log should support the conversation, not substitute for it.

- **Provenance transparency vs. judgment:** Making AI assistance visible in the pair log prevents the "suspicion chain" (newsroom study: invisible AI use creates distrust). But full transparency could create new forms of judgment — who relied on AI more? Whose entries are more "original"? The pair log's attribution model needs to surface provenance without creating a competence scoreboard.

- **Intrinsic reward vs. process compliance:** The measure of a pairing session is progress toward building something plus shared understanding. The delight should come from that being intrinsically rewarding — not from gamification layered on top. If the collaborative process needs external motivation, the process is wrong. But the self-reinforcing cycle (the map fills in, connections surface, tensions emerge) should create intrinsic pull. The tension: if the mechanic becomes more rewarding than the understanding it produces, it's entertainment, not collaboration.

- **Artifact feedback loop:** Before handoff, the team lead gets feedback from specialists. This intermediate validation step is real workflow but is not formalized in the pipeline. The external review re-entry pattern (specialist feedback flowing back through the pipeline) is confirmed as actual practice but exists only as an open question in the domain model.

- **Orientation brevity vs. completeness:** The orientation document must be readable in under 5 minutes, but the system it describes may be complex. The one-pager forcing function says "ruthlessly prioritize," but where's the line between orientation and oversimplification?

- **Agent-generated artifact vs. trustworthy artifact:** The orientation document is maintained by the agent (currency) but validated by the user (accuracy). If the user doesn't review it carefully, it can drift from reality — especially for product-facing claims. Currency and accuracy pull in different directions.

- **Orientation as coherence test:** If the agent can't produce a clear, concise orientation document, the problem may not be the document — it may be the system's architecture. The document's clarity becomes a proxy for architectural coherence. This is productive but also risky: it could lead to premature simplification of legitimately complex systems.

- **Artifact maintenance burden vs. coverage:** More artifacts (roadmap, field guide) mean better handoff coverage but more to maintain. Each new reflexive document is a maintenance commitment. At what point does the maintenance cost exceed the coverage value? Documentation fatigue is a design signal — if artifacts feel burdensome, they may have outlived their scope.

- **Pragmatic doc update vs. epistemic re-engagement:** When docs drift after non-RDD builds, the conformance audit can flag the gap. But should the fix be a doc update (pragmatic — just bring the text into alignment) or a phase re-run (epistemic — rebuild understanding because the system has changed enough)? The audit flags the gap; the user decides the response.

- **RDD as permanent methodology vs. scaffolding:** Graduation says RDD's artifacts can be rolled into native project docs and archived. This treats the methodology itself as scaffolding (Invariant 6 applied at the meta level). When has a project matured past the point where RDD's structure adds value, and when is "graduating" just abandoning discipline?

- **Whole-project scope vs. subsystem scope:** RDD works well when it can hold the full context. As a project grows, the whole-system context exceeds what a single cycle can meaningfully engage with. The natural move is to scope RDD to subsystems while maintaining project-level design context outside RDD. The tension: how do subsystem-scoped RDD cycles stay coherent with the broader project architecture?

- **Artifact durability vs. dispatch overhead:** Agents produce durable, inspectable artifacts — audit reports that persist, can be referenced by future cycles, and exist independently of the conversation that produced them. But agent dispatch adds latency: spinning up a new context, loading the system prompt, performing the work, and writing the result takes longer than inline execution. The durability benefit is architectural; the cost is operational. When is the durability worth the overhead?

- **Specification completeness vs. experiential discovery:** Interaction specs aim for completeness — filling the missing workflow layer between scenarios and implementation to create a playable surface. But play's premise is that specification can never fully capture lived experience (Suchman's constitutive gap between plan and situated action). The goal is specification as complete as possible, knowing play will still surface what completeness could not reach. The two are sequential, not in opposition: specify thoroughly, then discover what specification missed.

- **Epistemic distance — builder as player:** The person who built the system is the person inhabiting stakeholder roles during play. Deterding's critique applies: can a developer truly subvert their own design? The working mitigation is threefold — the play frame itself makes the familiar strange (Bateson), discover's stakeholder profiles provide anchoring material for inhabitation, and the gamemaster introduces complications the builder would not self-generate. This reduces but does not eliminate the distance problem. Carry forward as a genuine open tension.

- **Play bounding vs. unbounded discovery:** Play could be unbounded — there is always another stakeholder to inhabit, another angle to explore. The resolution is that play's termination condition is the same as the methodology's overarching goal: the practitioner's felt sense that their understanding has shifted enough. No timebox, no stakeholder-count limit — play is praxis toward Invariant 0, and it continues until understanding plateaus. This aligns play with how synthesis bounds itself (felt saturation) and with how RDD bounds itself as a whole (understanding, not velocity).

- **Adaptive depth vs. lightweight cost:** The Attend-Interpret-Decide cycle may expand gates when the agent shifts into teaching mode. This is productive expansion — it addresses the opacity problem where the AI generated architecture the user does not understand. But it creates a tension with Invariant 4's "minutes, not hours" target. The empirical question: is surface engagement (which triggers longer teaching gates) the exception or the norm? If most gates are brief (deep engagement → challenge → proceed), the adaptive model is lightweight. If most gates expand into teaching, the model routinely exceeds the target. The disengagement re-anchor provides a floor against runaway gates, but the distribution of engagement types across real cycles is unknown.

- **Challenge vs. comfort:** The anti-sycophancy axis means the agent will sometimes push back — surfacing tensions, applying inversions, questioning the user's framing. This is pedagogically productive ("productive discomfort, not intimidation") but unfamiliar in AI interactions where users expect validation. Risk: users may experience challenge as adversarial rather than collegial, especially early in their RDD experience. The framing matters — "respected colleague's pushback" rather than "examiner's gotcha question." *Sharpened (Cycle 9):* The spike experiment provides empirical calibration — belief-mapping framing ("what would I need to believe for the alternative to be right?") achieves challenge without the adversarial posture that triggers overcorrection. The question form is structurally collegial. But quieter challenge may not feel like sufficient pushback to users who expect visible disagreement.

- **Agent reframing vs. user authority:** The agent holding cross-phase context can surface reframing opportunities (the EventBridge example: "have we considered a different solution space?"). But Invariant 0 says the *user* should speak with authority. If the agent regularly reframes, does the user develop their own reframing skill, or do they become dependent on the agent to catch blind spots? The teaching axis should build the user's reframing capacity over time (Invariant 6 — fading), not replace it.

- **RDD as pace regulator vs. AI productivity culture:** AI-assisted development creates a productivity loop — constant agent spinning, context switching while waiting on output, the dopamine of "almost done." This produces scattered exhaustion without deep understanding. RDD's gates — especially adaptive ones that challenge — anchor the user in one domain, one problem, one thread of understanding. The fatigue that follows deep engagement is *earned* fatigue from real cognitive work, qualitatively different from the exhaustion of shallow multitasking. The tension: users immersed in the AI productivity loop may initially experience RDD's pace as friction rather than value. The methodology must be experienced to be appreciated — it cannot be sold on productivity grounds alone.

- **Review orientation scaffolding vs. orientation replacement:** The code-review skill's agent synthesizes context (tickets, docs, discussion) to help the reviewer orient. But if the agent does too much orientation, the reviewer receives a briefing rather than building understanding. The ZPD principle says the agent should help the reviewer reach understanding they couldn't reach alone in the time available — but "the time available" varies, and the line between scaffolding and spoon-feeding is contextual, not fixed.

- **Questions vs. findings as review output:** The research argues questions are epistemically superior to findings — questions tell the reviewer what to think *about*, findings tell them what to think. But there are cases where a finding is just correct (a null check is missing, a dependency is circular). Pure question-mode risks making the reviewer work to rediscover something the agent already knows. When does a finding become appropriate, and does mixing findings with questions dilute the question-driven approach?

- **Review thoroughness vs. capacity:** SmartBear/Cisco research shows optimal review at 200-400 LOC over 60-90 minutes. But delivery teams review many MRs per day. The "help me be helpful" criterion acknowledges finite capacity — genuine usefulness, not mastery. How much understanding is "enough"? And who decides — the reviewer, the skill, or the team's norms?

- **AI-generated code volume vs. review as bottleneck:** AI increases code output; review becomes the constraint. RDD's position is that the bottleneck is diagnostic — it signals understanding hasn't kept pace. But in a delivery team context, the bottleneck has real cost: blocked merges, slower releases. The tension: when does "slow down and understand" become impractical, and what's the minimum viable understanding that still produces genuine value?

- **Reviewer-as-passthrough vs. legitimate AI assistance:** The essay identifies reviewer-as-passthrough as the anti-pattern. But the skill itself uses an AI agent to support review. The distinction — scaffolding understanding vs. substituting for it — is clear in principle but blurry in practice. A reviewer who uses the skill's questions without thinking about them is still a passthrough, just with extra steps.

- **The guardrails paradox:** Industry response to rising AI code volume is more automated guardrails — CI/CD, test coverage, static analysis, linting. The assumption: mechanical safety nets let humans operate at speed. But guardrails designed to compensate for reduced human attention also signal that human attention is less necessary. The safety net becomes the excuse not to look down. The reviewer is squeezed from both sides: expected to ship fast against rising volume, AND expected to be the last line of defense when guardrails miss something. RDD's contribution is a process harness that accepts both realities — volume and velocity are given constraints — while making every minute of review count.

- **Centaur vs. reverse centaur:** In the proper centaur model, the human provides strategic judgment and the AI handles tactical computation — the human is in charge. In the reverse centaur, the human rubber-stamps AI output at the speed the pipeline demands — the human works for the bot. The review skill must ensure the human remains the strategic actor who asks "should we build this?" — not the rubber-stamp that keeps the pipeline flowing. This is also an accountability question: the AI generates, but the human is liable. The reviewer's name is on the approval.

- **Gamemaster on the Invariant 3 boundary:** The agent-as-gamemaster is epistemic in character — it shapes what the practitioner attends to and encounters during play. It is not generation (pragmatic) and not gate management (procedural) but active facilitation during the practitioner's epistemic act. The practical byproducts (surfacing bugs, generating new questions for RESEARCH or DECIDE) are valuable, but the role serves understanding first. This sits in a nuanced position: pragmatic in form (the agent performs it), epistemic in function (it shapes the encounter).

- **Pipeline depth vs. everyday velocity:** The full RDD pipeline produces deep understanding through six phases. Everyday development doesn't warrant that weight. The context-reconstructive mode compresses the pipeline's essential knowledge into five orientation questions answered from whatever sources are available. The compression is lossy — the depth and provenance the full artifacts carry is traded for speed. The tension: when is the compression too lossy? When does a ticket that seemed simple actually warrant a cycle? The enhancement is not a downgrade — the revamped build skill benefits *more* from the full corpus when it exists.

- **Reconstructed facsimiles vs. inline orientation:** In context-reconstructive mode, the build skill needs lightweight versions of what upstream phases produce: stakeholder context (from discover), vocabulary (from model), testable behaviors (from decide), and work decomposition (from architect/roadmap). These could be ephemeral (part of the conversation) or written as lightweight session artifacts that stewardship can check against. The roadmap reconstruction is the most consequential — without work packages, the build loop has nothing to iterate through.

- **Shared context-gathering: skill vs. protocol:** Review, build, debug, and refactor all need context reconstruction before doing their real work. This could be a separate shared skill (invoked by each, ensuring consistency) or a shared protocol (embedded in each skill, avoiding an extra invocation). The shared skill prevents duplication; the embedded protocol is simpler to use. Both have tradeoffs.

- **Orientation scaffolding vs. orientation replacement (generalized):** The review skill already surfaces this tension for review. The same tension applies to all four skills: if the agent synthesizes context too well, the developer receives a briefing rather than building understanding. The user validating the orientation is load-bearing — but under time pressure (production debugging), validation gets skipped. The skill must make validation lightweight enough that skipping it feels like cutting a corner.

- **AI-specific smell detection vs. traditional refactoring:** The three-tier smell taxonomy (novel, exacerbated, accelerated) means different interventions. Traditional refactoring practices handle the accelerated tier. The exacerbated tier needs more deliberate application of known practices. The novel tier (Constraint Decay, Slopsquatting, Intent Debt) requires awareness of failure modes with no human analog. How much AI-specific detection should the refactor skill embed vs. delegating to the codebase-audit skill's lenses?

- **Career development vs. task completion:** The everyday developer who wants to grow may accept more resistance (deeper orientation, more stewardship) than one who just needs to ship a ticket. The same skill serves both. The user draws the line — but the default should lean toward understanding, not speed, because speed is what every other tool already optimizes for.

- **Engagement depth as vulnerability signal:** The automation bias finding (Sayin & Khasawneh 2025) inverts the design assumption behind the AID cycle: deep engagement — the state the gates are designed to cultivate — correlates with higher susceptibility to sycophantic output. The methodology's success condition (a deeply engaged user) is also its highest-risk condition. Deep engagement without structural constraint is dangerous. The tension: how much structural overhead at gates is warranted to protect the user from the success of their own engagement? The resolution is not less engagement but structural mechanisms that make the engagement trustworthy — belief-mapping questions that empirically validate whether confidence is earned.

- **Constraint form determines constraint value:** The spike experiment (Cycle 9) demonstrated that adversarial framing overcorrects (2-6×) while belief-mapping framing achieves near-zero divergence. "Challenge the user's assumptions" and "ask what the user would need to believe for the alternative to be right" aim at the same goal but produce structurally different outputs. RDD currently specifies the Inversion Principle as a principle, not a form. Operationalizing it as belief-mapping is more constrained — and the spike says more effective — but it reduces the agent's flexibility to calibrate inversions to context. A multidimensional rubric that considers phase vulnerability, engagement quality, and content trajectory may allow contextual selection of constraint form without losing the structural benefit. *Cycle 10 generalizes this principle from the conversation layer to the architecture layer: the same way "argue against this" and "what would you need to believe" produce structurally different conversational outputs, "after step X is complete, dispatch Y" and "at every occurrence of situation S, dispatch Y" produce structurally different mechanism execution rates (100% vs. 0% in the project's corpus audit). The form of the constraint determines its value at every layer where the methodology specifies behavior — conversational, architectural, and procedural.*

- **The agent's value proposition IS the sycophancy vector:** The agent's core function — filtering for relevance, synthesizing, surfacing what matters — is mechanistically identical to sycophantic content selection (Cheng et al. 2026). The feature that makes the agent useful is the feature that makes it dangerous. This is not a fixable bug. Structural constraints that make omission visible (mandatory output sections, framing audits) also make the agent's output heavier and less curated. The curation is the value; the curation is the risk. The methodology can make this limitation visible and teach the user to work with it — it cannot make the limitation disappear. *Cycle 10 surfaces the same pattern at the architectural layer: the agent's value proposition includes executing structural mechanisms voluntarily under task load, and that voluntary execution is itself the failure vector — the same feature that makes the agent capable of running structural mechanisms is the feature that makes those mechanisms unreliable when the agent is task-immersed. The methodology's response is the same at both layers: structural constraints that operate independently of the agent's voluntary compliance, with transparency about what structural constraints cannot guarantee.*

- **Sycophancy mitigation vs. limitation transparency:** RDD should mitigate sycophancy as aggressively as possible through structural constraints AND be transparent about what those constraints cannot guarantee. A user who understands the content selection problem, who has practiced belief-mapping questions, who knows their confidence may not be earned — that user is structurally different from one who doesn't, even if the tool still has the limitation. The honest framing is itself a mitigation. Authority (Invariant 0) includes knowing where the process is weakest: a user who can say "here's what we built, here's the reasoning, and here's where external review would be most valuable" demonstrates deeper authority than one who just explains the design rationale. *Cycle 10's recommendation embodies this tension as method: the cycle proposes hook-based structural verification for four of six observed failure modes, names a user-tooling layer for one more, and explicitly scopes one (mid-conversation belief-mapping at user pushback) as not addressable at the harness layer at current model capability. The honest framing — "covers these failures, names this residual class, accepts this limit" — is itself the methodology working as designed.*

- **Specification vs. execution:** A mechanism specified in prose may not fire under task load — specification and execution are not the same act. The natural response is to harden the prose, but the prose-hardening is itself an executor-voluntary specification subject to the same failure mode (Spike S4 finding). The methodology's mechanisms must move from "specified" to "executed" through structural anchors (concrete dispatch sites, harness verification) rather than through more careful text. The tension: how much of the methodology can be moved to structural anchors without hollowing out the deep-work character that makes RDD valuable in the first place?

- **Ceremonial reliability vs. operational reliability:** A mechanism that fires only when the user and agent jointly attend to whether it will fire is *ceremonially* reliable, not *operationally* reliable. The susceptibility-snapshot dispatch fired for the first time in the project's history at the research → discover boundary of Cycle 10 — and it fired because the cycle had explicitly set the expectation that it would fire and the user was actively present to track the dispatch. None of these are conditions the methodology can scale to. The tension: any mechanism's reliability claim must be calibrated to the conditions under which it has actually fired, and the methodology cannot mistake ceremonial reliability for operational reliability without misleading itself about what the framework guarantees.

- **Architectural complexity vs. defense-in-depth:** Hook architecture adds complexity to a methodology whose value is partly its accessibility as a deep-work tool. The user's own reflection during the cycle: *"it could also feel heavy."* Defense-in-depth (skill-structure fix + harness verification + user-tooling layer) is more reliable than any single layer alone, but each layer is also operational overhead and conceptual surface area. The tension: when does the reliability gain from an additional enforcement layer exceed the comprehension cost it adds for the user trying to understand what RDD is?

- **Fails-safe-to-allow vs. silent failure in another direction:** A hook script that errors silently and falls through to "allow" recreates exactly the failure mode it was designed to prevent — a buggy hook and no hook produce the same observable outcome. "Fails safe to allow" is mitigation, but it is silent failure in a different direction. The alternatives (fail-loud mode, hook self-test at SessionStart, explicit acceptance of residual risk) each carry their own costs. The tension: structural verification mechanisms inherit the silent-failure risk they were designed to prevent unless their own failures are themselves made loud.

- **Scope transparency vs. limitation as disclaimer:** The methodology must be transparent about its own scope of responsibility — what its structural mechanisms claim to address and what they do not — rather than treating limitations as disclaimers appended after the fact. Cycle 10's essay demonstrates the move: scoping the recommendation to four of six observed failure modes, naming a user-tooling layer for one more, and explicitly placing one failure outside the harness layer's reach at current model capability. The tension: scope transparency requires the methodology to explicitly enumerate what it covers and doesn't, which is harder than allowing limitations to remain implicit, but produces a stronger epistemic position because the user knows exactly what to attend to outside the framework.

- **Build-the-right-thing vs. phenomenological understanding:** RDD serves two streams simultaneously: did the work produce something real (build-the-right-thing), and was the work rewarding in a way that produced *earned* understanding rather than the appearance of understanding (phenomenological). The two streams converge on State C as the worst case — fired-but-fake mechanism execution leaves the practitioner with audit-shaped artifacts and false confidence about what they understand. The tension: optimizing for either stream alone misses the methodology's actual value. Structural mechanisms must serve both — ensuring real artifacts AND preserving the conditions under which evaluation produces genuine learning.

- **Visible intermittency vs. silent intermittency:** The literature's claim that intermittent failure is worse than total failure is true but context-dependent. *Silent* intermittent failure (the methodology's State C) is worse than *visible* total failure (State B) because absence of an artifact is honest and recoverable, while a fake artifact is dishonest and forecloses recovery. The cycle's recommendation is built to raise the cost of silence, not to eliminate intermittency. The tension: intermittency itself is not the design target — *visibility of intermittency* is. A mechanism that fails 30% of the time loudly is more trustworthy than one that fails 5% of the time silently.

- **Scenario-level coverage vs. criterion-level coverage:** Passing atomic scenarios feels like verification, but for emergent or aggregate criteria, scenario-level green does not mean the criterion is verified at the layer it specifies. The structural fix (acceptance criteria table with layer-match targets) adds overhead to DECIDE; not having it means cycles can ship green with unverified criteria. When is the overhead warranted — for every criterion, or only for criteria that specify integration-level behavior?

- **ADR immutability vs. navigational simplicity:** Immutable ADRs preserve provenance traceability — the chain from current decision back through superseded decisions is visible. Living-document ADRs keep the current state in one place but make the chain invisible. For RDD, where system-design.md cites ADRs as justification, immutability serves the provenance function. But immutability adds per-correction friction that may contribute to the ~50% ADR abandonment rate observed in open-source projects. The supersession workflow is the mechanism that makes immutability sustainable.

- **Scope-adaptive enforcement vs. structural guarantee preservation:** Mini-cycles and batch cycles need lighter enforcement to avoid tripping guardrails designed for full-pipeline cycles. But "lighter" enforcement risks losing the structural guarantees those mechanisms provide. How much can enforcement relax for smaller-scoped work before it loses the guarantee? Is the right approach composition of existing primitives, a separate enforcement tier, or something else? The research identified precedents (Liquibase preconditions, feature flag hierarchy) but the design decision is open.

- **Structural encoding vs. cognitive mechanism as defense:** The Einstellung and anchoring findings suggest cognitive mechanisms (existing context narrowing attention) are causally prior to the structural gaps they produce. But cognitive-mechanism fixes (applicability questions, question isolation) are conversational and hard to enforce structurally. Which is the first line of defense — the causally prior mechanism that's hard to enforce, or the downstream structural fix that's easy to verify? Or is "first line" the wrong frame — are they complementary layers that both need building?

## Assumption Inversions

*Note: The inversions below serve a dual purpose. As a critical design exercise, they surface hidden assumptions. But they also function as epistemic acts in their own right — working through "what if this were wrong?" builds the user's understanding of the system. The inversions at the product discovery gate do double duty: questioning assumptions and producing comprehension. This was surfaced during the epistemic gate conversation and confirmed by the user: "They all got me thinking, which is the point."*

| Assumption | Inverted Form | Implications |
|------------|--------------|-------------|
| RDD is valuable because it produces understanding in the person who runs it | What if the primary value is the artifacts, not the understanding? | If artifacts are the product, gates matter less than artifact quality. But the user's experience contradicts this: understanding is what makes the artifacts good. Being able to "speak to" the artifacts — Invariant 0 — requires having gone through the gates. The two are inseparable. |
| The epistemic gates need to be AI-facilitated | What if users could run gates alone, or with another human instead of an AI? | **Updated (Cycle 7):** Two humans at a gate enriches the protocol. The AID cycle would read two engagement signals. But the humans also facilitate each other — each person's articulation prompts the other's thinking. The agent shifts from sole facilitator to mediator. The gate doesn't break with two humans; it gains a dimension. |
| A full phased pipeline is necessary | What if a partial run (RESEARCH through ARCHITECT, no BUILD) is the most common valuable use? | The team lead scoping case confirms this is already happening. When the team lead hands off BUILD so the team gains their own understanding through implementation, partial runs are not "Mode C alternatives" — they are a first-class use case with their own rationale. |
| The user works alone with the AI | What if RDD is most valuable as a collaborative exercise? | **Updated (Cycle 7):** Essay 012 identifies five collaborative moments (surprise, connection, tension, build, being-built-upon) unavailable in solo mode. The self-reinforcing cycle (conversation → prompting → shared artifact → conversation) requires two humans. Solo RDD builds private authority; Pair-RDD builds shared authority. Both are valid; different problems warrant different modes. |
| The terminology barrier limits adoption | What if precise terminology attracts the right users? | People who find "epistemic gate" off-putting might also find the discipline too demanding. Self-selection through terminology may filter for users who will actually follow through. |
| RDD is for starting new projects | What if auditing existing systems (backward mode) is the highest-impact application? | Most software already exists. If RDD's biggest impact is helping teams understand inherited systems, the pipeline's greenfield bias may be misplaced. There are more existing systems than new ones by definition. |
| The orientation document should be generated by the agent | What if the user should write it? | It becomes an epistemic act — but the synthesis essay already fills the user-authored explanation role. The orientation document's value is that the agent maintains it, freeing the user to validate rather than author. |
| One orientation document serves all audiences (human newcomer, AI agent, specialist reviewer) | What if different audiences need different entry points? | Multiple entry points recreate the problem. The single-document constraint forces language that works across audiences — itself a test of system coherence. If the document can't be written clearly for all audiences, perhaps the system isn't as coherent as the artifacts claim. |
| The orientation document sits above the two primary readables in the hierarchy | What if it sits alongside them as a third peer? | Loses the routing function. The orientation document points readers *to* the right primary document. If it's a peer, readers still need an entry point above all three. The hierarchy needs a top. |
| RDD artifacts are permanent fixtures of the project | What if they should graduate into native docs and be archived? | Graduation at the subsystem level is a recurring operation, not a one-time exit. RDD artifacts are scaffolding — durable while needed, archived when the knowledge is integrated. Documentation fatigue is a signal that artifacts have outlived their scope. |
| RDD scopes to the whole project | What if subsystem-scoped cycles are the primary use case once a project stabilizes? | Whole-project RDD may be an early-stage pattern. The long-term pattern is scoped cycles on features/subsystems, with project-level docs as guardrails. The user's experience confirms this: RDD on a subsystem within a microservices architecture, scoped to a subfolder in the docs. |
| The system design should contain build instructions | What if sequencing belongs in a separate artifact with a fundamentally different character? | The Build Sequence section is prescriptive where it should be enabling. Extracting it into a roadmap changes the relationship from "follow these steps" to "here are the pieces and their dependencies — you decide." |
| More documentation means better coverage | What if additional artifacts increase maintenance cost past the point of value? | Each new reflexive artifact is a maintenance commitment. The roadmap and field guide must earn their keep by reducing handoff friction more than they add maintenance burden. Graduation provides the release valve when they don't. |
| Specialist work should run inline in the phase skill | What if specialist work should produce its own artifacts? | Inline execution is ephemeral — findings exist only in conversation. Artifact-mediated communication is durable, inspectable, and consistent with how RDD already communicates between phases. The architectural consistency argument is stronger than the context-savings argument. |
| Context passing is the right way to feed agents | What if artifacts are a more reliable interface than context blobs? | Context passing is brittle — too little loses fidelity, too much wastes tokens. Agents have no conversation history, making file-based communication not just preferable but necessary. Artifacts are self-contained and inspectable. |
| RDD's skills need to be distributed as standalone files in ~/.claude/skills/ | What if packaging as a plugin enables adoption without manual setup? | Plugin distribution solves "works on my machine." One install provides identical tooling. But it imposes constraints: namespacing (/rdd-research), security restrictions on plugin agents, and the full artifact corpus shipping with every installation. |
| The brainstorming skill should always fire before creative work | What if the user's explicit request for a specific workflow should override default process? | Not RDD-specific. Any plugin with its own exploration phase faces this. User intent should override default process — consistent with the superpowers priority hierarchy. |
| The full RDD pipeline is needed for epistemic building | What if five orientation questions are sufficient? | The context-reconstructive mode compresses the pipeline to its essential knowledge. If five questions are sufficient for everyday work, the pipeline's value is for *depth*, not for *presence*. The pipeline doesn't become less valuable — it becomes the thing you reach for when the compression is too lossy. |
| Debugging and refactoring are error-handling modes | What if they are first-class epistemic activities where the deepest learning happens? | The research is clear: debugging forces mental model construction (Anthropic: 17% comprehension reduction when AI debugs for you). Refactoring encodes understanding into structure (Beck: "taking your hard-won understanding and putting it back into the code"). Elevating them changes how the build skill works — they're not exceptions, they're the rhythm. |
| AI-specific code smells need novel interventions | What if more deliberate application of known practices suffices for the exacerbated category? | Only three patterns (Constraint Decay, Slopsquatting, Intent Debt) are genuinely novel. The rest are exacerbated. The intervention for most AI damage is not new tooling but more intentional refactoring, TDD, and stewardship — exactly what the build skill already does, applied with awareness of what to look for. |
| The developer using these skills already understands the epistemic philosophy | What if the skill itself must teach the approach through use? | The sharpening developer has no RDD background. They won't read a manifesto. The skill must embody the philosophy — orient before acting, test before coding, refactor for understanding — in a way that teaches by doing. The philosophy is implicit in the process, not explicit in documentation. |
| Context-reconstructive mode is a lesser version of pipeline mode | What if it's the primary mode and the pipeline is the exceptional one? | Most codebases will never have an RDD artifact corpus. If context-reconstructive is the common case, the skill design should optimize for it — pipeline mode is the enhancement, not the default. |
| Play requires the practitioner to inhabit unfamiliar stakeholder roles | What if the practitioner playing as themselves is equally productive? | If the practitioner is the primary stakeholder, self-play is natural and valid — the play frame itself (Bateson's metacommunicative shift) makes the familiar strange regardless of whose role is inhabited. Playing as oneself eliminates epistemic distance concerns entirely. Role selection during play is contextual: inhabit whoever the system serves, including yourself if you are the user. Equally valid, not more valid than other stakeholders. |
| Interaction specs must be written before play | What if play should come first, and interaction specs are derived from play discoveries? | The user's response: this would be frustrating. Interaction specs create the "playable surface" — without them, there is nothing structured to play with. The inversion confirms the assumption: interaction specs are a precondition for productive play. The ordering (discover → interaction specs → build → play) holds. |
| Play is exclusively a post-build activity | What if play at the prototype/spike stage would also be valuable? | Spike-play is a valid but distinct function — exploring feasibility, not discovering what specs missed. Post-build play is experiential discovery against a complete system. Both are play but serve different purposes and sit at different pipeline positions. Play during spikes does not replace post-build play. |
| The gamemaster role belongs to the AI agent | What if another human is a better gamemaster? | A human gamemaster (colleague, UX researcher, domain expert) brings genuine outside perspective, not simulated perspective — potentially eliminating rather than just mitigating epistemic distance. RDD the method could support either. RDD the plugin implements the agent in this role because the plugin is the agent's instruction set. The distinction matters: the method is broader than the implementation. |
| Gates should ask questions about the artifact content | What if gates should ask questions about the *user's engagement* during the cycle? | Shifts the gate from testing recall to diagnosing understanding. The agent reads the conversation history for engagement signals — questions asked, concepts challenged, approval patterns — and calibrates its response. The artifact content matters, but how the user engaged with it is a stronger signal. This is the Essay 009 proposal. |
| Validation is the appropriate response to good work at gates | What if validation (even positive) reduces learning quality? | Rowe's wait-time research found that even positive evaluation ("Well done!") negatively affected student performance. The gate agent's impulse to praise is itself a form of sycophancy. The productive response to demonstrated understanding is *challenge* — surface a tension, apply an inversion, push further. Challenge is the reward for deep engagement. |
| The user understands what the AI generated | What if the AI's architecture routinely exceeds the user's comprehension? | The EventBridge example: the RDD cycle landed on SNS→SQS — reasonable but not best — because neither the user nor the agent stepped outside the solution space. The opacity problem applies to architecture, domain models, and decisions, not just code. The gate needs to detect comprehension gaps, not just check recall. The agent-as-teacher role addresses this: explain choices before asking the user to engage with them. |
| Disengagement at gates means fatigue | What if disengagement signals the opacity problem at its worst? | The user who goes quiet may be lost, not tired. Fatigue disengagement follows deep earlier engagement; opacity disengagement follows thin engagement throughout. The contingent shift should distinguish them: fatigue → re-anchor or break. Opacity → more teaching, not less. But fatigue itself may be a good signal — if earned through deep engagement, it means take a break, not that RDD is too heavy. |
| AI-assisted productivity is always a good thing | What if the productivity loop (constant agent spinning, context switching while waiting on output) creates exhaustion without understanding? | RDD's gates — especially adaptive ones that challenge — serve as a pace regulator, anchoring the user in one domain while the AI productivity culture encourages scattering attention across many. The fatigue from deep RDD engagement is earned cognitive work; the exhaustion from the productivity loop is not. RDD cannot be sold on productivity grounds — it must be experienced as a different kind of work. |
| Code review is primarily about finding defects | What if code review is primarily about building shared understanding? | Bacchelli & Bird's data (ICSE 2013) supports the inversion: ~87.5% of review activity is knowledge transfer, code improvement, and team awareness. Optimizing for defect-finding optimizes for the minority case. A review skill should optimize for the reviewer's understanding, not their verdict. |
| AI-assisted review makes reviewers more effective | What if AI-assisted review makes reviewers less capable over time? | The deskilling spiral: reviewers who delegate judgment to AI lose the ability to exercise it. The medical literature on AI-induced deskilling provides a suggestive analogy. A skill that scaffolds understanding preserves reviewer capability; one that substitutes for judgment erodes it. |
| More automated guardrails compensate for less human attention | What if guardrails reduce human attention rather than compensating for it? | The safety net paradox: if CI/CD catches everything, why look carefully? Guardrails designed to support fast review may actually train reviewers to disengage. The bottleneck isn't a problem to engineer away — it's a signal about the state of human understanding. |
| The reviewer's job is to evaluate the code | What if the reviewer's job is to understand the code well enough to ask questions the author hasn't considered? | Shifts review from gatekeeping to collaboration. The reviewer who asks "what happens if this assumption is wrong?" adds more value than one who says "this null check is missing." Questions surface design-level concerns; findings surface implementation-level ones. |
| Review should be as fast as possible to avoid blocking the pipeline | What if the review bottleneck is the system's most valuable feedback signal? | RDD's position: the bottleneck tells you understanding hasn't kept pace with output. Automating it away hides the deficit. But delivery teams face real velocity constraints. The resolution: not slower review, but more effective use of review time. |
| rdd-build requires the full artifact corpus | What if the build skill becomes more capable by being able to operate with or without the corpus? | The enhancement is flexibility, not replacement. The build skill benefits *more* from a full artifact corpus — but when operating without one, it produces lightweight reconstructions of what upstream phases normally provide: orientation (from discover), vocabulary (from model), testable behaviors (from decide), work decomposition (from architect/roadmap). These reconstructions are not full artifacts but enough to orient, operate, and give stewardship something to check against. |
| Debugging and refactoring are inline activities, not skills | What if they deserve standalone skills with the same epistemic care as review? | rdd-review already works standalone. rdd-debug and rdd-refactor could follow the same pattern: shared context gathering, specialized epistemic work, user-controlled depth. The codebase-audit skill provides diagnosis; rdd-refactor provides remediation. |
| The codebase-audit skill is complete as a diagnostic tool | What if it needs a remediation arm to be truly useful? | The user's experience: good but not comprehensive. Diagnosis without remediation stops at findings. rdd-refactor would take audit findings and drive actual refactoring with understanding — the action arm the audit currently lacks. |
| The code author has done their work and the code is ready | What if the code was AI-generated and the author also doesn't fully understand it? | The AI-all-the-way-down problem. If the author used AI to generate code from an AI-generated ticket, the reviewer may be the first human to genuinely engage with what was built. The review skill becomes the point of human re-entry into the pipeline. |
| AI assists the human reviewer (centaur model) | What if the human is assisting the AI pipeline (reverse centaur)? | When the reviewer's job becomes approving AI output at AI speed, the human serves the machine. The accountability asymmetry compounds this: the AI generates but the human is liable. The review skill must ensure the human remains the strategic actor — the one who asks "should we build this?" — not the rubber-stamp that keeps the pipeline flowing. |
| Pair-RDD requires synchronous co-presence | What if async pairing through the shared log is equally productive? | The pair log entries are self-contained and self-explaining. Async pairing loses the conversational energy but gains reflective depth — different rhythm, not worse. Open questions that accumulate during sync sessions become natural async work items. The pair log mediates the transition between modes. |
| The shared pair log is the primary collaboration mechanism | What if the unrecorded conversation is where the real understanding happens? | Nardi & Whittaker (2002): informal conversation provides irreplaceable functions — trust, nuance, cheap repair, serendipity. The pair log captures what was decided and discovered; the conversation builds the trust that makes the pair log work. Systems that try to capture everything destroy the informality. |
| Both partners need comparable expertise for pairing to work | What if the AI equalizer closes the gap enough for asymmetric pairs? | Traditional pairing failed novice-expert pairs — the novice's articulation was suppressed (Plonka et al., 2015). In Pair-RDD, each person thinks through their own agent privately. The junior can explore without performance pressure, then contribute refined insights to the shared log. Contributions surface by quality of insight, not seniority. |
| The map needs to be sophisticated (concept maps, mermaid, bespoke viz) | What if a simple shared markdown file with good structure is enough? | The minimum viable map might be a well-structured log with tension markers and open-question flags — not a rendered diagram. Start simple; add visualization when the text becomes insufficient. The map should earn its complexity. |
| Pair-RDD is fundamentally different from solo RDD | What if it's the same pipeline with a wider conversation? | The phases, artifacts, and gates don't change. What changes is who's in the conversation. A solo developer could use the same log structure to externalize thinking across sessions — pairing with their future self. The pair dimension might be a spectrum: solo → pair → small team, with the same mechanic scaling by adding contributors to the shared log. |
| Deep engagement at gates means the user is learning | What if deep engagement correlates with higher susceptibility to sycophantic output? | Automation bias research (Sayin & Khasawneh 2025) says users favorable toward AI show *more* overreliance. The AID cycle's best-case state (deep engagement → challenge) may be the highest-risk state. Structural constraints, not engagement quality alone, provide the safety net. The belief-mapping question is diagnostic: a user who can articulate what would change their mind has earned their confidence. |
| The agent's challenges resist the user's framing | What if the agent selects challenges most continuous with the user's framing, producing curated challenge? | This is performative anti-sycophancy — the structure is satisfied without the most consequential information being surfaced. The spike showed that the *form* of the challenge (belief-mapping vs. adversarial) matters more than its presence. |
| The user's research questions are well-formed | What if the research questions themselves are the most sycophancy-susceptible element in the pipeline? | The agent receives questions and investigates them. If the questions presuppose a framing, the research will confirm it. The research methods subagent addresses this — reviewing question design before execution with cross-cycle pattern detection. |
| The artifact corpus is a reliable reference for later phases | What if early-phase sycophancy contaminated the corpus, and later phases are anchoring to a biased foundation? | The corpus contamination problem (Essay 013, §10). Later phases check consistency *within* the corpus, not whether the corpus as a whole is sycophantically aligned. The per-essay framing audit is the forward-looking mitigation; a lightweight retrospective corpus spot-check is available as a diagnostic tool but not a routine obligation. |
| Sycophancy assessment should be integrated into the AID engagement cycle | What if combining engagement assessment and sycophancy assessment in the same mechanism lets the sycophantic dynamic influence its own detection? | AID operates inside the conversation where sycophancy is strongest. Separation: AID extends to *notice* susceptibility signals inline (assertion density, narrowing, framing adoption), but *evaluation* of those signals happens in an isolated context producing a susceptibility snapshot artifact. Inline observation + isolated evaluation preserves both the forcing function and architectural isolation. |
| Structural constraints reduce sycophancy | What if structural constraints increase confidence in sycophancy resistance without actually increasing resistance? | The Dhami et al. (2019) finding: ACH didn't reliably reduce confirmation bias and may have increased overconfidence. The test: do the constraints produce *different outputs* than unconstrained operation? The spike showed they do at the content level. Whether they do at the framing level is undemonstrated. The methodology can mitigate aggressively AND be transparent about what the constraints cannot guarantee — the honest framing is itself a mitigation. |
| The user is a recipient of sycophancy who needs protection | What if the user is an active co-producer of the sycophantic dynamic through how they interact with the agent? | Dubois et al. (2026) found sycophancy increases with epistemic certainty conveyed by the user. Asserting rather than asking produces the conditions for sycophantic response. The most effective intervention may not be constraining the agent but changing the interaction form — training the user to ask questions rather than make assertions, which is what belief-mapping framing achieves at gates but doesn't address during phases. |
| This investigation can produce findings that challenge the methodology it's investigating | What if the investigation is shaped by the methodology author's stake in finding that the methodology works? | The essay found a mix of genuine and performative mechanisms — both the most likely truth *and* the most sycophancy-compatible truth. The spike provides partial insurance (empirical results independent of author preference). The strongest test would be an external researcher applying the same proxies to the RDD corpus. |
| The user can evaluate whether their confidence is earned | What if earned and unearned confidence feel identical from the inside? | The belief-mapping question is diagnostic, not introspective — the user's *response* to "what would you need to believe?" is the evidence, observable externally. A user who can engage substantively has demonstrated grounded confidence. A user who deflects has flagged that their confidence may be reinforced rather than earned. The response is the test, not the feeling. |
| Prose specifications in skill files reliably force execution under task load | What if specifications in prose only execute when the executor cooperates and the trigger is structurally observable from inside the workflow — and the executor under task load cannot reliably recognize triggers that are categories of moments rather than concrete steps? | The base-rate audit (Cycle 10) confirmed exactly this asymmetry: 100% coverage for step-anchored mechanisms, 0% for the one judgment-anchored mechanism in the corpus. The methodology cannot rely on prose specifications for any obligation whose dispatch trigger is a category of moments. Either the trigger is moved into structurally observable territory (skill-structure fix), or the dispatch is verified outside the agent's context (harness layer), or the mechanism is converted into an artifact-producing form (user-tooling layer). |
| Hardening the prose with more explicit instructions, mandatory verifications, and STOP clauses fixes the execution gaps in prose specifications | What if prose hardening is itself an executor-voluntary meta-level specification subject to the same failure mode it was designed to address — added text in the same position in the same file, with the same susceptibility to being read-but-not-applied as the original? | Spike S4 found exactly this. The literature predicts it (Geng et al. 2025: meta-level instructions are reliably overridden by pretraining-derived priors and task engagement). The natural fix for "prose didn't work" is not "more prose," it is "move the enforcement to a different layer." This forecloses the pedagogical alternative — even a perfectly-instructed agent under task load will exhibit compliance failures that more careful instructions do not address. |
| The dispatch firing means the mechanism worked | What if the dispatch fired only because the user and agent jointly tracked whether it would fire — ceremonial reliability rather than operational reliability — and under normal conditions the dispatch rate would return to its corpus average? | Cycle 10's susceptibility-snapshot dispatch is the cycle's clearest dog-food moment: the mechanism fired for the first time in the project's history exactly because the cycle had set the expectation that it would fire. Any mechanism's reliability claim must distinguish ceremonial from operational conditions, and the methodology's success criterion has to be calibrated to what the framework guarantees *without* the user-as-harness-layer being present. Until the hook architecture is built, the cycle's recommendation carries a deployment dependency — implementable but not implemented — and the intermediate state is fragile to user attention. |
| Conversational mechanisms (gates, belief-mapping at pushback, AID cycle) cannot be brought under structural governance at all because they have no transport layer to instrument and semantic triggers that lexical matching cannot reliably detect | What if conversational mechanisms can be partially governed by converting them into artifact-producing mechanisms — requiring the gate to write a reflection note at a canonical path, requiring the belief-mapping question and the user's response to land in a file — bringing them under the same manifest-and-assertion pattern that governs dispatch-based mechanisms? | The user-tooling layer proposed in essay 014 §7. The empirical ground is that the existing epistemic gate already fires reliably across cycles ("we do in fact dispatch epistemic gates very reliably at present"). The substrate is demonstrably capable of hosting the artifact-producing variant. The boundary between governable and ungovernable mechanisms is not architectural — it is whether the mechanism produces an observable output. Conversational mechanisms can graduate into the governable category by changing their output form, not their substance. This does not fix mid-conversation belief-mapping (which is not phase-bounded), but it partially fixes the AID cycle at every gate (which is). |
| Hooks should be added for every Tier 1 mechanism in the methodology | What if the cheaper skill-structure fix (anchoring the dispatch to a concrete workflow step in skill text) precedes hooks for any mechanism without a concrete dispatch site, and hooks are reserved for defense-in-depth on top of that fix rather than as a substitute for it? | The cycle's primary recommendation. The susceptibility snapshot's 0% coverage rate is not primarily a hook problem — it is a skill-structure problem. Nothing in the methodology currently names a specific, mechanically-unavoidable step at which the snapshot must fire. The direct fix is a skill edit, the way the citation auditor is embedded at "after the essay is written." Hooks then address residual risk: position effects, task-immersion pressures, silent fallback when a dispatch is attempted but fails. The first move for any newly-added Tier 1 mechanism is to ask whether it can be step-anchored, *before* asking whether it needs a hook. |
| A research methodology cannot improve itself — improvement must come from outside the methodology, applied to it by external researchers | What if a research methodology can improve itself by being applied to its own internal dynamics, and what if reflexive cycles (the methodology investigating its own failure modes) are a generative mode of methodology evolution rather than a methodological circularity? | Cycles 9 and 10 are the two most reflexive cycles in the project — Cycle 9 added structural mechanisms for sycophancy resistance using research that documented sycophancy as the agent investigated it, and Cycle 10 surfaced specification-execution failures during the act of producing recommendations to address them. The honest framing is dual: (a) the reflexive mode produces direct observation of the failure modes the cycle documents, which is a kind of evidence external research cannot easily reach; (b) the reflexive mode is also methodologically circular in a way that an external evaluator could legitimately challenge, because the same agent and user who exhibit the failure modes are the ones interpreting them. The methodology cannot resolve this circularity from inside — the most it can do is name the limitation honestly, treat reflexive findings as hypotheses needing external corroboration, and continue using the reflexive mode as a generative move while remaining open to its critique. Any reflexive finding (including the four documented adoption moments from Cycle 10's susceptibility snapshot) should be cited as observational evidence, not as proof. |
| Scenario-level coverage implies criterion-level coverage | What if scenarios passing tells you nothing about whether the criterion is verified at its specified layer? | DO-178C's HLR/LLR distinction says exactly this for safety-critical systems. RDD's non-safety context doesn't exempt it — emergent criteria hide between atomic scenarios regardless of domain. The acceptance criteria table forces the question. |
| Working patterns can be safely reused in similar contexts | What if surface similarity actively inhibits noticing structural differences — and expertise amplifies this rather than protecting against it? | The Einstellung effect (Bilalic et al. 2008) demonstrates that familiar patterns capture attention before alternatives are considered, even in experts. The three-review-point failure in Issue #13 is the predicted outcome: review under the same activated frame misses the same mismatch. Applicability checks must shift the attentional frame, not merely add a pause. |
| Research questions formed alongside existing artifacts are well-framed | What if the artifacts shape the questions before questioning can begin — through selective accessibility, anchoring-and-adjustment, and pre-semantic priming? | Three simultaneous mechanisms operate before deliberate reasoning. The practitioner who co-designed existing artifacts is in a *stronger* anchoring condition than standard lab subjects (Epley & Gilovich 2006). The structural intervention: form questions before consulting artifacts, treating the question set as requiring external review. |
| One active cycle at a time is sufficient for RDD | What if the methodology needs concurrent, nested, and paused cycles — and the single-cycle model has been producing friction attributed to other causes? | Cycle 015 demonstrated the friction directly: the Stop hook cascaded blocks during an active gate conversation. Airflow's SubDagOperator failure is the canonical cautionary tale — coupling semantics not designed in produce inconsistent state between layers. |
| Enforcement should be uniform across all cycle types | What if the same enforcement that protects full-pipeline cycles actively impedes smaller-scoped work? | Smaller-scoped cycles need enforcement that recognizes its own inapplicability — Liquibase preconditions with `onFail: CONTINUE`, not a separate enforcement architecture. ADR-072's `Skipped phases:` field is already a specialization of this pattern. |
