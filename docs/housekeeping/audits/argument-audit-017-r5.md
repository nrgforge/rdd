### R4 Correction Verification

**R4 P2 (Issue 2.1) — Ernst & Robillard scope qualification and schema-carry-forward caveat in §4.5's "brief purpose-built orientation artifacts" sub-bullet: Resolved.**

The R4 report found that the sub-bullet admitted a new intervention class on the basis of Ernst & Robillard's prior-familiarity finding without repeating the scope qualification present at §3 and §5, and that the cognitive-economy test's dependence on schema-carry-forward — a mechanism not directly empirically established — was unstated at the point of admission.

In R5, the sub-bullet now reads: "Ernst & Robillard's prior-familiarity dominance — within the scope qualification of §3 (their study covered applied and creative tasks, not orientation-level comprehension specifically) — points directionally toward this intervention." It then adds: "The cognitive-economy test — whether the artifact's reading cost pays back in reduced cognitive load downstream — depends on schema-carry-forward from a pre-read into subsequent comprehension, a mechanism the cycle's reviewed literature motivates directionally but does not directly establish empirically." The closing sentence defers operationalization to DECIDE explicitly.

Both gaps named in R4 Issue 2.1 are addressed. The scope qualification is now present at the point of invocation. The unvalidated mechanism is named as directional rather than established. The issue is closed.

**R4 P3 (Issue 3.1, partial) — §4.5 closing paragraph reframed from "share a structural property" to "illustrate a structural direction": Resolved.**

The R4 report found that the §4.5 closing paragraph's "share a structural property" formulation overstated the uniformity of cognitive-economy support across the five sub-patterns. In R5, the paragraph opens "The five responses, taken together, illustrate a structural direction worth naming" and adds: "The five sub-patterns within §4.5 vary in how directly the cognitive-economy support is established (in-place restructuring is the strongest case; small new artifacts rest on a directional rather than directly-validated mechanism), and that variation is itself something downstream phases will need to address."

Both the framing softening and the explicit acknowledgment of differential support are present. The issue is closed.

**R4 Framing P2-A — §6 DECIDE implications expanded to direct DECIDE to examine whether the cognitive-economy criterion is purely cognitive or whether non-cognitive admissibility belongs alongside it: Resolved.**

The R4 framing audit found that the cognitive-economy frame was silent on non-cognitive forms of value (relational, normative, trust-building) that the field evidence captured. In R5, the §6 DECIDE paragraph now includes: "A related decision DECIDE should examine: whether the criterion is intended to be purely cognitive or whether non-cognitive admissibility (relational, normative, trust-building value that does not register as cognitive load reduction) belongs alongside the cognitive criterion as a separately-evaluable axis."

The sentence directs DECIDE to examine this question explicitly as a design decision rather than leaving it invisible. The issue is closed.

**R4 P3 (Issue 3.2 — Heijstek UML parenthetical, Diátaxis "maps" softening): Persists by deliberate acceptance.**

Both items remain unaddressed, consistent with the R5 brief's declaration that the cycle accepts these as not-blocking. Both are noted below in P3 for completeness. No new disposition is available.

### Question 3: What would change if the dominant framing were inverted?

**Dominant framing of R5:** The optimization target is cognitive economy on the human side. New human-facing additions are admissible when they earn back their reading cost in reduced downstream cognitive load; agent-facing context may grow. The five §4.5 sub-patterns vary in how directly cognitive-economy support is established. DECIDE is directed to examine whether non-cognitive value belongs alongside cognitive economy as a separately-evaluable criterion.

**Inverted framing:** Cognitive economy, even as now qualified, is a proxy for the underlying goal rather than the goal itself. The underlying goal — making the human experience of working within the framework feel coherent — encompasses relational and normative dimensions (stakeholder trust, sense of agency, sense of being respected as a reader) that cognitive-load reduction does not fully capture.

Under the inverted framing, the R5 corrections improve the essay's accuracy about the cognitive dimension while leaving the relational dimension visible only in §6's DECIDE note. The §6 sentence directing DECIDE to examine non-cognitive admissibility is the essay's acknowledgment of this gap; it surfaces the question at the gate. Under the inverted framing, a reader would argue that the gap belongs in §4.5's organizing constraint, not deferred to §6, because the design decisions §4.5 enables may optimize for cognitive economy while inadvertently degrading stakeholder trust. That argument is not falsified by the R5 changes; it is surfaced for user judgment at DECIDE.

The inverted framing does not newly weaken the essay's argument — the R4 audit already identified this gap, and the R5 §6 addition is the essay's response. No new inversion finding emerges from R5's changes.
