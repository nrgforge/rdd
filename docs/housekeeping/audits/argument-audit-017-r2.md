### P2 — Should Fix

**Issue 2.1 — §4.5's closing sentence re-introduces a methodology-self-credit framing that the §6 correction cancelled**

- **Location:** §4.5, final paragraph ("The methodology's response to a comprehension failure is not more methodology; it is sharpening what the methodology already produces.")
- **Claim:** The sentence frames the five responses as the methodology's own response to a comprehension failure, attributing the response to the methodology as agent.
- **Evidence gap:** The §6 revision — adopted specifically to correct R1's finding that methodology credit was overreaching — reframes the methodology as scaffold rather than author. The §4.5 closing sentence moves in the opposite direction: "The methodology's response... is sharpening what the methodology already produces" positions the methodology as both the recipient of a problem and the originator of its solution. The joint contributors identified in §6 (user openness, isolated subagent contexts, question-isolation protocol) produced the diagnostic recalibration; the methodology's structural role enabled the conditions but did not author the responses. The same attribution problem the §6 revision was asked to fix is re-introduced at the close of §4.5 in sharpened form — the sentence is rhetorical and memorable, which increases rather than decreases its capacity to carry the wrong reading forward.
- **Recommendation:** Revise the closing sentence of §4.5 to name the structural property of the responses (operating at the margin of the existing corpus) without crediting the methodology as agent. A repair such as "Each response operates at the margin of the existing corpus, sharpening what is already there rather than adding to it" preserves the structural insight without the attribution overstep.

**Issue 2.2 — §4.5's "agent-mediated orientation" candidate is held with appropriate caution, but the distinction from the rejected pattern rests on a premise ("intended modality") that the section does not establish**

- **Location:** §4.5, final substantive sub-section ("Agent-mediated orientation as a deliberate design pattern (candidate, not adopted here)")
- **Claim:** The section distinguishes agent-mediated orientation (an interactive, conversational on-ramp) from AI-as-prosthetic (humans needing AI to translate documents that look like they should be human-readable). The distinction is characterized as "genuine but fragile — it can shade into the rejected pattern under usage drift."
- **Evidence gap:** The section names the fragility but does not establish what would prevent or detect the drift. The source material (research log, constraint-removal response) records the user's normative position precisely — objection to "AI as human-reading-prosthetic" — but provides no criterion for when a conversational on-ramp has drifted into prosthetic behavior. The essay presents the distinction as a reason to hold this as candidate territory for downstream phases, which is a sound procedural response to an unresolved empirical question. However, the section gives the impression that the distinction is principled and resolvable in principle, whereas the source material does not support the claim that a reliable distinction criterion exists. The §6 DECIDE implication correctly notes that an ADR "examines the AI-as-orienter / AI-as-prosthetic distinction explicitly, with the user's normative position as load-bearing input" — but the essay implies this examination will produce a principled resolution when the research log records no basis for one.
- **Recommendation:** Add a sentence acknowledging that no criterion in the current literature or the cycle's own findings demarcates the distinction operationally. The honest scope is: the distinction is conceptually real but operationally unresolved, and downstream phases will need to supply the criterion, not just the examination. This is a softening of the current framing's implied confidence that DECIDE can settle the question.

**Issue 2.3 — The "reading-path infrastructure" sub-pattern in §4.5 is asserted as evidence-backed but the evidence is not cited at the point of the claim**

- **Location:** §4.5, "Reading-path infrastructure inside the existing entry point" sub-section
- **Claim:** "The methodology already places per-stakeholder reading paths inside `ORIENTATION.md`. These paths are the operational instance of information scent — they tell the reader what to skip in addition to what to read."
- **Evidence gap:** The claim that existing reading paths in `ORIENTATION.md` constitute "operational information scent" is a design inference, not a finding from the literature reviewed. The Information Foraging Theory literature (Pirolli & Card 1999, cited throughout) establishes information scent as the cue that predicts informational payoff for a first-encounter forager — but the mapping from ORIENTATION.md's reading paths to that definition is asserted, not derived. Neither the Q2 nor the Q6 lit-review examines `ORIENTATION.md` as an artifact or makes findings about whether its current reading paths succeed at providing information scent. The claim that enhancement is the intervention ("making the paths sharper, audience-segmented, and explicitly purpose-anchored") follows from the design inference rather than from field or literature evidence. This is a reasonable design hypothesis, but it is presented as if the literature has validated it.
- **Recommendation:** Signal explicitly that this is a design inference from the information-scent framework applied to the existing artifact, not a finding confirmed by the literature. A framing such as "Information scent theory implies that..." or "The IFT framework applied to the existing ORIENTATION.md structure suggests..." would accurately scope the claim without eliminating it.

### P3 — Consider

**Issue 3.1 — §4.5's "visual scent for the corpus" sub-pattern introduces a new artifact (a corpus map diagram) while asserting it does not add to reading load, without examining whether a new diagram inside ORIENTATION.md meets the "no net add" constraint**

- **Location:** §4.5, "Visual scent for the corpus" sub-section
- **Claim:** "A single corpus map — a diagram showing what each artifact covers, with audience and purpose annotations — gives the forager the scent that selects which artifacts to consume and which to skip. The map is a diagram, not a document; it lives inside the existing entry point. This trades a small amount of new visual material for a substantial saving in cognitive overhead spent navigating the corpus."
- **Issue:** The section's constraint — "context-building must not net out to more documentation for the reader to consume" — is applied to the five responses as a set, but the corpus-map sub-pattern introduces new visual material into the existing entry point. The claim that this "trades" new material for a saving in overhead is directionally plausible but is asserted without evidence that the net effect is reduction rather than addition. The Q5 lit-review's findings on diagram quality (Hohpe: diagrams can also be inconsistent, obsolete, or misleading; diagram quality is a variable, not a constant) apply here: a poorly designed corpus map increases cognitive overhead rather than reducing it. The essay presents the corpus-map as straightforwardly reducing reading load, which is a conditional claim stated as unconditional.
- **Recommendation:** Add a qualifier acknowledging that the net-reading-load-reduction of the corpus map is conditional on diagram quality and appropriate granularity — consistent with the §4.4 treatment of diagrams as "well-suited for spatial-structural orientation at appropriate granularity." This is a one-sentence softening consistent with the essay's own §4.4 position.

**Issue 3.2 — The Heijstek (2011) UML caveat from R1 P3-2 remains unaddressed**

- **Location:** §4.4, Heijstek citation; §5, Heijstek open question
- **Status:** The R1 P3.2 finding asked for a brief parenthetical at §4.4's Heijstek citation noting that the experiment used formal UML. §5 acknowledges the gap ("Heijstek (2011) sample caveat is noted in §5 but not at the point of citation in §4.4"). The R2 essay does not add this parenthetical at §4.4. The gap persists.
- **Recommendation:** Add "the experiment used formal UML diagrams" in parentheses after the Heijstek citation in §4.4. The §5 qualification alone is not adequate for a reader who treats §4.4 as the primary reference. This was a P3 in R1 and remains so.

**Issue 3.3 — The Diátaxis "maps the same divergence" framing from R1 P3-3 remains unaddressed**

- **Location:** §3, framework 3 (Diátaxis paragraph)
- **Status:** The R1 P3.3 finding asked for softening of "Diátaxis maps the same divergence" to "Diátaxis's purpose-typing framework is consistent with the same divergence." The Q6 lit-review explicitly states that Diátaxis's boundary-crossing warning applies to *type* mixing, not audience mixing, and that "Diátaxis is silent on these dimensions, not prescriptive about them." The R2 essay retains "Diátaxis (Procida) maps the same divergence" in §3 without modification. This overstates the precision of the analogy as identified in R1.
- **Recommendation:** Soften to "provides a structural analog for the same divergence" or "is consistent with the same divergence." One-word change at §3's Diátaxis paragraph. This remains P3.

**Issue 3.4 — §4.5's "in-document orientation leads" sub-pattern cites F-pattern research as "direct" support but the F-pattern is a reading behavior finding, not a documentation design prescription**

- **Location:** §4.5, "In-document orientation leads at the top of each Tier-2 artifact" sub-section
- **Claim:** "F-pattern research is direct: critical information must appear in the upper-left zone of the document or it will not be read on first encounter."
- **Issue:** This is an accurate summary of the F-pattern finding's design implication as expressed in the Q2 lit-review. However, the word "direct" implies the F-pattern research tested this specific intervention (adding a 2–3 sentence orientation lead). What the F-pattern establishes is a reading-behavior baseline (most readers scan rather than read, concentrating attention in the upper portion). The design prescription — add an orientation lead — is a reasonable inference from that baseline, not a directly tested intervention. Morkes and Nielsen (1997, cited in the essay) tested scannable layout as a design condition and measured usability improvement, which is closer to a direct test, though not of orientation leads specifically. Characterizing F-pattern as "direct" support overstates the proximity of the evidence to the specific intervention described.
- **Recommendation:** Change "F-pattern research is direct" to "F-pattern research implies" or "the F-pattern baseline makes this direct." This is a one-phrase precision adjustment consistent with the essay's careful handling of evidence scope elsewhere.

## Section 2: Framing Audit

The framing audit examines what the essay's content selections made visible and what they did not, comparing choices against the full evidence base.

### Question 1: What alternative framings did the evidence support?

The three alternative framings identified in R1 remain available and have been partially addressed by §4.5's addition. Their status under R2 is as follows.

**Alternative framing A (comprehension failure as tooling-gap / context-building problem): Substantially addressed.**
The R1 framing finding that the essay's four responses did not give equal weight to the intervention implied by the strongest empirical anchor (prior-familiarity dominance) has been addressed by §4.5. The new section explicitly opens with "The literature's strongest specific empirical anchor — Ernst & Robillard's prior-familiarity dominance — points toward an intervention at the *reader's context*, not at the document's organization." This is a material improvement. Alternative framing A is no longer underrepresented.

**Alternative framing B ("thread-the-needle" approach is wrong in principle): Unchanged.**
The essay retains the framing that the user's "thread-the-needle" experience may reflect poor threading implementation rather than threading being wrong in principle. The source material continues to support the stronger claim — the Nguyen et al. (2025) empirical finding that agent-optimized density produces readability scores equivalent to legal documents and that "structures optimized for agent orientation create context debt for humans" — as a basis for the "wrong in principle" reading. This finding is cited in the Q2 lit-review but not in the essay. The essay's "better threading" framing remains the dominant one, with the structural-incompatibility reading available only to a reader who consults the lit-review directly.

**Alternative framing C (five threads are independent problems, not a family): Unchanged.**
The essay's integrated synthesis framing continues to treat the five threads as sharing a structural family. This framing is intellectually coherent and more useful for downstream phases than thread-separatism. The R1 framing note stands: this is a framing choice the evidence does not require, and it produces a narrative coherence that slightly overwrites the finding that the mechanisms are not mutually exclusive with no generic ranking.

**New alternative framing introduced by §4.5: The "no net reading load" constraint as itself a framing choice.**
§4.5's organizing constraint — "context-building must not net out to more documentation for the reader to consume" — is presented as a design requirement imported by the user, not as a finding from the literature. Under this constraint, the section evaluates five sub-patterns. But the constraint itself forecloses a class of interventions the literature would support: a single high-value pre-document orientation artifact (a brief conceptual introduction, a one-page narrative sketch of the system) targeted specifically at zero-prior-familiarity readers. The Ernst & Robillard finding suggests such an artifact might have higher leverage than any in-place restructuring. The constraint rules it out by definition — "a reader who arrives at a 50-document corpus and is offered a 51st document as 'the document that orients you to the other 50' has been handed more reading load." This is a valid concern, but it forecloses the highest-leverage intervention identified by the cycle's strongest empirical anchor. A reader who holds the "prior familiarity is the dominant variable" view would see the no-net-add constraint as the most consequential framing choice in §4.5.

*Belief mapping:* For the "no-net-add" constraint to be the right design principle, a reader would need to believe either (a) that reading-load reduction is the correct objective function for addressing the comprehension failure, or (b) that a dedicated zero-prior-familiarity orientation artifact would not actually solve the problem despite the prior-familiarity finding. Neither premise is examined in §4.5. The constraint is asserted as a given, not derived.

### Question 2: What truths were available but not featured?

**Available truth A: Nguyen et al. (2025) provides the strongest available quantitative support for §4.1's companion-file rationale but is not cited.**
The Q2 lit-review retrieved arXiv 2511.12884 (Nguyen et al. 2025), an empirical study finding that CLAUDE.md-style agent context files exhibit a median readability score of 16.6 (legal-document level) and that "structures optimized for agent orientation create context debt for humans." This is the most specific available quantitative evidence for the claim in §4.1 that companion files reduce human reading load by moving agent-context material out of human-facing documents. The essay cites Rukmono et al. (2024) and Ernst & Robillard (2023) as empirical anchors for §4.1 but does not cite Nguyen et al. The omission does not change the essay's conclusions, but §4.1's companion-file rationale would be materially strengthened by including the specific readability-score finding as the empirical basis for "agent-context material is genuinely hostile to human first-encounter reading."

**Available truth B: The "no-net-add" constraint, as applied in §4.5, may be in tension with the Mintlify-observed statistic that AI agents now account for 45% of documentation requests.**
The Q6 lit-review (audience-corpus-organization.md, §7.1) notes Mintlify's finding that AI agents account for approximately 45% of requests on Mintlify-powered documentation sites, suggesting that many artifacts serve both audiences at roughly comparable rates. If a similar proportion holds for RDD's corpus, a single high-value human-orientation artifact would be encountered by agent readers at substantial volume — meaning the "51st document" is also potentially valuable agent context. The no-net-add constraint makes this point moot by design, but the 45% finding is at least relevant to whether the constraint correctly characterizes a dedicated orientation artifact as "more reading load." The essay does not surface this tension, though it does cite the companion-file pattern (which was the Q6 lit-review's resolution to the dual-audience problem). This is a minor framing gap: the constraint's premises are not stress-tested against the evidence that complicated the premise.

**Available truth C: The IFT-and-CLT support for agent-mediated orientation as schema-building is cited in §4.5 but the parallel from the Q4 lit-review (conversation analysis support for interaction-based schema building) is not.**
The Q4 lit-review documents the ExploreLLM CHI 2024 finding that decomposing tasks into visible sub-task structure reduces load for exploratory tasks, and that the absence of a schema forces the receiver to perform structural inference work. This finding is directly relevant to §4.5's agent-mediated orientation candidate — it provides empirical (not just theoretical) support for the claim that "schema-building can happen through interaction as readily as through reading." The essay appeals to IFT and CLT "in principle" support but does not cite the CHI 2024 empirical finding that most directly anchors the claim. A reader evaluating the agent-mediated-orientation candidate at the DECIDE phase would benefit from knowing this empirical basis exists.

### Question 3: What would change if the dominant framing were inverted?

**Dominant framing of §4.5:** Context-building interventions must operate within the existing corpus at the margin, without adding to the reader's required reading load.

**Inverted framing:** A dedicated, minimal zero-prior-familiarity orientation artifact — authored specifically for the failing audience, not embedded in existing documents — is the highest-leverage intervention the cycle's evidence supports.

Under the inverted framing:

- The five sub-patterns of §4.5 become secondary refinements rather than the primary response. Reading-path enhancement, orientation leads, and corpus map are useful but address symptoms rather than the root cause identified by Ernst & Robillard.
- The no-net-add constraint is revealed as a design preference, not an evidence-derived requirement. Adding one well-targeted artifact for a specific failing audience is a different kind of "reading load" than adding noise to an existing corpus.
- Agent-mediated orientation moves from "candidate, not adopted here" to potentially the most direct available implementation of the prior-familiarity intervention: an interactive on-ramp that builds the reader's schemas before they encounter Tier-2 documents.
- The §4.5 closing paragraph's characterization — "the methodology's response to a comprehension failure is not more methodology; it is sharpening what the methodology already produces" — becomes a rhetorical constraint rather than a derived conclusion.

The inverted framing does not falsify §4.5's sub-patterns; they remain defensible responses to real mechanisms. But it reveals that §4.5 embeds a design philosophy (work within what exists; do not add artifacts) that is in tension with the strongest available empirical finding. The inverted framing should be surfaced at the epistemic gate so the user can evaluate whether the constraint should be load-bearing in downstream phases.

### Framing Issues

**Framing P2-A — §4.5's "no net reading load" constraint forecloses the highest-leverage intervention without examining whether the constraint is warranted by the evidence**

- **Location:** §4.5, organizing constraint paragraph and the section as a whole
- **Claim:** "Context-building must not net out to more documentation for the reader to consume." The section takes this as a given and evaluates only interventions that satisfy it.
- **Evidence gap:** The Ernst & Robillard finding — the cycle's strongest specific empirical anchor — points to prior familiarity as the dominant variable. The most direct intervention is something that builds prior familiarity before document encounter, which is necessarily an additional artifact or interaction. The no-net-add constraint eliminates this class of intervention by definition. The constraint is user-provided (the essay characterizes it as "the constraint the cycle adds to this response direction") but is not examined against the evidence that motivated the direction. The Q2 lit-review explicitly flagged: "the cycle's intervention scope should include context-building tools (orientation summaries, conceptual introductions), not just document restructuring." Accepting a constraint that rules out orientation summaries as "more reading load" without examining whether orientation summaries would have different effects than reading-load-as-noise is a framing choice that deserves surfacing.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief acknowledgment in §4.5 that the constraint forecloses the class of interventions most directly suggested by the strongest empirical anchor, and that the DECIDE phase will need to evaluate whether the constraint is correctly scoped. This does not require abandoning the constraint — it requires naming it as a constraint rather than as an obvious design principle.

**Framing P2-B — The agent-mediated-orientation candidate's "fragile distinction" framing underweights the risk that usage-drift makes the distinction operationally void**

- **Location:** §4.5, agent-mediated-orientation sub-section
- **Claim:** The distinction between agent-mediated orientation (intended modality) and AI-as-prosthetic (workaround) is characterized as "genuine but fragile." The section uses this framing to hold the candidate for downstream phases.
- **Evidence gap:** The essay does not establish what would prevent drift from the first to the second under real usage conditions. The research log records the user's normative position precisely: objection to AI as reading prosthetic means humans needing AI to translate documents that look like they should be human-readable directly. An interactive conversational on-ramp designed to orient a zero-prior-familiarity reader is, by the research log's own definition, a human using AI to understand documents they would otherwise find overwhelming — which is structurally equivalent to the rejected pattern. The "intended modality" distinction preserves a conceptual difference but does not provide an operational criterion that would distinguish the two at usage time. The essay implies that the DECIDE phase can establish this criterion; the source material provides no basis for that confidence.
- **Recommendation:** The essay should explicitly name that no criterion currently exists that would distinguish the two at usage time, and that whether such a criterion can be established is itself an open question for downstream phases — not merely whether to adopt the modality. This softens the implied confidence that DECIDE can resolve the distinction, which the evidence does not support.

**Framing P3-A — Nguyen et al. (2025) absent from essay despite being the strongest available empirical anchor for §4.1's companion-file rationale**

- **Location:** §4.1, companion-file rationale; §2 Sources Reviewed
- **Claim:** The companion-file pattern is supported by the literature as reducing human reading load when agent-context material is moved out of human-facing documents.
- **Evidence gap:** The Q2 lit-review surfaced Nguyen et al. (2025) as providing specific empirical grounding — median readability score 16.6 for agent context files — for the human-agent structural divergence the essay argues in §4.1. The essay cites Rukmono et al. (2024) and Ernst & Robillard (2023) as empirical anchors but not Nguyen et al. The omission is not blocking but represents a synthesis selection that weakens §4.1's evidentiary base relative to what the lit-review made available.
- **Recommendation:** Cite Nguyen et al. (2025) in §4.1's companion-file rationale as the empirical quantification of the human-readability cost of agent-optimized density. One sentence with the readability-score finding would suffice. This is a P3 because the section's argument holds without it; it is noted for completeness and because the DECIDE phase may cite §4.1 as evidence, at which point the stronger empirical anchor becomes more important.

**Framing P3-B — The essay's §4.5 closing paragraph characterizes the five responses as a coherent set but the set is heterogeneous on the "no net add" criterion the section establishes**

- **Location:** §4.5, closing paragraph ("The five responses, taken together, share a structural property worth naming...")
- **Claim:** "Each operates at the margin of the existing corpus rather than producing a parallel corpus. Companion files sit beside, not above... Context-building tools enhance entry points and orientation leads rather than introducing new top-level reading."
- **Issue:** The corpus-map sub-pattern introduces new visual material into `ORIENTATION.md`. Companion files are by definition additive new files. The closing paragraph's characterization — "each operates at the margin of the existing corpus" — applies cleanly to reading-path enhancement and in-document orientation leads, partially to the corpus map and companion files, and not at all to agent-mediated orientation as a candidate. The "shared structural property" claim is broadly true in spirit but is overstated in the characterization. A downstream reader who parses the claim literally will note that companion files are new documents and the corpus map is new content.
- **Recommendation:** Soften "each operates at the margin of the existing corpus" to "each operates at or near the margin of the existing corpus, adding minimally rather than producing a parallel structure." One-clause adjustment that preserves the rhetorical point without overstating the uniformity of the five responses.

## Summary

The R2 essay successfully resolves all six R1 findings. The P1 correction (Ernst & Robillard scope at point of invocation) is now load-bearing at the right location. The four P2 corrections (primary-mechanism framing, four-mechanism ledger attribution, continuous-documentation empirical weight, methodology credit scope) are all applied accurately. The two framing corrections (Decker citation and context-building underrepresentation) are addressed.

The new §4.5 is the substantive addition under review. Its core move is sound: it closes the most consequential framing gap from R1 by explicitly grounding the fifth response in the cycle's strongest empirical anchor. The section is well-organized and holds the agent-mediated-orientation candidate with evident care. No P1 issues arise from the addition.

Three P2 issues surface in R2, all introduced by or related to §4.5:

- **Issue 2.1** is the most consequential: the §4.5 closing sentence re-introduces the methodology-self-credit framing that the §6 revision was specifically asked to correct. These two paragraphs now point in opposite directions, and the §4.5 sentence's rhetorical memorability gives it disproportionate weight relative to its precision.

- **Issue 2.2** surfaces a confidence claim the evidence cannot support: the essay implies that the DECIDE phase can establish an operational criterion distinguishing agent-mediated orientation from AI-as-prosthetic, when the source material provides no basis for this.

- **Issue 2.3** is a standard evidence-scope issue: the "reading-path infrastructure" claim is a design inference from the IFT framework presented as if the literature had validated it for the specific artifact.

The four P3 issues (§4.5's corpus-map conditionality, the persisting Heijstek UML parenthetical, the persisting Diátaxis "maps" softening, and the F-pattern "direct" precision) are minor and do not affect the essay's core argument.

The framing audit's most significant new finding is the no-net-add constraint (Framing P2-A). This constraint is the structural backbone of §4.5, yet it forecloses the class of interventions most directly suggested by the cycle's strongest empirical anchor. The constraint may be warranted — it reflects a genuine concern about compounding the problem — but it is presented as an obvious design principle rather than as a consequential choice the evidence does not require. It should be surfaced at the epistemic gate as a user judgment, not absorbed as settled.

The essay is ready for the epistemic gate with Issue 2.1 applied. Issues 2.2, 2.3, and the Framing P2 items are surfaced for user judgment. The P3 items are minor and can be applied or deferred without affecting gate readiness.
