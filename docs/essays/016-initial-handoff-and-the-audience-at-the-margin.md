# Initial-Handoff and the Audience at the Margin
*2026-04-27*

## Abstract

External stakeholders consistently report a pattern when handed a mature RDD artifact corpus: the entry-point document (`ORIENTATION.md`) reads well, but the substantive Tier-2 documents (system design, product discovery) become overwhelming once the reader passes the entry point — to the degree that the de facto workaround is feeding the docs to AI assistants for summarization. Cycle 017 investigated this through five literature reviews spanning cognitive science, information foraging, technical communication, conversation analysis, and human-vs-agent documentation practice. The investigation produced a material recalibration of the diagnosis: the most structurally fundamental mechanism for the comprehension failure is *context-mismatch* between initial-handoff reading (zero schema, zero information scent) and the ongoing-reference reading the documents were designed for, with density-ordering and artifact-form effects as separable additional mechanisms rather than the unifying explanation. The literature's supported response is not directory-level audience separation but a small set of structural moves operating at the margin of the existing corpus, with the optimization target being human-side cognitive economy rather than total artifact count: context-building enhancements that build human-reader schemas via in-place restructuring, agent-facing context that humans need not consume, and small purpose-built human-facing artifacts admitted only where cognitive economy is positive; companion files at predictable paths for genuinely-divergent agent-context content; upstream decomposition for multi-item agent turns; continuous documentation patterns for the corpus-internal-identifier graduation problem; and diagrams as one tool in an audience-adaptive explanation toolkit rather than as a universal substitute for prose.

## 1. The cycle's entry point

A research-driven development methodology produces a layered artifact corpus — essays, product discovery, domain models, architecture decision records, system design, scenarios, interaction specifications, roadmaps, and a top-level orientation document. The corpus is designed to be self-supporting: a reader new to the project should be able to navigate from the orientation document outward to whatever depth the situation requires. Field experience across multiple iterations of building these corpora and handing them off to external technical stakeholders surfaces a consistent pattern. The orientation document succeeds at its first-encounter function. Stakeholders report the corpus is "fantastic" at that level. Once they attempt to read the substantive Tier-2 documents, the experience changes. Reading becomes overwhelming. Stakeholders bypass direct reading and feed the documents into their own AI assistants for summarization and interpretation.

The methodology's larger purpose, as the user articulates it, is to use generative AI to speed up toil while preserving understanding. Stakeholders routing around the documents and using AI as a reading prosthetic is not the failure of *AI as code-construction-context-builder* — that role is acknowledged as legitimate. It is the failure of direct human readability for documents the methodology presents as primary readables. The pattern is a specific instance of a broader concern in current technical work about AI's effects on comprehension and agency (Decker 2026); the cycle engages that concern narrowly through its readability and turn-structure threads, not through Decker's full political and equity argument. The cycle takes care of the user a step further: making the human experience of working within the framework feel coherent rather than overwhelming.

Five threads were brought into the cycle at entry. Corpus-internal vocabulary — work-package identifiers, ADR numbers, axis labels — leaking into durable code and tests, where the references later become dangling under reorganization or graduation. Tier-2 document legibility on first encounter, particularly the practice of leading with high-density structural content (an architectural-drivers table, a stakeholder map) without first establishing orientation. Folder hygiene, specifically whether infrastructure-style artifacts (audit reports, dispatch logs, session documents) should live at hierarchical equivalence with substantive product artifacts. Verbosity, captured in the heuristic "1500 words to say what could be 500 plus a diagram." And agent interaction pattern, where the agent's default response shape under multi-item pressure (clarifying questions plus decision context plus gate response in a single turn) feels hostile to comprehension.

The user's entry hypothesis was that these threads share a mechanism: high information density front-loaded in the documents, with the methodology's existing design optimized for production discipline at the cost of readerly and interactional usability. The cycle was instructed to test this hypothesis rather than build from it as a premise.

## 2. The constraint-removal: an audience finding the user did not enter with

ADR-082's question-isolation entry protocol calls for naming the most consequential existing artifact in the research context and asking what the problem would look like if that artifact were not available. For this cycle, the named artifact was the system design document — the Tier-2 readable that external technical stakeholders concretely bounce off. The user's response did not defend the artifact's current shape. It partitioned the document into three regions with distinguishable audience-value profiles. The system design diagram carries the load-bearing element of the artifact's high-level orientation function for human technical stakeholders. Module descriptions are hedged as "perhaps helpful." The remainder of the document — extended structural detail, architectural drivers, fitness criteria — was characterized as "more helpful to the AI agent in constructing context for what is built or to be built." That is, agent-consumption material rather than human-reader material.

This is an audience-model finding the cycle did not enter with. The current artifact serves at least two audiences (human technical stakeholders and AI agents preparing to build) with a single artifact-shape. The two audiences want different things from the artifact. The user's prior hypothesis (front-loaded density) is not directly supported by this response. The response says "the diagram carries the value; the rest is mostly for agents" — a different mechanism (audience conflation) than density-ordering, and a different lever for intervention.

The cycle held this finding as a refined hypothesis, not as confirmed mechanism. The user's experience-based diagnosis — that the corpus has tried to "thread the needle" with documents serving agents and humans alike, and that the threading is now producing hard-to-comprehend documents — was recorded as a candidate diagnosis to test against literature, not as a settled premise. Both hypotheses (density-ordering and audience-conflation) entered the research loop as competing candidate mechanisms.

## 3. Diagnosis recalibration: context-mismatch as the candidate primary mechanism

Five lit-reviews converged on a finding that neither the user's entry hypothesis nor the constraint-removal's refinement quite anticipated. The most empirically supported and structurally fundamental mechanism is **context-mismatch**: initial-handoff and ongoing-reference are structurally different reading tasks, and the documents were designed for the latter while being encountered as the former. Four converging frameworks support this account. (Density-ordering, audience-conflation, and artifact-form mismatch remain real and independent mechanisms; the claim is structural fundamentality and convergent support, not exclusive causal primacy.)

**Cognitive Load Theory** (Sweller 1988, 1998, 2019) supplies the mechanism. A first-encounter reader has zero schemas for the artifact. Intrinsic cognitive load — the load arising from element interactivity in novel material — is at maximum. Any extraneous load imposed by the presentation design at this moment compounds an already-overloaded system. Front-loading dense reference material applies maximum extraneous load at the precise moment intrinsic load is highest. The reader does not fail because the document is poorly written; they fail because the document presupposes a schema they do not have.

**Information Foraging Theory** (Pirolli & Card 1999) supplies the motivational account. A first-encounter reader has zero *information scent* — the proximal cues that predict where informational payoff lies. Scent is the first-encounter reader's only navigation tool, because they have no prior internal map of the document structure. A document that assumes prior navigation knowledge — section cross-references, unexplained indexing conventions, lookup-optimized structure — provides no scent for the forager and will be abandoned or scanned superficially. The literature's name for the missing capability is exactly the human-vs-agent distinction the cycle's constraint-removal surfaced: foragers consume scent and decide what to retrieve; recipients consume what is provided. The two audiences need fundamentally different artifact affordances.

**Diátaxis** (Procida) maps the same divergence to documentation purpose. Tutorials serve learners acquiring orientation; reference serves established practitioners consulting information during work. An external reviewer encountering a Tier-2 reference document for the first time has tutorial or explanation needs, not reference needs. The artifact-form mismatch is structural, not stylistic.

**The expertise reversal effect** (Kalyuga et al. 2003) supplies the strongest single empirical anchor. Designs effective for novice first-encounter readers lose their effectiveness for expert returning-reference readers, and vice versa — a measurable comprehension difference, not a preference difference. A returning practitioner's schemas make orientation scaffolding redundant and potentially interference-generating; a new collaborator's lack of schemas makes dense reference material genuinely incomprehensible at first encounter. The same artifact cannot optimally serve both reading tasks without paying measurable costs to at least one audience.

The most direct empirical finding comes from Ernst & Robillard (2023), who found in a study of architecture documentation comprehension (n=65) that **documentation format was not a significant predictor of comprehension; prior familiarity with the source code was the dominant variable**. The finding's strongest support is for *applied and creative tasks* requiring use of source code, not specifically orientation-level comprehension by zero-prior-exposure readers — the exact failing scenario in the cycle's field evidence. Whether prior-familiarity dominance generalizes to that scenario is not directly tested by the study and remains open. With that scope qualification held, the finding still recalibrates the cycle's diagnostic frame substantially: the cycle entered believing the failure was about how the documents were *organized*; the strongest available empirical finding suggests reader context is at least as consequential as document organization, and possibly more so.

The cycle's entry hypothesis (front-loaded density) is not falsified. Density-ordering remains a real and independent mechanism. Sweller's coherence principle and front-loading research give it independent support. Even after context-mismatch is addressed, density-ordering will continue to fail first-encounter readers. But density-ordering is one mechanism among several rather than the unifying explanation. The constraint-removal's refinement (audience conflation) is similarly real but addressable within unified artifacts via content-level layering — it does not require directory-level separation. The user's "thread-the-needle is failing" diagnosis is more nuanced than first held: layered delivery from shared source IS a refined form of threading the needle, and the user's experience that current threaded-needle documents are hard to comprehend may indicate poor implementation of threading, not that threading is wrong.

The cycle's threads share a structural family — context, audience, and reading-task awareness in artifact and turn design — rather than a single mechanism. The unifying frame the user proposed at entry ("optimized for production discipline; this cycle shifts toward readerly and interactional usability") is partially supported as a *direction* but not as a single-mechanism *diagnosis*. The threads need distinct interventions calibrated to their distinct mechanisms.

## 4. Five supported responses

### 4.1 Companion files for dual-audience artifacts

The literature's preferred answer for serving humans and AI agents from the same body of project knowledge is not directory-level audience separation. Diátaxis, DITA, and Information Mapping — the three classical documentation taxonomies — all organize by content *type* (tutorial, how-to, reference, explanation), not by audience type. None addresses the human-vs-agent axis directly. DITA handles audience separation as a metadata attribute (`@audience`) with conditional processing output; the answer is *tagging, not folder structure*. The 2024–2025 practitioner consensus bifurcates by scenario. Where human-facing and agent-facing material draw on the same facts presented differently (a documentation site, a knowledge base), unified source with audience-conditional rendering or frontmatter layering is preferred — separate corpora create maintenance divergence and synchronization failure. Where the content is functionally divergent — agent-facing material requires different facts than human-facing material — the supported pattern is **parallel-sibling companion files at predictable paths**: `llms.txt` alongside HTML on a website (Howard 2024); `AGENTS.md` alongside `README.md` in a code repository. Companion files are *additive*, not *substitutive*. The primary corpus retains its type-based organization. Audience optimization happens at the margin, via files that are easy to find by convention rather than by reorganization of the primary tree.

A second consideration emerges from the agent side. Comprehensive unified documents may actively degrade agent performance by burying critical context in the lost-in-the-middle zone of long context windows. This is a genuine argument for a purpose-built, compressed agent-facing artifact — but the argument supports a companion file, not a separate directory. The companion file pattern accommodates both halves: a focused agent-context file at a predictable path solves the compression problem for agents while the primary corpus retains coherent type-based organization for humans.

The implication for RDD's existing structure: the methodology's `housekeeping/` placement (ADR-070) for infrastructure artifacts (dispatch logs, migration markers, cycle status) is well-supported by the dotfile convention (`.github/`, `.config/`). The methodology's question of whether to extend that placement to the agent-context portions of `system-design.md` is partially answered by the literature: do not extend it as a directory move. The supported pattern is either (a) audience-tagged sections within the existing artifact rendered conditionally, or (b) a companion file at a predictable path (`AGENTS.md`-style) that holds the agent-context material, leaving the human-facing artifact with the diagram and human-readable orientation it carries best. The choice between (a) and (b) depends on the content-divergence threshold — whether the human and agent versions need genuinely different facts. That is a per-artifact judgment, not a literature-settleable principle.

### 4.2 Upstream decomposition for multi-item agent turns

The cycle's thread on agent turn structure entered with a candidate solution: when the agent has multiple items needing user attention in a single turn, enumerate them before walking through. The literature confirms this pattern addresses two of the four mechanisms underlying multi-item-turn comprehension failure — but leaves two unaddressed.

The four mechanisms are distinguishable. The Q4 lit-review's synthesis distinguishes them by drawing on independent sources rather than from a single unified framework in the literature; the four-mechanism account is the lit-review's analytical structure, not a directly cited model. *Structural ordering* — high-density content front-loaded without orientation. *Volume* — too many items, exceeding the working memory ceiling Cowan (2001) places at three to five chunks. *Scope* — the agent mixing heterogeneous concern types (a gate response, clarifying questions, decision context) that should have been separated upstream into distinct turns. *Priority opacity* — the user cannot determine which item demands a response, and Sperber & Wilson's relevance theory predicts users rationally deprioritize underprocessed items rather than working harder. The literature documents the underlying phenomenon empirically. Nielsen Norman Group eyetracking research (Nielsen 2006 and subsequent studies in the same program) finds approximately 79% of readers scan and 16% read fully on first encounter of long content. Srinivasaprasad et al. (2025) find unstructured chatbot responses elevate physiological arousal and perceived effort. The Nielsen Norman Group's accordion-editing study finds users rework long AI outputs rather than comprehending them.

The "enumerate-then-walk-through" pattern resolves volume (by chunking the items into a list) and priority (by signaling order). It does not resolve structural ordering (the content within each item may still front-load density) or scope. *Scope* is the most neglected mechanism in the cycle's framing. The agent should decide, before producing the turn, that a gate response and clarifying questions belong in separate turns — not just that they should be enumerated before delivery. Pragmatics and conversation analysis support this account directly. Grice's maxims of quantity and manner prohibit over-contribution per turn. Adjacency-pair structure shows why multiple simultaneous first-pair-parts produce recipient design failure: the addressee follows the highest-relevance path and abandons the rest, which is exactly the field evidence the cycle entered with — clarifying questions go unanswered because the gate response is more relevant in the moment.

The implication is that turn structure is a layered design problem: upstream decomposition first (does this even belong in one turn?), then within-turn structural ordering (lead with orientation, not density), then volume control (chunk to working-memory limits), then explicit priority signaling. The methodology's existing turn-structure discipline (visible in the orchestrator skill text and AID gate guidance) addresses pieces of this but does not name the full ledger. The cycle's user-facing turn discipline — short enumeration, walk through one at a time — is a partial pattern that addresses ordering and priority. Encoding the upstream-decomposition lever requires structural anchoring per Invariant 8: a concrete dispatch site (the AID gate, the skill text, a hook prompt) where the agent must decide whether items belong together before producing the turn.

### 4.3 Continuous documentation for nomenclature graduation

The cycle's thread on corpus-internal vocabulary leaking into durable code and tests entered framed as a naming-strategies question. The literature redirects the framing. The most empirically supported lens is doc-code coupling. The "continuous documentation" framing (Rosenbaum 2021) names the lens as a practitioner pattern — documentation should be always-up-to-date, created when most needed, and code-coupled — but the empirical weight belongs to the peer-reviewed studies the framing draws from rather than to the practitioner framework itself, which contains no academic citations. The failure mode is a synchronization problem, not fundamentally a naming problem. Empirical baselines establish that staleness is routine, not marginal. Tan, Wagner & Treude (2024) found in a sample of three thousand-plus GitHub repositories that most contained at least one outdated code-element reference in documentation at some point. Aghajani et al. (2019) found up-to-dateness failures account for roughly one in four documentation content issues across their cross-source study of 878 artifacts. Hata et al. (2019) found approximately ten percent of links in source code comments are dead. Xiao et al. (2023) found seventy percent of distinct links in commit messages decay. Passive trust in reference stability is untenable at any scale.

Naming-strategy choice still matters for the rate at which dangling references accumulate. Opaque identifiers (DOIs, Wikidata Q-numbers) outperform semantic identifiers significantly — Coble & Karlin (2023) found DOIs at 99.1% resolution success against approximately 69% for plain URLs over the same window. The Internet Engineering Task Force's RFC series demonstrates the value of sequential identifiers with immutability and explicit supersession: numbers are assigned once, never reused, and obsolescence is handled by a new number that points back to the old one bidirectionally. The architecture decision record community follows the same pattern. The Stable Dependencies Principle (Martin 2002) supplies a policy implication: corpus artifacts that reference stable codebase surfaces (public APIs, versioned interfaces) accumulate fewer dangling references than those referencing volatile internals.

The literature has a real gap. Empirical research on dangling code-element references runs in the doc→code direction — does the documentation reference code that no longer exists? RDD's specific failure mode (Issue #17) runs in the reverse direction — does the code reference documentation identifiers (work-package numbers, ADR numbers) that no longer exist after corpus reorganization or graduation? This direction is not studied empirically. Graduation ceremonies as a named design problem are not addressed in the literature reviewed. The implication is that RDD has a genuine, novel contribution available here: a graduation-check mechanism that scans codebase candidates for corpus-internal identifier strings before graduation is approved, modeled on Tan et al.'s detection tool but pointing in the reverse direction. This is an engineering contribution drawn from the corpus's own structure rather than a port from prior practice.

### 4.4 Diagrams as one tool in an audience-adaptive toolkit

The constraint-removal response identified the system design diagram as the load-bearing element of the artifact's high-level orientation function for human stakeholders. The cycle held this as a hypothesis to test against literature rather than as confirmed prior art. The literature's verdict is qualified support, not unconditional confirmation.

The cognitive science case is sound but conditioned. Larkin & Simon (1987) establish that diagrams outperform sentential representations specifically for *search-intensive* and *recognition-dependent* problems — content with strong spatial, topological, or parallel-relational structure. Outside those conditions the advantage is not asserted. Dual coding theory (Clark & Paivio 1991) and Mayer's Cognitive Theory of Multimedia Learning (2001) both argue for *integrated* verbal-and-visual representation, not for diagram substitution of prose. Heijstek, Kühne & Chaudron (2011), in a controlled experiment on software architecture documentation (n=47), found text users scored as well or better than diagram users overall, and significantly better on topology-related questions — the authors state the findings "question the role of diagrams in software architecture documentation." Ernst & Robillard (2023), already cited as the strongest empirical anchor for the context-mismatch mechanism, found documentation format was not a significant predictor of comprehension. Rukmono et al. (2024), interviewing seventeen practitioners, found diagrams dominate in practice but are treated as one tool in an audience-adaptive "explanation window," not as a universal substitute.

The cycle's working hypothesis ("diagrams superior for first-encounter human-stakeholder orientation") is not robustly supported. It is conditional on content type and granularity. Diagrams excel for spatial, parallel, and complex content used strategically alongside prose. Brown's C4 model operationalizes this in a useful way: hierarchical zoom levels (system context, container, component, code) explicitly separate stakeholder audiences by diagram granularity. Hohpe (2020) argues diagrams enforce conceptual precision because they are harder to cheat than words. Both findings are consistent with the orientation use case if the diagrams are well-formed and granularity-appropriate.

A provenance correction belongs here. The orientation pass surfaced a frequently-circulated claim — Carnegie Mellon Software Engineering Institute research finding that teams using architecture diagrams report forty percent fewer miscommunications than text-only teams. The Q5 lit-review's direct retrieval found no trace of the statistic in the cited blog post, no matching study in CMU SEI's Digital Library, no traceable primary attribution. The claim circulates in secondary marketing content without source. It is dropped from RDD's reasoning entirely and should not be cited. This is itself an instance of the methodology's research discipline at work: a claim that supported the cycle's preferred direction failed verification and was dropped, not preserved.

The implication for the cycle's response is calibration rather than reversal. Diagrams remain valuable. The system-design.md diagram remains the load-bearing element of that artifact's orientation function for the user's external stakeholders. But the supported claim is narrower than "diagrams superior to prose." It is: diagrams are well-suited for spatial-structural orientation at appropriate granularity, used alongside prose that handles non-spatial content. The cycle's thread on verbosity ("1500 words to say what could be 500 plus a diagram") expresses a real intuition but the actual lever is content-type-to-form matching, not diagram-substitution-of-prose.

### 4.5 Context-building under cognitive-economy on the human side

The literature's strongest specific empirical anchor — Ernst & Robillard's prior-familiarity dominance — points toward an intervention at the *reader's context*, not at the document's organization. The interventions of §4.1–§4.4 are all structural moves on the documents themselves. They make better artifacts. They do not directly address the reader's missing schemas at the moment of first encounter.

The constraint the cycle adds to this response direction is asymmetric, and worth stating precisely: the optimization target is *cognitive economy on the human side*, not *what artifacts exist in the corpus* and not *strict avoidance of any addition to human reading*. More artifacts for agents — companion files holding dense context, structured on-ramps, instruction packets — are explicitly admissible and may be desirable. They offload schema-construction work from humans onto agents. Additions to what humans read are admissible when the addition earns back its cost: a brief purpose-built artifact that builds schemas a zero-prior-familiarity reader needs to navigate the rest of the corpus may pay for itself many times over in reduced cognitive load downstream. What the constraint forecloses is human reading additions that fail this cognitive-economy test — most directly, comprehensive reference material added to a corpus that already overwhelms its readers. The literature supports several moves that satisfy the constraint: enhancing what humans already read, adding small high-leverage human-facing material that earns its keep, and routing schema-construction work through agent-facing artifacts that humans never need to consume directly. Where the asymmetric framing meets a real bound is the agent-mediated-orientation pattern, examined as a candidate at the end of this section: what is acceptable to *load* onto agents depends on whether the agent's role stays clear of the AI-as-reading-prosthetic pattern the user has explicitly rejected.

**Reading-path infrastructure inside the existing entry point.** The methodology already places per-stakeholder reading paths inside `ORIENTATION.md`. IFT implies that such paths function as information scent — telling the reader what to skip in addition to what to read — though the literature reviewed does not directly study `ORIENTATION.md`-style reading paths as artifacts. The candidate intervention is enhancement of the existing structure: making the paths sharper, audience-segmented, and explicitly purpose-anchored — *read these for X; ignore the rest until Y*. This is in-place restructuring within an artifact the reader already has. No new documents.

**In-document orientation leads at the top of each Tier-2 artifact.** F-pattern research is direct: critical information must appear in the upper-left zone of the document or it will not be read on first encounter. The intervention is a 2–3 sentence orientation lead at the top of each Tier-2 document — what the document is, who reads it for what, and where to go if the reader's purpose is different. This is restructuring within existing artifacts. Ordering changes, not volume changes.

**Visual scent for the corpus.** A single corpus map — a diagram showing what each artifact covers, with audience and purpose annotations — gives the forager the scent that selects which artifacts to consume and which to skip. The map is a diagram, not a document; it lives inside the existing entry point. This trades a small amount of new visual material for a substantial saving in cognitive overhead spent navigating the corpus.

**Companion files for agents (§4.1) reduce human reading load.** The dual-audience pattern is itself a context-building move that nets *less* reading for the human. When agent-context material is moved out of human-facing documents and into a companion file at a predictable path, human readers stop encountering that material as overhead. The companion-file pattern simultaneously addresses the audience-conflation mechanism from §3 and the cognitive-economy constraint of this section.

**Brief purpose-built orientation artifacts where cognitive economy is positive.** Ernst & Robillard's prior-familiarity dominance — within the scope qualification of §3 (their study covered applied and creative tasks, not orientation-level comprehension specifically) — points directionally toward this intervention: a zero-prior-familiarity reader's strongest leverage point is acquiring schemas needed to make the rest of the corpus tractable, before encountering the substantive Tier-2 documents. The literature on tutorials in Diátaxis (Procida) and explanation-adaptation patterns (Rukmono et al. 2024) supports brief purpose-built artifacts as a documented design pattern for orientation-style reading tasks. The cognitive-economy test — whether the artifact's reading cost pays back in reduced cognitive load downstream — depends on schema-carry-forward from a pre-read into subsequent comprehension, a mechanism the cycle's reviewed literature motivates directionally but does not directly establish empirically. A 1–2 page tutorial-style on-ramp introducing the corpus's domain vocabulary, key constraints, and reading paths can build schemas that make subsequent reading tractable — net-positive in cognitive economy even though it adds reading time. The constraint that makes this a sustainable pattern: such artifacts must remain *brief*, *purpose-built*, and *targeted at zero-prior-familiarity readers as the named audience*, not the corpus's general readership. A multi-page exhaustive introduction becomes the next overwhelming document and reverses the cognitive economy. Operationalizing the cognitive-economy test for a specific candidate artifact is itself a design decision the cycle defers to DECIDE.

**Agent-mediated orientation as a deliberate design pattern (candidate, not adopted here).** A sharper distinction is worth surfacing. The user has objected to *AI as reading prosthetic* — humans needing AI to translate documents that look like they should be human-readable directly. *Agent-mediated orientation* is a different role in concept: an interactive, conversational on-ramp that builds the reader's schemas through dialogue rather than through document reading. The literature on IFT and CLT supports the underlying principle — schema-building can happen through interaction as readily as through reading, and interaction handles zero-prior-context better than dense reference material does. Whether the role is acceptable to the methodology's stakeholders, and whether an *operational* criterion can be established that distinguishes the two roles in practice rather than only in concept, are both open questions. The distinction is conceptually genuine but operationally fragile — it can shade into the rejected pattern under usage drift, and no source in the cycle's reviewed literature establishes a tested criterion for separating them. This is candidate territory for downstream phases; the cycle does not adopt it here, and adoption would itself depend on whether the operational criterion problem can be solved.

The five responses, taken together, illustrate a structural direction worth naming: each operates at the margin of the existing corpus rather than producing a parallel corpus, and the load asymmetry runs in one direction across audiences — human cognitive load shrinks while agent-facing context may grow. The five sub-patterns within §4.5 vary in how directly the cognitive-economy support is established (in-place restructuring is the strongest case; small new artifacts rest on a directional rather than directly-validated mechanism), and that variation is itself something downstream phases will need to address. Companion files sit beside, not above, and they offload material from humans onto agents. Upstream decomposition shapes the agent's turn-production, not the artifact set. Continuous documentation couples docs to code rather than producing new top-level reading. Conditional diagrams supplement prose rather than replacing it. Context-building tools enhance entry points and orientation leads, add agent-facing context that humans need not consume, and admit small targeted human-facing artifacts only where cognitive economy is positive.

## 5. What the literature does not settle

Five questions survive the research loop. They cannot be closed by further lit-review; they require either spike, field study, or are tractable through the cycle's downstream phases rather than further upstream investigation.

The Ernst & Robillard (2023) finding that prior-familiarity dominates format effects was demonstrated for *applied and creative* tasks (questions requiring use of source code). Whether the dominance holds specifically for *orientation-level* comprehension by zero-prior-exposure readers — the exact failing scenario in the cycle's field evidence — is not directly tested by their study. The Heijstek (2011) finding that text users matched or outperformed diagram users used formal UML diagrams; whether the result generalizes to RDD's informal architectural diagrams is not established. The initial-handoff versus ongoing-reference distinction is not formalized as a named reading task in any major empirical research program reviewed; the theoretical case across CLT, IFT, Diátaxis, and the expertise reversal effect is strong, but a direct empirical study of this distinction in technical documentation specifically would close the most consequential remaining gap. No empirical study directly tests "enumerate-then-walk-through" as a named pattern, and the task-type dependency of optimal chunking granularity is unresolved. Whether the C4 zoom-level hierarchy maps coherently onto RDD's three-tier artifact hierarchy is a structural mapping question the cycle's downstream phases will need to settle by design rather than by literature.

These gaps are honest scope-of-claim per Invariant 8 and ADR-069. The methodology cannot guarantee what the literature does not establish. The cycle's findings are well-supported for the diagnosis recalibration and the four response patterns; the open questions name where the methodology operates on partial evidence and downstream phases (DISCOVER's stakeholder model updates, MODEL's vocabulary extensions, DECIDE's ADRs) will be the place where partial evidence becomes structural commitment.

## 6. Implications for downstream phases

The cycle has not yet reached DISCOVER, MODEL, DECIDE, or ARCHITECT. The essay's purpose is to crystallize understanding sufficient to enter those phases with a coherent diagnostic frame and a vocabulary for the design moves the literature supports. Several implications follow.

For DISCOVER, the human-vs-agent stakeholder distinction is empirically grounded and structurally consequential. Both are stakeholders; their needs diverge in ways the literature documents (foragers vs recipients, expertise reversal, agent-vs-human-readability divergence in document design). The product discovery document already names both audiences. The update should make the divergence explicit rather than treating the two as variations of a single stakeholder type. Discovery should additionally treat *zero-prior-exposure first-encounter readers* as a distinct stakeholder type with distinct schema-building needs — the readers most directly addressed by the context-building tooling of §4.5.

For MODEL, the cycle should consider importing the vocabulary the literature has developed for these distinctions. *Forager and recipient* (Pirolli & Card) names the human-vs-agent audience axis. *Expertise reversal effect* (Kalyuga) names the measurable novice-vs-expert reading flip. *F-pattern* (Nielsen) names the first-encounter scanning behavior. *Information scent* names what first-encounter readers lack. *Companion file pattern* names the dual-audience artifact strategy that scales. *Continuous documentation* names the doc-code coupling lens. *Adjacency-pair recipient design failure* names what multi-initiation turns produce. These are not casual additions; they are the discipline of using existing vocabulary instead of re-inventing it, and they connect the methodology's findings to a wider community of practice.

For DECIDE, several candidate ADRs surface. The placement of agent-context content (companion file at predictable path versus audience-tagged sections within existing artifacts versus full artifact split) is an architectural decision that needs its own ADR with rationale, alternatives considered, and consequences. The implementation of the §4.5 context-building tooling — particularly whether agent-mediated orientation is adopted as an intended modality or held out as a candidate not yet endorsed — needs its own ADR that examines the AI-as-orienter / AI-as-prosthetic distinction explicitly, with the user's normative position as load-bearing input. The cognitive-economy constraint itself warrants explicit encoding as a methodology principle: human reading additions are admissible when they earn back their cost in reduced downstream cognitive load, and the test for "earning back" is itself a methodology decision that needs articulation rather than left to per-artifact judgment. A related decision DECIDE should examine: whether the criterion is intended to be purely cognitive or whether non-cognitive admissibility (relational, normative, trust-building value that does not register as cognitive load reduction) belongs alongside the cognitive criterion as a separately-evaluable axis. The methodology debt items the cycle inherited from prior session hotfixes — the ADR-064 amendment for the demoted phase-manifest check, the In-progress-phase-mechanism role-shift documentation, and the question of whether ADR-070's `housekeeping/` placement should be revised in light of this cycle's findings — all need to land here. The cycle's specific question, "should primarily-agent-facing documentation be scoped to a dedicated subdirectory?", has a literature-supported answer that informs the ADR but does not write it: the answer is "no, use companion files at predictable paths or in-document audience tagging — not directory separation."

For ARCHITECT, the system-design document is itself an artifact this cycle has analyzed in detail. The cycle's findings have direct implications for how that document is organized at the next iteration. The diagram retains its load-bearing role. The agent-context material has a literature-supported relocation pattern (companion file). Whatever choices the cycle commits to in DECIDE will need structural realization in the system-design document itself — the cycle's research will become its own dogfood at that point, and the writing of the next system-design will be a test of whether the diagnosis was correct.

For BUILD, the nomenclature-leakage thread (Issue #17) names a graduation-check mechanism that reverses the direction of Tan et al.'s existing detection tool. This is a candidate work package: a pre-graduation scan that detects corpus-internal identifier strings in codebase candidates before they ossify into durable artifacts. The mechanism is structurally anchored at the moment of graduation, satisfying Invariant 8.

The cycle's largest second-order finding may be the methodological one. The user's entry hypothesis (front-loaded density) was a partial finding that, taken alone, would have led to the wrong intervention (verbosity reduction, ordering changes within Tier-2 documents). The constraint-removal's refinement (audience conflation) was a closer finding that, taken alone, would have led to a different wrong intervention (directory-level audience separation). The literature's recalibration to context-mismatch as the structurally fundamental mechanism, with the four-mechanism Q4 ledger and the companion-file Q6 pattern and the continuous-documentation Q1 lens, produces an integrated diagnostic frame that none of the entry hypotheses anticipated. The structured research loop scaffolds this recalibration without authoring it. Three co-contributing factors made the recalibration possible: the user's openness to revising hypotheses across stages rather than defending the entry framing; the lit-reviewer specialist subagents operating in isolated contexts that exploited Architectural Isolation, providing the second-order critique the in-context agent cannot generate independently; and the question-isolation protocol's structural requirement that the question set be articulated and reviewed before the corpus is consulted, surfacing assumptions the user had imported unconsciously. Without any one of these, the cycle would have implemented a coherent and partially-correct fix to a partially-incorrect diagnosis. The methodology's research discipline is the scaffold; the recalibration is the joint product of scaffold, openness, and isolated evaluation.
