# The Outline as Exhibition: Form, Audience, and Curation in RDD Synthesis
*2026-03-10*

## Abstract

The RDD synthesis skill produces an outline — citation-audited, argument-audited — that a writer can use as a springboard for an essay, talk, or other communicative act. But what makes a good outline for this purpose? This essay investigates the question through six research threads: established narrative structures for nonfiction (Bascom, Miller & Paola), experimental and "wild" forms including the hermit crab essay (Miller & Paola) and constraint-based writing (OuLiPo), the relationship between discovery type and narrative form, audience and medium as form-shaping forces, epistemic posture and indeterminacy (Montaigne, John Cage), and curation as authorship (the Museum of Jurassic Technology, the wunderkammer tradition). The central finding is that the outline is not a blueprint but an exhibition design — a curated arrangement of artifacts from the research trail where meaning emerges from selection, juxtaposition, and the spaces between. Four dimensions shape its construction: discovery type, narrative form, audience and medium, and epistemic posture. The framing conversation should navigate all four — not by discussing forms in the abstract but by trying them on, because discovery comes from the outside: from externalized structural experimentation, not from thinking about structure. The synthesis phase is also not always terminal; the act of finding the form can generate new questions that re-enter the research cycle.

---

## 1. The Thin Vocabulary Problem

The RDD synthesis skill, as designed in essay 003, moves through three conversational phases: journey review, novelty surfacing, and framing. The framing conversation is where the writer and agent co-produce the outline — the central question, the volta, the structural form. The skill offers a set of narrative tools for this conversation: the ABT sentence, the story spine, the braided structure, the volta placement. It also proposes three inversions the writer can try: the obvious takeaway is wrong, the process is more interesting than the product, the reader's assumed context is the story.

These tools work. But the vocabulary is thin. "Braided structure" is mentioned alongside "story spine" as though they are comparable items on a menu, when in fact they operate at different levels of abstraction. The ABT is a sentence-level tool; braiding is a structural pattern; the inversions are framing moves. The framing conversation needs a richer, more organized repertoire — one that helps the writer navigate not just "what structure?" but "what kind of telling? for whom? in what posture toward certainty?"

The question is not whether the outline should have a fixed format — ADR-013 correctly rejects that. The question is whether the framing conversation has enough range to help the writer discover the form their material demands.

## 2. Seven Shapes and the Hermit Crab

Writing craft literature offers a taxonomy of structural patterns for essay-length nonfiction. Tim Bascom's "Picturing the Personal Essay" identifies visual shapes that correspond to different structural logics:

The **narrative with a lift** builds chronologically through tension toward a turning point — a rising diagonal. The **whorl of reflection** circles a subject from multiple angles without predetermined conclusion — a spiral. **Formal limits of focus** create coherence through disciplined exclusion, chiseling away everything outside a strict temporal or spatial frame — a rectangle. The **segmented essay** arranges discrete framed moments separated by white space, unified by theme rather than chronology — a row of boxes. The **braided structure** weaves two or more narrative strands that alternately surface and submerge, each enriched by the others — interlocking curves. The **lyric essay** prioritizes image, mood, and compression over logical argument — waves or diagonal slashes. **Dipping into the well** interrupts forward narrative with vertical descents into philosophical depth, then resurfaces — a line with downward loops.

These patterns are not mutually exclusive. A lyric essay can be braided; a segmented essay can contain vertical descents. They are composable structural primitives, and the framing conversation can draw on them as a vocabulary rather than a menu.

But the most consequential form for synthesis may be one that sits outside this taxonomy entirely. Brenda Miller and Suzanne Paola coined the term **hermit crab essay** in *Tell It Slant* (2003): an essay that inhabits the shell of a non-essay form. An essay structured as a recipe, a field guide, a job application, an obituary, a set of prescription side effects. The borrowed form is not decoration. Miller and Paola's central claim is that it *generates content the writer would not have found otherwise*: "allowing form to dictate content" helps writers "bypass what our intellectual minds have already determined as 'our story.'"

This is the formal name for an impulse that surfaced naturally in the research for this essay: telling the story of an RDD cycle in the form of an algorithm, or as the dream of a machine, or as a Borgesian review of imaginary software, or as a Knuthian literate program where the essay IS the code explained. These are not whimsical suggestions. They are instances of a principle with a research pedigree: formal constraint unlocks creative discovery.

The OuLiPo tradition — the "Ouvroir de littérature potentielle" founded by Queneau and de Lionnais in 1960 — provides the philosophical foundation. Constraints are not limitations but catalysts. Queneau's *Exercises in Style* retells a single mundane anecdote in ninety-nine stylistically distinct ways. The constraint forces the writer to discover content and meaning that free composition would never surface. Each retelling reveals a different aspect of the same event — not because the event changed, but because the form changed what was visible.

But the OuLiPo principle, stated as a literary technique, understates the mechanism at work. Ensemble-based devising traditions in theater — Viewpoints, Laban, the practices of companies like SITI Company, Double Edge Theatre, and Frantic Assembly — make a stronger claim: **discovery comes from the outside.** The compelling material does not emerge from planning or deliberation. It emerges from physical, externalized experimentation with structure. The practitioner creates an unexpected arrangement — of bodies in space, of movement qualities, of temporal patterns — and the content reveals itself through the doing. The form is not a container for pre-existing content. The form is the instrument of discovery.

This has a direct implication for how the synthesis framing conversation should work. The conversation should not be purely discursive — discussing forms in the abstract and selecting one. It should involve *trying forms on*: drafting an opening paragraph as a recipe, sketching the talk as an Ignite deck, arranging the artifacts as a gallery walk — not to commit to any of them but to see what each reveals. The form is a lens, and the writer does not know what it will show until they look through it.

The synthesis skill already sits in a space that invites hermit-crab thinking. RDD artifact trails include structured decision records, formal domain models, reflective prose, research logs, behavior scenarios. The trail is already multi-genre. An outline that proposes inhabiting one of those genres — or a genre from entirely outside the trail — may produce a more interesting piece of writing than a conventional essay ever could. And the act of trying on a shell that does not fit is not wasted work. It is diagnostic: the mismatch between form and material reveals what the material actually needs — sometimes more clearly than deliberation ever could.

## 3. Discovery Type as Structural Suggestion

The synthesis skill mines the artifact trail for five novelty signals: explicit surprise, reframing, assumption denial, negative case integration, and superseded decisions. These signals identify *what* is interesting. But they also carry implicit suggestions about *how* to tell it.

Different types of discovery have structural affinities based on where their energy lives:

An **explicit surprise** — "I was wrong about X" — concentrates energy at the moment of reversal. The turn IS the story. This naturally suggests the narrative with a lift, where chronological tension builds toward the volta, or any structure that foregrounds a single dramatic pivot.

A **reframing** — the problem definition itself changed — distributes energy across a conceptual shift. The new frame is the discovery. This suggests either the whorl of reflection (circling the subject until the new frame becomes visible) or the hermit crab (the new frame literally becomes the form the essay inhabits — the reframe enacted, not just described).

An **assumption denial** concentrates energy in the gap between what the audience believes and what the evidence shows. This suggests structures that build the assumption before dismantling it — the segmented essay that constructs a familiar picture in its first sections and then reveals why it is wrong, or the Borgesian inversion that presents the expected world and then rotates it.

A **negative case integration** places energy in the tension between the main account and its counter-example. The braided structure is its natural home: one strand carries the primary narrative, the other carries the counter-example, and their weaving creates meaning that neither strand produces alone.

**Superseded decisions** distribute energy across a temporal arc of evolving understanding. The segmented essay — each decision as a section, showing the progression of thought — or the dialogue form (past self vs. present self, or one ADR arguing with a later one) lets this evolution become the structure rather than background.

This mapping is speculative. The writing pedagogy literature does not explicitly connect discovery types to structural forms. But the logic is principled: each novelty type has an energetic center, and structural forms differ in where they place emphasis. The synthesis framing conversation can use this mapping not as prescription but as a starting offer — "Your research trail shows a reframing. What if the essay itself enacted that reframe by inhabiting the new frame as its form?" — that is more generative than "pick from a list."

## 4. Audience and Medium as Creative Constraint

The synthesis skill, as designed in essay 003, assumes the output is a written essay. But the writer's material might call for a conference talk, a nerd nite presentation, a Story Collider spoken-word piece, a five-minute Ignite talk, an explanation to a child, or a pitch to strangers on the street. Each of these imposes constraints that function exactly like OuLiPo rules — they force creative compression and surface content that unconstrained writing misses.

The constraints differ in kind. A Story Collider piece (ten minutes, no slides, true personal story) demands that the writer find *themselves* inside the technical work — the science becomes the setting, not the subject. A nerd nite talk (fifteen to twenty minutes, bar, beer, humor, "expert or over-informed") demands deep knowledge delivered with entertainment. An Ignite talk (five minutes, twenty slides auto-advancing every fifteen seconds) demands that the writer identify whether they actually have *one* point. Explaining to a child — the Feynman principle, taken to its extreme in Randall Munroe's *Thing Explainer* with its vocabulary of only the thousand most common words — demands that the writer verify whether they actually understand their own material.

Nancy Duarte's *Resonate* provides a structural framework specific to presentations: the sparkline, an oscillation between "what is" and "what could be" that transforms the audience from passive listeners into participants in a journey. The audience is the hero; the speaker is the mentor. This is a fundamentally different structural logic than the essay's, and it produces a fundamentally different outline.

The key insight is that audience constraint works exactly like the hermit crab's borrowed shell. It generates content the writer would not have found otherwise. "Explain your domain model to a five-year-old" might reveal something about the model that the formal essay would not. "Give this as a Story Collider piece" might force the writer to discover why this work matters to them personally — not just technically. "Pitch this to a stranger on the street" strips away every assumption about shared context and reveals whether the core insight can stand alone.

This gives the synthesis framing conversation a powerful new move: "Who would you want to tell this to, and how?" The answer shapes the outline as much as the discovery type does. And the hermit crab extends to presentations: a conference talk structured as a cooking show, a nerd nite presentation delivered as nature documentary narration, a workplace presentation framed as a courtroom trial, a lightning talk structured as a magic trick (setup, misdirection, reveal).

## 5. The Essay as Attempt, the Outline as Score

The previous sections assume the writer knows — or can discover through conversation — what they want to say, to whom, and in what form. But sometimes the writer does not know. Sometimes the writing itself is the exploration.

The word "essay" comes from the French *essayer*: to attempt, to test, to try. Montaigne did not use it to name a genre. He used it to describe a posture. "What I write here is not my teaching, but my study; it is not a lesson for others, but for me." He sought "a sinuous language which could digress, circle back, move forward and make unexpected discoveries, where manner and matter were indivisible." His essays are "severed at the moment he senses he is approaching a conclusion" — the process is the product. "I have no more made my book than my book has made me."

For synthesis, this means: sometimes the audience reveals itself *through* the act of writing. The writer does not choose an audience and then compose for them. The writer begins exploring, and the material itself discloses who needs to hear it. The outline for this posture is not a map to a known destination. It is a structured invitation to wander — with enough structure to prevent aimlessness, but enough openness to permit surprise.

John Cage's concept of indeterminacy pushes this further. Cage defined indeterminacy as "the ability of a piece to be performed in substantially different ways." His scores for works like *Variations I* and *II* use transparent plastic sheets with graphical notation that can be repositioned independently, creating specifications for unique instantiations. The score provides measurable parameters and structural relationships, but the realization is different each time. The score is not a script. It is a structured space for possibility.

An indeterminate outline would not be "Section 1: Introduction, Section 2: Background." It would be a set of elements — themes, references, turning points, images, questions — with structural relationships (this resonates with that, this contradicts that, this provides scale to that) but no fixed sequence. The writer realizes the outline differently depending on the medium, the audience, the mood, the moment. The same outline might produce a written essay one week and a nerd nite talk the next, depending on which elements the writer activates and in what order.

Counterfactual and time-travel structures add a temporal dimension to this indeterminacy. RDD trails are rich with superseded decisions, abandoned spikes, and evolved understanding. "What if the other choice had been made at ADR-003?" is not idle speculation — it reveals why the actual choice matters. Counterfactual history seeks "by conjecturing on what did not happen, in order to understand what did happen." The time-travel essay visits a decision point, takes the road not traveled, shows what happens, and returns with new understanding.

These three — Montaigne, Cage, the counterfactual — share a common principle: they reject the assumption that the writer knows where they are going before they start. This introduces a fourth dimension to outline design, alongside discovery type, narrative form, and audience:

**Epistemic posture** — does the writer know their argument (determined), sense something but need the writing to reveal it (exploratory), or have elements and relationships with no fixed realization (indeterminate)?

The posture changes what the outline even *is*. A determined outline is a map. An exploratory outline is a Montaigne-style invitation. An indeterminate outline is a Cage score.

## 6. The Outline as Exhibition

All of the preceding — forms, hermit crabs, discovery mappings, audience constraints, epistemic postures — converge on a metaphor that was not anticipated when the research began but that, in retrospect, integrates the findings more coherently than any of them do individually: the outline is an exhibition.

The Museum of Jurassic Technology, founded by David Hildebrand Wilson in Los Angeles in 1988, operates as a "museum about museums." Its method: deliberate juxtaposition of verifiable scientific artifacts with fabricated histories and impossible inventions. Lawrence Weschler, in *Mr. Wilson's Cabinet of Wonder*, describes the visitor's experience as a **shimmer** — "continually shimmering between wondering at the marvels of nature and wondering whether any of this could possibly be true." Wilson himself: "There is a truth that exists that is beyond a three-dimensional truth, a more complex truth than verifiability. Ecstatic truth is the truth of the imagination."

The museum's ancestor is the Renaissance *wunderkammer* — the cabinet of curiosities. Collectors assembled objects that bore scientific or historical merit alongside outright fabrications. The juxtaposition of disparate objects "encouraged comparisons, finding analogies and parallels." The arrangement created "abrupt transitions of gaze from one object to another, constantly changing the scale of perception for an arresting effect." The collection was personal — it reflected the collector's identity, not a taxonomy. And by the 1980s, curated exhibitions came to be understood as "the creative work of curator-auteurs": the curator does not create the objects but creates meaning through selection, arrangement, juxtaposition, sequence, and the spaces between.

This is precisely what synthesis does with the research trail. The essays, ADRs, spikes, reflections, domain models, and code are the objects. The synthesis skill is the curator. The outline is the exhibition design.

The exhibition metaphor integrates what the other frameworks describe in isolation:

**Selection** — the curator chooses which artifacts to include and which to leave out. Negative space creates meaning. Not every ADR is interesting; not every research question led somewhere worth following. The outline's power comes partly from what it excludes.

**Juxtaposition** — meaning emerges from proximity. A superseded ADR placed next to the spike that killed it tells a story that neither tells alone. A formal domain model term placed next to the informal language the writer used before formalizing it reveals the conceptual journey. The outline arranges artifacts for resonance.

**Scale shifts** — the wunderkammer moves from the cosmic to the microscopic and back. An outline can zoom from a single variable-naming decision to a philosophical implication of that name, and back to the code. The shift in scale is itself a source of wonder.

**The shimmer** — the Museum of Jurassic Technology cultivates the productive state between wonder and doubt. The synthesis outline need not resolve every tension. Sometimes the most honest and most interesting move is to present artifacts in juxtaposition and let the reader shimmer between interpretations. This is not intellectual laziness; it is the recognition that some truths are more complex than any single account can contain.

**The personal voice** — why these objects? Why this arrangement? The curation reveals the curator. The outline is not neutral. It is the writer's statement about what mattered, what surprised them, what they want others to see. This is where Montaigne's posture survives even in a determined outline: the selection is always personal.

The exhibition metaphor also resolves a tension between the argument audit and the curatorial logic. The argument audit checks logical soundness — claims follow from evidence, framing does not overreach. But curation operates by a different logic: associative, resonant, spatial. An exhibition that "works" is not necessarily one where every claim follows deductively from the previous one. It is one where the juxtapositions create a coherent emotional and intellectual experience. The synthesis outline can hold both registers: an argumentative backbone that the audit verifies, and a curatorial arrangement that creates the experience of encountering the ideas.

## 7. Four Dimensions

The research converges on four dimensions that shape how the synthesis outline should be constructed. These are not sequential steps but simultaneous considerations that the framing conversation navigates:

**Discovery type** — what kind of insight did the research trail produce? The five novelty signals (surprise, reframing, assumption denial, negative case integration, superseded decisions) each carry implicit structural suggestions. The framing conversation should surface which signals are strongest and offer forms that match their energy.

**Narrative form** — what structural shape will the telling take? The vocabulary ranges from established patterns (braided, segmented, whorl, lyric, narrative lift, vertical descent) through hermit-crab forms (algorithm, field guide, recipe, proof, dialogue, literate program, exhibition catalog) to meta-forms (Borgesian review of the imaginary, speculative nonfiction, counterfactual). The form is not chosen from a menu; it emerges from the conversation as the writer discovers what their material demands.

**Audience and medium** — who receives this, and how? The range spans from written essay to conference talk to nerd nite to Story Collider to explanation-for-a-child to pitch-on-the-street. Each medium imposes constraints that function as creative catalysts. And the audience may not be known at the start — the material may disclose its audience through the act of composition.

**Epistemic posture** — does the writer know their argument? The range spans from determined (know the argument, need the best form) through exploratory (sense something, need writing to reveal it) to indeterminate (have elements and relationships, each realization discovers something new). The posture determines what the outline even is: a map, an invitation, or a score.

The framing conversation in the synthesis skill should navigate these four dimensions, not prescribe outcomes within them. But "navigate" is too passive a word. The conversation should involve externalized experimentation — trying forms on, drafting quick sketches in candidate structures, testing an audience constraint to see what it surfaces. The writer and agent do not discuss the dimensions in the abstract and then select. They create small structural experiments — a paragraph drafted as pseudocode, the opening rewritten for a five-year-old, the artifacts arranged as exhibition stops — and observe what each experiment reveals about the material. Some experiments will fail, and the failures are informative.

The outline that emerges is the exhibition design — a curated arrangement of artifacts from the trail, structured for a particular audience and medium, in a form suggested by the nature of the discovery, held with the appropriate degree of certainty. It is shaped not by deliberation alone but by the residue of structural experiments that showed the writer what their material actually demands.

## 8. What the Outline Contains

Regardless of which form, audience, medium, and posture emerge from the framing conversation, the outline retains certain properties:

**Pre-populated references.** The writer should not need to hunt for supporting material. Full quotes, attribution, and source context from the research trail are embedded in the outline at the points where they are relevant. This is curatorial work — placing the right artifact at the right point in the exhibition.

**The volta.** Every effective piece of nonfiction has a turn — the moment where the reader's understanding shifts. The outline identifies where this turn lives, even if (in the exploratory or indeterminate posture) the turn's exact nature is not yet known. In a determined outline, the volta is stated. In an exploratory outline, the volta is a question mark at a specific structural location. In an indeterminate outline, the volta is a set of candidate moments any of which could serve.

**Structural relationships.** Even in an indeterminate outline, elements are not a flat list. They have relationships: this resonates with that, this contradicts that, this provides scale context for that, this is the same concept seen from a different angle. These relationships are the transparent sheets of Cage's score — they can be repositioned, but they are not arbitrary.

**The argumentative backbone.** The argument audit verifies that claims follow from evidence and framing does not overreach. This backbone exists even in a lyric or hermit-crab form — the logic may be subterranean, but it must be sound. A poem can be logically valid; a recipe can be argumentatively coherent. The audit checks the logic, not the genre.

**The curatorial arrangement.** Layered over the argumentative backbone, the experiential structure — the sequence that creates wonder, surprise, scale shifts, and the shimmer. This is the dimension that makes the piece worth reading, hearing, or encountering. The argument makes it true; the curation makes it alive.

The outline is the exhibition design. The writer builds the exhibition.

## 9. Synthesis as Re-Entry

Essay 003 positions synthesis as a terminal phase — optional, post-build, the final epistemic act. The outline is the deliverable; the writer produces the essay outside the pipeline. This framing is correct as far as it goes, but it misses something that became visible only through the research for this essay: the act of finding the form is itself a mode of inquiry.

When a writer tries to tell the story of an RDD cycle as a recipe and discovers that the "ingredients" do not combine coherently, that incoherence is a finding. It reveals a gap in understanding that the build phase did not surface — perhaps because the build phase tests whether the software works, not whether the concepts that produced it hold together under a different structural logic. When a writer tries to explain the project to a child and cannot, that failure is diagnostic. When arranging the artifacts as an exhibition reveals that two ADRs contradict each other in a way no one noticed during the decision phase, that contradiction is a new research question.

Synthesis, then, is not always terminal. The framing conversation can produce an outline — and it can also produce questions that re-enter the RDD cycle. "We tried to tell this as a braided essay and discovered that the two strands don't actually illuminate each other — which means the connection we assumed between X and Y may not exist." That is a research finding, surfaced by structural experimentation, and it belongs back in the research log.

This creates a potential loop: RESEARCH → MODEL → DECIDE → BUILD → SYNTHESIS → RESEARCH. The loop is not mandatory. Most synthesis cycles will produce an outline and stop. But the option to re-enter — to treat formal experimentation as a discovery mechanism that feeds back into research — means synthesis is not just the end of the story. It is a way of asking whether the story is actually understood.

The devising tradition has a name for this. In ensemble-based theater, the work is never "finished" in the way that a manuscript is finished. Each performance is a realization of the score, and each realization reveals something new about the material. The synthesis outline, understood as a score rather than a script, participates in the same logic: each realization — each essay written, each talk given, each gallery walk constructed — is both a communicative act and a research act. The writer learns from the telling. And what the writer learns may change what there is to tell.
