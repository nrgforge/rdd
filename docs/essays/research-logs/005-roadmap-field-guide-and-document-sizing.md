# Research Log: Outline Structure for RDD Synthesis

## Question 1: What narrative structures are available for essay-length nonfiction built from research trails?
**Method:** Web search
**Findings:**

Seven established structural forms identified from writing craft literature (primarily Tim Bascom's "Picturing the Personal Essay" via *Creative Nonfiction*, and Miller & Paola's *Tell It Slant*):

1. **Narrative with a Lift** — chronological progression shaped by tension, building to a turning point. Suited to dramatic discoveries.
2. **Whorl of Reflection** — circling a subject from multiple angles (film, literature, experience, philosophy) without predetermined conclusion. Suited to complex topics needing multiple perspectives.
3. **Formal Limits of Focus** — strict temporal/spatial boundaries create coherence through disciplined exclusion. Everything outside the frame is chiseled away.
4. **Segmented Essay** — discrete sections separated by white space, unified by theme not chronology. Each segment is its own framed moment.
5. **Braided Structure** — two or more narrative strands weave together, each enriched by the others. One surfaces while another submerges. Suited to juxtaposing viewpoints or time periods.
6. **Lyric Essay** — prioritizes image, mood, and impression over logical argument. Compressed language creates visceral understanding. Fragments accumulate meaning.
7. **Dipping into the Well** — forward narrative interrupted by vertical descents into philosophical depth, then resurfacing. Suited to bridging personal experience with universal meaning.

**Implications:** These are not mutually exclusive — a lyric essay can be braided, a segmented essay can contain vertical descents. They're structural patterns that can combine. The current synthesis skill mentions "braided" but treats it as one option among unnamed others. This taxonomy gives the framing conversation a richer vocabulary.

## Question 2: What are the experimental/unconventional forms — including "wild" approaches?
**Method:** Web search
**Findings:**

**The Hermit Crab Essay** (coined by Brenda Miller & Suzanne Paola, *Tell It Slant*, 2003): An essay that inhabits the shell of a non-essay form. Examples: essay-as-recipe, essay-as-field-guide, essay-as-Craigslist-ad, essay-as-obituary, essay-as-prescription-side-effects. The borrowed form is not decoration — it *generates* content the writer wouldn't have found otherwise. "Allowing form to dictate content" helps writers "bypass what our intellectual minds have already determined as 'our story.'"

This is the formal name for the user's impulse to tell a story "in the form of an algorithm" or "as a robot's dream."

**Borgesian/Nabokovian Meta-Forms**: The fictional academic text — review of an imaginary book (Borges), scholarly commentary on a fictional poem (Nabokov's *Pale Fire*). The essay as artifact that comments on itself. Relevant because RDD synthesis already sits between academic and narrative registers.

**OuLiPo Constraint-Based Writing**: Constraints as creative catalysts. The principle: formal restriction unlocks discoveries that free writing misses. Raymond Queneau's *Exercises in Style* (one anecdote retold 99 ways) is a direct precedent for asking "what if we told this discovery through a radically different lens?"

**Speculative Nonfiction**: An emerging genre that incorporates imagination and wonder into factual writing — "exploring truths that might otherwise be hidden by strict adherence to settled fact."

**Literate Programming** (Knuth, 1984): Programs as literature. The flow of thought written as essay with embedded code. The program explains itself. Directly relevant for technical research trails.

**Algorithmic Narrativity**: Fiction and nonfiction structured as computation — loop-like repetitions, branching paths, recursive structures. Interactive storytelling that turns readers into participants.

**Implications:** The synthesis skill's framing conversation could offer a spectrum from conventional (braided, segmented) to radical (hermit crab, Borgesian, constraint-based). The key insight from Miller & Paola: the borrowed form isn't a gimmick — it generates content. When the synthesis conversation asks "how should we tell this?", a wild form might unlock more interesting writing than a safe one.

## Question 3: Does the type of discovery suggest which narrative form to use?
**Method:** Web search + synthesis of findings
**Findings:**

The writing pedagogy literature doesn't explicitly map discovery types to forms. But synthesizing across the research, a principled mapping emerges when cross-referenced with the synthesis skill's five novelty signals:

| Novelty Signal | Nature of Discovery | Structurally Suggestive Forms |
|---|---|---|
| Explicit surprise | "I was wrong about X" | Narrative with a Lift (the turn IS the story); volta-driven essay |
| Reframing (Dorst Abduction-2) | The problem definition itself changed | Hermit crab (the new frame IS the form); Whorl of Reflection (circling reveals the reframe) |
| Assumption denial (Davis) | Audience belief is challenged | Borgesian inversion; segmented essay (build the assumption, then dismantle) |
| Negative case integration | Counter-examples forced revision | Braided structure (the counter-example as its own strand, in tension with the main thread) |
| Superseded decisions | Earlier choices overturned | Segmented essay (each decision as a section, showing evolution); dialogue form (past self vs. present self) |

This mapping is speculative but principled — each novelty type has a structural affinity based on where the energy of the discovery lives.

The volta (essay's turn/pivot) is the structural expression of insight. The distance between narrator and moment creates room for the turn. Different types of insight create different kinds of turns, which suggest different structural shapes.

**Implications:** The synthesis skill could use this mapping not as prescription but as a *starting offer* in the framing conversation. "Your research trail shows a reframing — what if the essay itself enacted that reframe by inhabiting the new frame as its form?" This is more generative than "pick from: braided, story spine, ABT."

## Potential Wild Forms for RDD Synthesis (speculative, from research)

- **Essay-as-algorithm**: Pseudocode that tells a story. Each function is a section. Variables are concepts. The algorithm's output is the insight.
- **Essay-as-dream-of-a-machine**: Speculative nonfiction. The machine processes the research trail and hallucinates meaning. What does it see that we missed?
- **Essay-as-field-guide**: "A Field Guide to [Domain Concept]" — taxonomic, observational, illustrated with examples from the wild.
- **Essay-as-proof**: Axioms, lemmas, theorems. The proof structure mirrors the argument structure. QED is the insight.
- **Essay-as-dialogue**: Socratic method. Two voices — the researcher before and after. Or: the system and its user. Or: the code and its tests.
- **Essay-as-review-of-imaginary-system**: Borgesian. A review of software that doesn't exist, revealing what the research learned about what *should* exist.
- **Essay-as-literate-program**: Knuth-style. The essay IS the code, explained. The reader follows the thought, and the program emerges.
- **Essay-as-choose-your-own-adventure**: Branching paths. The reader's choices enact the decision space the research explored.
- **Essay-as-recipe**: Ingredients are concepts. Steps are the methodology. "Serves: one working system."
- **Essay-as-autopsy-report**: What killed the previous approach? Clinical, forensic, revelatory.

## Question 4: What happens when audience and medium become form-shaping forces?
**Method:** Web search
**Findings:**

The previous questions assumed a written essay as the output. But the synthesis output could be a talk, a performance, an explanation. This introduces two new dimensions: **who receives it** and **how they receive it**.

### Presentation Formats as Structural Constraints

Each format imposes constraints that function like OuLiPo rules — they force creative compression:

| Format | Constraints | What It Forces |
|---|---|---|
| **Conference talk** (20-45 min) | Slides, stage, expert audience | Duarte's sparkline: oscillate between "what is" and "what could be." Audience is the hero; speaker is the mentor. |
| **Lightning / Ignite** (5 min) | 20 slides × 15 seconds, auto-advance | One idea, one compelling illustration. Extreme distillation reveals whether you actually have a point. |
| **PechaKucha** (6:40) | 20 slides × 20 seconds | Visual storytelling. Words serve images, not vice versa. |
| **Nerd Nite** (15-20 min) | Bar, beer, humor, "expert or over-informed" | Informal authority. You can go deep but must stay entertaining. Three speakers per night means audience compares you. |
| **Story Collider** (10 min) | No slides, true personal story, spoken word | The science is the setting, not the subject. The story is about *you* and science, not science alone. Coached one-on-one. |
| **Open mic / spoken word** | Performance, rhythm, audience energy | The body and voice carry meaning. Repetition, cadence, surprise. Written for the ear. |
| **Explain to a child** | Simple words, no jargon, concrete examples | Feynman principle: if you can't explain it simply, you don't understand it. Munroe's *Thing Explainer*: 1,000 most common words only. The constraint generates new understanding of *your own material*. |
| **Workplace presentation** | Stakeholder context, "so what" for the business | Action-oriented. Every insight must connect to a decision someone needs to make. |
| **Random people on the street** | 30 seconds, no context, no assumed interest | Hook in the first sentence or lose them. What's the one thing that would make a stranger stop walking? |

### Key Insight: Audience Constraint as Creative Catalyst

The audience constraint works exactly like the hermit crab's borrowed shell — it generates content the writer wouldn't have found otherwise. "Explain your domain model to a five-year-old" might reveal something about the model that the formal essay wouldn't. "Give this as a Story Collider piece" forces the writer to find the personal story inside the technical work.

This means the synthesis framing conversation has a powerful new move: **"Who would you want to tell this to, and how?"** The answer shapes the outline as much as the discovery type does.

### The Hermit Crab Extends to Presentations

The hermit crab concept isn't limited to writing. A talk can inhabit a borrowed form too:
- A conference talk structured as a cooking show
- A nerd nite presentation delivered as a nature documentary narration
- A workplace presentation framed as a courtroom trial
- A lightning talk as a magic trick (setup, misdirection, reveal)

### Three Dimensions That Shape the Outline

Synthesizing across all four questions, the outline emerges from the intersection of:

1. **Discovery type** — what kind of insight emerged (surprise, reframe, assumption denial, etc.)
2. **Narrative form** — the structural shape (braided, hermit crab, segmented, volta-driven, etc.)
3. **Audience × Medium** — who receives it and how (essay, talk, performance, explanation)

These aren't independent — they interact. A reframing discovery told to a child might suggest a completely different form than the same reframing told at a conference. The synthesis framing conversation should explore all three dimensions, not just form.

**Implications:** The synthesis skill currently assumes the output is a written essay stored at `./docs/synthesis/NNN-name-outline.md`. The output could instead be:
- A talk outline with speaker notes, timing marks, and slide concepts
- A performance script with stage directions
- A structured explanation with audience-specific analogies
- A written essay (the current default)

The outline format should adapt to the chosen medium while retaining the core properties: pre-populated references, argument-audited structure, and a clear volta.

## Question 5: What about writing without a known audience, indeterminacy, and temporal displacement?
**Method:** Web search
**Findings:**

Three threads emerged that share a common posture: **not knowing the destination when you start.**

### The Essay as Attempt (Montaigne)

The word "essay" comes from the French *essayer* — to attempt, to test, to try. Montaigne didn't use it to name a genre; he used it to describe a *posture*. "What I write here is not my teaching, but my study; it is not a lesson for others, but for me." He sought "a sinuous language which could digress, circle back, move forward and make unexpected discoveries, where manner and matter were indivisible."

Montaigne's essays are "severed at the moment he senses he is approaching a conclusion" — the process is the product. "I have no more made my book than my book has made me."

**Implication for synthesis:** Sometimes the writer doesn't know the audience because the writing itself is the exploration. The audience reveals itself *through* the act of writing. This is fundamentally different from the audience-first communication design of Questions 1-4. The outline for this posture isn't a map to a destination — it's a structured invitation to wander.

### Indeterminacy (John Cage)

Cage defined indeterminacy as "the ability of a piece to be performed in substantially different ways." His key move: the score provides a *framework* — measurable parameters, structural relationships — but the realization is different each time. In works like *Variations I* and *II*, transparent plastic sheets with graphical notation can be repositioned independently, creating specifications for unique instantiations.

Cage's indeterminacy exists in two states: uncertainty of composition (the writing) and uncertainty of performance (the reading/delivery). His scores are not scripts. They are structured spaces for possibility.

**Implication for synthesis:** What would an indeterminate outline look like? Not "Section 1: Introduction, Section 2: Background" but a set of elements (themes, references, turning points, images) with structural relationships but no fixed sequence. The writer — or speaker — realizes the outline differently each time. The outline is a *score*, not a *script*.

This could mean: the same outline produces a written essay one week and a nerd nite talk the next, depending on which elements the writer activates and in what order.

### Time Travel and Counterfactual as Narrative Technique

Time travel narratives are "exercises in narratology and the theorization of temporality." The deliberate anachronism — placing present understanding into the past, or past assumptions into the present — is a nonfiction technique, not just a fiction device.

Counterfactual history seeks "by conjecturing on what did not happen, or what might have happened, in order to understand what did happen." The "What If?" essays (Robert Cowley, ed.) present historians exploring how slight turns of fate at decisive moments could have changed everything.

**Implication for synthesis:** RDD trails are rich with superseded decisions, abandoned spikes, and evolved understanding. "What if we'd made the other choice at ADR-003?" isn't idle speculation — it reveals why the actual choice matters. The time-travel essay structure: visit the decision point, take the road not traveled, show what happens, return to the present with new understanding of why the path taken was (or wasn't) the right one.

This connects to the braided structure — one strand is the actual timeline, the other is the counterfactual. They illuminate each other.

### What These Three Share

All three — Montaigne, Cage, counterfactual — reject the assumption that the writer knows where they're going before they start. They propose:

- **The outline as attempt** (Montaigne): structured wandering toward an unknown conclusion
- **The outline as score** (Cage): a set of elements with relationships but no fixed realization
- **The outline as time machine** (counterfactual): visit the past to understand the present

This challenges the synthesis skill's current assumption that the outline is produced *after* the framing conversation resolves a clear central question. Sometimes the central question isn't clear. Sometimes the outline is itself the method for finding it.

### A Fourth Dimension?

The three dimensions from Question 4 may need a fourth:

1. **Discovery type** — what kind of insight emerged
2. **Narrative form** — the structural shape
3. **Audience × Medium** — who receives it and how
4. **Epistemic posture** — does the writer know what they're saying, or is the writing the process of finding out?

The posture dimension ranges from:
- **Determined** — "I know my argument, now I need the best form to deliver it" (most conference talks, workplace presentations)
- **Exploratory** — "I sense something here but the writing will reveal what it is" (Montaigne, lyric essay)
- **Indeterminate** — "I have elements and relationships; each realization discovers something new" (Cage, open score)

## Question 6: What does curation add — specifically the Museum of Jurassic Technology sensibility?
**Method:** Web search
**Findings:**

### The Museum of Jurassic Technology

Founded by David Hildebrand Wilson in 1988 in Los Angeles. Wilson received a MacArthur Fellowship in 2001. Lawrence Weschler wrote *Mr. Wilson's Cabinet of Wonder* about it — a Pulitzer finalist.

The museum's method: deliberate juxtaposition of verifiable scientific artifacts with fabricated histories and impossible inventions. The visitor "continually finds himself shimmering between wondering at the marvels of nature and wondering whether any of this could possibly be true." Weschler argues this **shimmer** — the capacity for delicious confusion — "may constitute the most blessedly wonderful thing about being human."

Wilson: "There is a truth that exists that is beyond a three-dimensional truth, a more complex truth than verifiability. Ecstatic truth is the truth of the imagination."

The museum operates as a "museum about museums" — it IS a hermit crab. It inhabits the shell of a legitimate museum to question what legitimacy means.

### The Wunderkammer (Cabinet of Curiosities)

The museum's ancestor: Renaissance wonder-cabinets where collectors assembled objects that bore scientific or historical merit alongside fabrications. The juxtaposition of disparate objects "encouraged comparisons, finding analogies and parallels." The arrangement created "abrupt transitions of gaze from one object to another, constantly changing the scale of perception for an arresting effect."

Key properties of the wunderkammer:
- **Personal** — the collection reflects the collector's identity, not a taxonomy
- **Juxtaposition generates meaning** — objects don't explain themselves; meaning emerges from proximity
- **Scale shifts** — moving from the cosmic to the microscopic and back
- **Wonder as epistemology** — the emotional response (wonder, doubt, delight) IS the learning

### Curation as Authorship

By the 1980s, curated exhibitions came to be understood as "the creative work of curator-auteurs" (after Harald Szeemann's term *ausstellungsmacher*). The curator doesn't create the objects — they create meaning through selection, arrangement, juxtaposition, sequence, and the spaces between.

This is exactly what synthesis does with the research trail. The essays, ADRs, spikes, reflections — these are the objects. The synthesis skill is the curator. The outline is the exhibition design.

### What Curation Adds to the Model

**Outline as exhibition.** The curator-writer selects artifacts from the research trail, arranges them for a visitor-reader's journey, and creates meaning through juxtaposition and pacing. Unlike a linear argument, the exhibition allows:

- **Non-linear traversal** — the visitor chooses their path (connects to Cage's indeterminacy)
- **Scale shifts** — zooming from a single code decision to a philosophical implication and back
- **The shimmer** — holding wonder and doubt simultaneously as a productive state
- **Negative space** — what you leave out creates meaning as much as what you include
- **The personal voice of the collector** — why *these* objects? Why *this* arrangement? The curation reveals the curator

**The shimmer as epistemic posture.** This refines the fourth dimension from Question 5. The Museum of Jurassic Technology doesn't just tolerate uncertainty — it *cultivates* it. The shimmer is between fact and fabrication, between "this is true" and "could this possibly be true?" In synthesis terms: the essay doesn't need to resolve every tension. Sometimes the most honest move is to present artifacts in juxtaposition and let the reader shimmer.

**Curation vs. argument.** The argument audit checks logical soundness. But curation operates by a different logic — associative, resonant, spatial. An exhibition that "works" isn't necessarily one where every claim follows from the previous one. It's one where the juxtapositions create a coherent emotional and intellectual experience.

This suggests the synthesis outline could have two registers:
1. **Argumentative backbone** — the logical structure that the argument audit verifies
2. **Curatorial arrangement** — the experiential structure that creates wonder, surprise, and the shimmer

The outline captures both: the argument (what must be true) and the exhibition (how it feels to encounter).

### Wild Forms Expanded

Adding to the list from Question 2:
- **Outline-as-exhibition-catalog**: Each section is an "exhibit" with a wall label. The catalog essay provides the through-line.
- **Outline-as-wunderkammer**: Artifacts from the research trail arranged by resonance, not chronology or logic. Scale shifts between macro and micro.
- **Outline-as-museum-audio-guide**: "Stop 1: Approach this decision from the left..." Guided but personal, with optional detours.
- **Outline-as-gallery-walk**: A presentation format where stations in a room each hold a different artifact. The audience physically moves. (This is a real presentation technique.)
