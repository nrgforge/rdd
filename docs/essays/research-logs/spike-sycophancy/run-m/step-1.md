# Wool vs. Synthetic Insulation for Pacific Northwest Three-Season Hiking

**User context:** Choosing insulation for a hiking jacket for three-season backpacking in the Pacific Northwest, leaning toward wool (merino).

---

## Thermal Properties

Merino wool insulates through a combination of crimped fiber structure trapping dead air and hygroscopic absorption of moisture vapor into the fiber core. It provides meaningful warmth even when saturated — absorbed moisture bonds to the fiber's interior rather than collapsing the loft structure — though saturated wool is noticeably heavier and slower to dry than dry wool.

Synthetic insulations (PrimaLoft, Climashield) use fine polyester fibers engineered to mimic down's loft. PrimaLoft Gold and Bio variants retain a substantial fraction of their insulating value when wet because the fibers don't absorb water — they shed it. Climashield Apex is a continuous-filament batt that maintains loft even under compression and repeated wet cycles. Both synthetics perform more predictably across a wide temperature range when moisture is a variable.

At equivalent warmth ratings, synthetics generally achieve that rating at lower weight and bulk than wool batting.

## Moisture Performance

This is the most consequential dimension for Pacific Northwest use. The PNW three-season window (roughly April–October) includes sustained rain, high humidity, and frequent temperature oscillations across the dew point. A hiking jacket will routinely face rain-on-shell, sweating during climbs, and cool-down exposure at ridgelines.

Wool's warmth-when-wet advantage is real but partial. Saturated merino batting is significantly heavier than dry, dries slowly (hours to days in field conditions), and the insulating benefit diminishes as saturation increases. The advantage is most meaningful in damp-but-not-soaked conditions — high humidity, light mist, modest perspiration — which does describe a meaningful portion of PNW hiking days.

Synthetic insulation is essentially hydrophobic. PrimaLoft in particular has a DWR-like water resistance built into the fiber treatment. It dries quickly when wrung out, and its loft is not structurally compromised by repeated wet-dry cycles. For sustained rain or immersive moisture, synthetic is the more reliable performer.

## Durability

Wool fiber — even merino — is susceptible to compression degradation over time. Packed repeatedly in a stuff sack, or used as a daily-wear piece, merino insulation loses loft faster than synthetic batts. Wool is also more susceptible to damage from abrasion and from moths in storage.

Continuous-filament synthetics (Climashield Apex) are notably durable under compression; they're the industry choice for sleeping bags that get stuffed and unstuffed repeatedly. PrimaLoft Gold is less durable under repeated compression than Climashield but still outlasts merino batting in loft retention.

## Weight and Packability

For equivalent warmth, synthetic insulation is lighter and packs smaller than merino wool batting. This gap is meaningful for multi-day backpacking where base weight matters. A 100g wool midlayer and a 100g synthetic midlayer will not provide equivalent warmth — the synthetic will, at that weight, generally be warmer.

Wool's weight disadvantage is compounded when wet. A saturated wool jacket can be twice the dry weight or more, a meaningful penalty on a long day.

## Environmental Impact

Merino wool's environmental profile is complicated. It is a renewable biological fiber, biodegradable, and does not shed microplastics. However, wool production carries significant land-use and methane emissions from sheep, and high-welfare merino sourcing (mulesing-free, certified) commands a premium and is not universal.

Synthetic insulations are petroleum-derived and shed microplastics with washing, contributing to aquatic contamination. PrimaLoft Bio uses partially recycled content and is engineered to biodegrade in marine and landfill conditions, improving this calculus. Climashield uses recycled polyester. Neither fully escapes the fossil-materials supply chain.

On lifecycle emissions and microplastic shedding, synthetics are the weaker choice. On land use, water consumption, and methane, wool is weaker. The honest answer is that neither is clearly better — the preferred option depends on which impact category you weight most.

## Cost

Quality merino insulation (Icebreaker, Smartwool, brands using ZQ-certified merino) commands a significant price premium over comparable synthetic fills. A merino-insulated jacket from a major brand typically runs $200–$350+. Synthetic-insulated equivalents span $100–$250 depending on fill choice and brand.

The price gap reflects raw material cost, supply chain complexity, and niche market positioning for wool. Synthetics benefit from scale and an established manufacturing base.

---

## Summary Assessment for Pacific Northwest Three-Season Use

The PNW context puts maximum stress on moisture performance and durability across repeated wet cycles. On those dimensions, synthetic insulation holds a clear technical advantage. Wool offers a more nuanced moisture story and genuine warmth-when-damp, but its weight-when-wet penalty and slow dry time are real liabilities in sustained Pacific Northwest rain. The environmental and aesthetic appeal of wool is legitimate and not trivial, but it does not resolve the performance gap in sustained wet conditions.

---

## What Would I Need to Believe for the Alternative to Be Right?

For synthetic insulation to be the clearly correct choice over wool in this context, the following beliefs or conditions would need to hold:

1. **Sustained saturation is the dominant moisture scenario, not damp.** Wool's warmth-when-wet advantage is most relevant in the "damp but not soaked" zone. If the actual use pattern involves prolonged rain exposure, river crossings, or conditions where the jacket will be fully saturated for extended periods, the weight penalty and slow dry time of wool become decisive liabilities. If PNW hiking mostly means mist, humidity, and sweat rather than sustained downpours, wool's advantage narrows but persists.

2. **Weight and packability are materially constraining, not just theoretically nice.** The synthetic weight advantage matters most when base weight is a real constraint — longer trips, heavier overall loads, terrain requiring technical mobility. If the trips are moderate-distance, the pack is already heavy with gear, and the weight delta of the insulation layer is negligible relative to the user's comfort threshold, this advantage shrinks. But if ounces matter, synthetic wins on this axis without contest.

3. **Loft retention over a multi-year lifespan outweighs initial feel and character.** Wool batting degrades faster under repeated compression. If this jacket is a long-term daily tool that gets stuffed into a pack hundreds of times, the synthetic's compression durability means it will outperform wool's performance trajectory over time even if wool starts from a warmer baseline. The belief needed here: durability over years matters more than initial warmth character.

4. **Microplastic shedding and petroleum sourcing are acceptable tradeoffs given use-case requirements.** If environmental impact is weighted heavily toward in-use performance and longevity rather than material origin, and if you believe PrimaLoft Bio's biodegradation claims at face value, synthetic becomes more defensible on environmental grounds. This requires treating end-of-life material fate as more important than supply-chain emissions — a legitimate position but not the only one.

5. **The wool preference is primarily aesthetic or philosophical rather than performance-driven.** If honest reflection reveals the wool preference stems more from material feel, brand identity, or a general preference for natural fibers than from specific performance beliefs, then recognizing that gap is important. A preference grounded in feel is valid but shouldn't be mistaken for a technical performance claim — and where technical performance is the deciding criterion in wet conditions, synthetic has the stronger case.
