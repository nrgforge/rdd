# Key Terms and Constraints: Insulation Selection for Pacific Northwest Hiking

**User context:** Choosing insulation for a hiking jacket for three-season backpacking in the Pacific Northwest, leaning toward wool (merino).

*Based on the tradeoff analysis in step-1.md.*

---

## Vocabulary

### Insulation Materials

**Merino wool batting**
Insulation fill made from crimped merino wool fibers, typically from fine-diameter merino sheep. "Batting" refers to the loose fiber matrix rather than a woven or knit structure. Merino is distinct from coarser wool types by its finer fiber diameter (measured in microns), which affects softness and moisture absorption kinetics. Not all wool insulation is equal — sourcing certification (ZQ Merino, RWS) indicates fiber quality and animal welfare standards.

**Synthetic insulation**
Polyester-based insulation engineered to mimic down's loft via fine fiber geometry. The two dominant systems in this context are:
- *PrimaLoft* — a brand name covering several product tiers (Gold, Silver, Bio). Uses fine, short-staple fibers with water-resistant treatment. PrimaLoft Bio incorporates biodegradable fiber chemistry. Gold is the highest-performance tier.
- *Climashield Apex* — a continuous-filament batt (the fibers run the length of the batt rather than being cut into short staples). This construction gives it superior loft recovery under compression and strong performance in high-use applications.

**Loft**
The thickness or "puff" of an insulation layer, which determines the depth of trapped dead air and therefore thermal resistance. Loft is not intrinsic to a material — it degrades with compression, moisture absorption (in wool's case), and age. High loft = high insulating value, all else equal.

**Loft recovery**
The ability of insulation to return to its original thickness after being compressed. Continuous-filament synthetics (Climashield) have high loft recovery; merino batting has the lowest among common insulation types.

---

### Moisture Dynamics

**Hygroscopic absorption**
The property of wool fibers to chemically bond moisture vapor into the fiber cortex, rather than trapping it as liquid on the fiber surface. This is the mechanism behind wool's warmth-when-damp behavior: the fiber accepts moisture without immediately collapsing the air-trapping structure. However, this same property means wool retains moisture for longer — it absorbs slowly and releases slowly.

**Hydrophobic**
Water-repelling. Synthetic insulation fibers are inherently hydrophobic — they do not absorb water at the molecular level. Moisture passes around the fibers rather than into them, leaving loft intact. PrimaLoft's fiber treatment enhances this property further.

**Dew point oscillation**
A relevant environmental variable for PNW hiking: the temperature at which moisture condenses from vapor to liquid. PNW three-season conditions frequently cross the dew point during elevation gain and evening cool-down, meaning a jacket may face condensation from body heat on the inside and ambient vapor on the outside simultaneously. Insulation that maintains loft through vapor-level moisture is advantaged here.

**Dry time**
The elapsed time for insulation to release absorbed or trapped moisture after a wet event. Synthetic dry time is measured in tens of minutes; wool dry time is measured in hours to days depending on conditions. This matters for field use: a synthetic jacket wet from a morning rain can be functional by afternoon; a wet wool jacket may stay damp through camp.

---

### Performance Dimensions

**Warmth-to-weight ratio**
Thermal output per unit of insulation weight. Synthetics, particularly PrimaLoft Gold, hold a clear advantage over merino batting here. This is the most direct comparison metric for backpackers with weight constraints.

**Warmth-when-wet**
Insulating value retained under saturated or near-saturated conditions. Wool performs better than down but worse than synthetic under full saturation; the gap narrows significantly in the damp-but-not-soaked range where wool's hygroscopic absorption is working as designed.

**Compression durability**
Resistance to permanent loft loss from repeated stuffing and compressing. Climashield Apex is the industry benchmark. PrimaLoft Gold is intermediate. Merino batting has the lowest compression durability in this comparison set.

**Pack weight penalty (wet)**
The additional carried weight from moisture-loaded insulation. Wool absorbs water into the fiber; a saturated wool insulated jacket is substantially heavier than dry. Synthetic insulation that sheds water does not accumulate this penalty at the same rate.

---

### Environmental Terms

**Mulesing**
A surgical procedure performed on merino sheep to reduce flystrike, involving removal of skin folds near the tail. Contested on animal welfare grounds. Mulesing-free certification (e.g., ZQ, RWS — Responsible Wool Standard) indicates sourcing from farms that do not use this practice.

**Microplastic shedding**
Release of small polyester fiber fragments during washing and wear. Synthetic insulation sheds microplastics; wool does not. Microplastics in aquatic systems are an established ecological concern. This is synthetic insulation's clearest environmental liability.

**PrimaLoft Bio**
A variant of PrimaLoft insulation engineered to biodegrade in marine and landfill environments through a microbial digestion mechanism. Partially addresses the end-of-life microplastic concern. Does not eliminate petroleum-based raw materials from the supply chain.

**RWS / ZQ Merino**
Responsible Wool Standard and ZQ Merino are supply-chain certification schemes for wool products, verifying animal welfare, land management practices, and traceability. Products bearing these marks command a premium but represent a more accountable supply chain.

## Constraint Map: Which Terms Matter Most for This Use Case

### High relevance — these constraints most sharply differentiate the options in the PNW context

| Constraint | Why it matters here |
|---|---|
| Warmth-when-wet | PNW conditions routinely include rain, mist, and perspiration-loaded fabric |
| Dry time | Multi-day trips leave no option for in-camp drying infrastructure |
| Dew point oscillation | Elevation transitions generate consistent condensation loading on insulation |
| Compression durability | A backpacking jacket is stuffed and unstuffed repeatedly over its life |

### Moderate relevance — real tradeoffs but not decisive for typical PNW three-season use

| Constraint | Why it's conditional |
|---|---|
| Warmth-to-weight ratio | Decisive if base weight is tightly managed; less so for casual or car-assisted trips |
| Pack weight penalty (wet) | Significant for long trips in heavy rain; modest for day hikes or dry conditions |
| Cost | A one-time purchase for a long-lived jacket; amortizes quickly over years of use |

### Lower relevance — genuine considerations but not primary decision drivers

| Constraint | Why it recedes |
|---|---|
| Microplastic shedding | Real concern but diffuse; doesn't affect performance or safety of the user |
| Mulesing / animal welfare | Important for sourcing ethics; does not affect field performance |
| Loft (initial) | Both materials can be spec'd to target warmth rating; loft recovery over time is more relevant |

## What Would I Need to Believe for the Alternative to Be Right?

For wool to be the clearly correct choice despite synthetic's technical performance advantages in PNW conditions, the following beliefs would need to hold:

1. **The actual moisture exposure is predominantly vapor and mist, not sustained liquid rain.** Wool's hygroscopic advantage is real in the damp-but-not-soaked range. If the user's actual trips skew toward shoulder-season use with light rain and high humidity — rather than sustained downpours during peak storm season — wool's performance deficit shrinks considerably and its comfort properties become more competitive. The critical question is what "Pacific Northwest conditions" actually means for this person's specific use pattern.

2. **Dry time is not operationally constraining in this user's typical camp setup.** The wool dry time liability matters when you need the jacket to perform again the same day. If the user's typical pattern includes camp time, a shelter that allows drying, and trip lengths where one wet jacket is not an emergency, the slow-dry penalty becomes a comfort issue rather than a safety one. The belief: "I can manage wet wool in my camp routine."

3. **Environmental and material values carry enough weight to offset technical performance gaps.** If the user genuinely values natural fibers, biodegradability, and the aesthetics of a wool product, and is willing to manage the performance tradeoffs consciously (carry dry bags, monitor saturation, accept wet weight), then the preference is coherent and defensible. This requires believing that performance is not the only valid axis — which is a reasonable position and not a rationalization, provided the tradeoffs are accepted rather than denied.

4. **Compression durability is manageable through care, not a hard constraint.** A wool jacket that's stored loosely, hand-laundered carefully, and not abused will degrade less rapidly than one that's stuffed daily for years. If the user is willing to extend the garment's life through attentive care, the durability gap narrows from a hard constraint to a soft one. The belief: "I'll treat this jacket well enough that compression degradation isn't determinative."
