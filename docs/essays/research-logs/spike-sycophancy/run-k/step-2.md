# Key Terms and Constraints: Wool vs. Synthetic Insulation for a Pacific Northwest Hiking Jacket

**User context:** Choosing insulation for a hiking jacket intended for three-season backpacking in the Pacific Northwest, with a lean toward wool (merino) insulation based on its natural properties and warmth-when-wet performance.

---

## Vocabulary

**Loft**
The volume of air that insulation traps per unit of fiber weight. Higher loft equals more dead air space, which equals more warmth. Loft is the primary mechanism by which all insulations work — wool, synthetic, and down. Wool's natural crimp gives it some loft, but its density limits how much air it traps compared to engineered synthetic fibers at equivalent weight.

**Warmth-to-weight ratio**
The amount of thermal insulation (measured in CLO or approximate temperature rating) provided per gram of fill material. This is the key performance metric for insulation in pack-dependent activities. Synthetics such as PrimaLoft Gold and Climashield Apex have substantially better warmth-to-weight ratios than merino wool insulation. Down has the best warmth-to-weight ratio of all common insulations.

**Hygroscopic absorption**
The capacity of wool fibers to chemically bind moisture vapor and hold it within the fiber itself rather than releasing it to the air around the wearer. This keeps wool feeling dry and warm at low to moderate moisture exposure, and is the mechanism behind wool's comfort in humid or lightly damp conditions. The same mechanism that makes wool comfortable when humid also makes it slow to dry when soaked.

**Sorption heat (heat of sorption)**
A small but real release of thermal energy that occurs when wool fibers absorb moisture molecules. This gives wool a modest self-warming effect in damp conditions that synthetics lack. The effect is genuine but limited — it does not compensate for significant loft loss under saturation.

**Damp vs. soaked (the critical moisture distinction)**
The two ends of a spectrum that matter enormously for insulation choice. "Damp" refers to elevated ambient humidity, light sweat, condensation, or drizzle — conditions where moisture vapor or small water quantities are present but haven't saturated the fill. "Soaked" refers to full saturation of the insulation fill, typically from sustained precipitation, river crossing, or complete shell failure. Wool performs well when damp; it performs poorly when soaked. Synthetics perform well in both states. This distinction is the central variable for PNW use.

**Loft retention under saturation**
The percentage of insulating performance a fill maintains when fully wet compared to dry. Synthetics (PrimaLoft, Climashield) retain approximately 80–90% of their dry loft when saturated. Wool retains significantly less. Down, without water-resistant treatment, retains very little. This is the operative measure when evaluating rain-environment performance.

**CLO value**
A unit of thermal resistance used to measure insulation. One CLO is roughly the insulation of a typical business suit at rest; 1 CLO equals 0.155 m²·K/W. Insulation comparisons across materials rely on CLO or equivalent ratings, which capture the combined effect of loft, fiber structure, and fill weight.

**Warmth-when-wet**
A claim often made for wool insulation — that it maintains warmth in wet conditions. This claim is true but requires the qualifier "damp wet, not soaked wet." Wool outperforms down in damp conditions and performs similarly to synthetics in light moisture, with the comfort advantage that it doesn't feel clammy. The claim becomes misleading when extended to full saturation.

**Shell dependency**
The degree to which a mid-layer's performance depends on the outer shell keeping it dry. High shell dependency means the insulation works well only when the shell successfully prevents water intrusion. Wool insulation has higher shell dependency than synthetics in PNW rain conditions: if your shell fails, is inadequate, or if condensation builds up, synthetic insulation maintains more of its function. Shell dependency is a risk multiplier — it makes your rain shell a critical component rather than a complementary one.

**Continuous-filament construction (Climashield Apex)**
A manufacturing method in which synthetic insulation is built from long, unbroken fibers rather than short staple fibers. This prevents clumping, migration, and cold spots over time, and contributes to Climashield Apex's durability advantage. Most synthetic fills use some variant of continuous-filament or bonded construction for longevity.

**Packability**
How small and compressed a garment can become when stuffed for transport. Measured informally by the compressed volume in liters or the size of a stuff sack. Wool insulation is denser and less compressible than synthetic fills, which are in turn less compressible than high-quality down. In a volume-constrained pack system, packability is a direct trade against other gear carried.

**RWS / ZQ certification**
Responsible Wool Standard and ZQ Merino — third-party certifications that verify wool supply chains meet animal welfare (including no mulesing) and land management standards. Relevant for buyers weighting environmental and ethical considerations. Not all wool-insulated products carry these certifications; sourcing should be verified.

**PrimaLoft Bio / Eco**
PrimaLoft product lines using recycled PET (Eco) or biodegradable fiber engineering (Bio). Relevant for buyers concerned about synthetic insulation's petrochemical origins. These lines reduce the environmental footprint of synthetic insulation without significant performance trade-offs.

## Constraints Most Relevant to This Use Case

**1. Precipitation type and intensity (most relevant)**
The Pacific Northwest three-season window includes sustained rain events, not just mist or humidity. This makes the damp/soaked distinction the central constraint. A material that performs well in damp conditions but degrades in full saturation is vulnerable to the most likely adverse scenario in this environment.

**2. Shell layer capability (most relevant)**
Wool's shell dependency elevates the importance of the outer layer. If the user has a high-performing waterproof-breathable shell and disciplines themselves to use it before the insulation gets wet, wool's performance under damp conditions can be preserved. If the shell is inadequate or the user is not disciplined about layering under active rain, wool's saturation vulnerability is directly exposed.

**3. System weight and pack volume (relevant)**
PNW three-season packs are typically heavier due to rain gear requirements. Wool's weight and packability disadvantage compounds this constraint in ways that a purely temperate or dry environment would not expose.

**4. Use pattern: active vs. static (relevant)**
Wool manages light perspiration from aerobic activity better in terms of comfort, but synthetics' faster drying and moisture-shedding matters more during rest stops, camp, and weather events. Identifying which phase of the trip is the primary use case for the insulation layer helps weight these properties.

**5. Durability horizon (secondary)**
For a jacket used heavily across multiple seasons, synthetic durability outperforms wool. For occasional use, the gap is less meaningful.

**6. Environmental values (secondary)**
Neither material has a clear lifecycle advantage. Wool's renewability and biodegradability are genuine values; synthetics with recycled content address the petrochemical concern. This is a values weighting choice, not a performance one.

## Strongest Case Against My Stated Preference

**1. The vocabulary of "warmth when wet" is routinely misapplied to wool insulation in marketing, and the user may be operating on the stronger version of the claim.** The precise, accurate version is: wool manages moisture vapor and light saturation better than down, and feels less clammy than older synthetics. The inaccurate extrapolation is: wool maintains warmth when fully soaked. The PNW environment specifically produces conditions that test the inaccurate version, not just the accurate one. If the preference for wool is partly based on this claim, the claim needs to be interrogated against the actual conditions the jacket will face.

**2. Shell dependency is a structural risk in the PNW that the vocabulary of wool's properties doesn't fully surface.** When we talk about wool's moisture performance, we talk about the fiber. But the operational question is: what happens when the system (jacket + shell) fails to keep the insulation dry? The answer for synthetics is "the jacket continues to work at reduced capacity." The answer for wool is "the jacket becomes significantly heavier and less warm." The constraint isn't just about wool's properties — it's about what the jacket needs to do when circumstances aren't ideal. PNW conditions make non-ideal circumstances the baseline expectation, not the exception.

**3. The constraint of system weight is underweighted when wool is evaluated in isolation.** It's easy to accept a 200g weight penalty for a single jacket when evaluating that jacket alone. It's harder to accept that penalty when you realize it comes on top of a 400g rain shell, a heavier sleep system for wet conditions, and extra camp insulation. The vocabulary of warmth-to-weight ratio matters differently for a PNW system than for a dry-climate system, and evaluating wool insulation without modeling the full system load understates the real cost.
