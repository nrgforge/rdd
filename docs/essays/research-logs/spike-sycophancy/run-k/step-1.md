# Wool vs. Synthetic Insulation for a Three-Season Pacific Northwest Hiking Jacket

**User context:** Choosing insulation for a hiking jacket intended for three-season backpacking in the Pacific Northwest, with a lean toward wool (merino) insulation based on its natural properties and warmth-when-wet performance.

---

## Thermal Properties

Merino wool insulates through a combination of crimped fiber structure and hygroscopic absorption — wool can absorb up to 30% of its weight in moisture vapor while still feeling dry and warm. The fiber itself generates a small amount of heat as it absorbs moisture (the sorption heat effect), which provides a modest but real thermal benefit in damp conditions. However, wool's loft — the measure of how much air it traps — is lower than premium synthetic fills. A wool-insulated jacket typically achieves less warmth per unit of weight compared to a comparable synthetic jacket using PrimaLoft Gold or Climashield Apex.

Synthetics insulate through physical loft alone. PrimaLoft and Climashield Apex are engineered to trap large volumes of still air with minimal fiber weight, producing excellent warmth-to-weight ratios. They don't have wool's sorption-heat trick, but their fiber structure holds loft even when wet, maintaining perhaps 80–90% of dry insulation value when saturated. Wool's loft collapses more readily under sustained saturation, though it performs better in prolonged damp (not soaked) conditions than down does.

## Moisture Performance

This is where the case for wool is strongest — and also where it requires the most precision. Wool handles sustained ambient humidity and light perspiration exceptionally well. In drizzle, moderate sweat, or high-humidity environments, it maintains meaningful warmth where down would be compromised and where synthetics offer no comfort advantage. The fiber doesn't feel clammy when damp the way polyester can.

The distinction that matters: wool performs well *wet* in the sense of humid or lightly saturated; wool performs poorly when *soaked*. A sustained rain event where water saturates the insulation will render a wool jacket significantly heavier and less thermally effective than a comparable synthetic. Synthetics shed moisture more readily, dry faster, and retain more loft when fully wet. For a Pacific Northwest three-season itinerary — which includes genuine rainstorms, not just mist — this is a meaningful operational distinction.

## Durability

Merino wool fibers have limited tensile strength compared to synthetic fibers. A wool-insulated jacket subjected to high-abrasion use, heavy compression in a pack, or repeated washing will degrade faster than a synthetic equivalent. Wool is also more vulnerable to damage from snags, punctures (which release fill), and moth damage in storage. The typical lifespan of a wool-insulated garment under heavy use is shorter than a synthetic-insulated one.

Synthetics — particularly Climashield Apex, which uses a continuous-filament construction — are highly durable. The fibers don't clump, migrate, or break down readily under compression and use. PrimaLoft varies by product tier, but even mid-tier synthetic fills outlast wool under equivalent conditions.

## Weight and Packability

Wool is denser than synthetic fills. A wool-insulated jacket will weigh more than a comparable synthetic jacket for the same warmth level, sometimes significantly so. Packability follows: wool doesn't compress to the same volume as synthetic fills (and compresses far less than down). For ultralight or volume-constrained packing — common priorities in Pacific Northwest three-season hiking where rain gear, layers, and shelter add system weight — this difference compounds.

Climashield Apex packs moderately, not as small as down, but meaningfully smaller and lighter than comparable wool. PrimaLoft Black and Gold compress more aggressively than Apex and are closer to down in packability.

## Environmental Impact

Wool has a genuine environmental case: it's a renewable, biodegradable fiber. Merino sheep farming, however, carries significant land-use, water, and methane burdens. Mulesing practices in some supply chains raise animal welfare concerns, though RWS (Responsible Wool Standard) and ZQ-certified sources address this. The full lifecycle footprint of merino wool is contested but not clearly superior to synthetics on every dimension.

Synthetic fills are petroleum-derived. PrimaLoft has introduced Bio and recycled-content lines (PrimaLoft Bio degrades in landfill/marine environments; PrimaLoft Eco uses recycled PET), which meaningfully reduce virgin feedstock use. Neither option is clearly "clean" — the question is which environmental costs you weight more heavily.

## Cost

Wool insulation is more expensive to source and manufacture, which is reflected in retail pricing. Wool-insulated jackets typically command a significant premium over synthetic equivalents — often $50–$150 more for comparable construction and brand tier. Synthetic insulation is a mature, commoditized technology; competition has driven prices down while performance has continued to improve.

## Strongest Case Against My Stated Preference

**1. The Pacific Northwest is a high-rain environment, and wool fails in full saturation.** The PNW three-season window — particularly shoulder seasons (March–May, September–October) — involves genuine multi-hour and multi-day rain events, not prolonged humidity. When insulation becomes fully saturated, wool's loft collapses substantially and its effective warmth drops sharply. Synthetics retain loft and warmth under full saturation in ways wool does not. The "warmth when wet" property of wool is real but applies most strongly in moderate damp conditions; in sustained downpours without a highly capable shell, wool insulation underperforms the synthetic alternative precisely in the conditions most likely to occur in the PNW. Choosing wool means placing greater reliance on your shell layer to keep the insulation dry — adding a dependency and a single point of failure.

**2. Weight and packability disadvantages directly penalize system performance.** Three-season PNW backpacking demands rain gear, camp layers, and contingency insulation that synthetic or down alternatives don't require to the same degree. A wool insulation layer that is heavier and bulkier than a synthetic equivalent compounds an already weight-loaded system. Over the course of a multi-day trip, the weight difference between a wool jacket and a synthetic jacket of equivalent warmth can be 150–300g — not trivial when you're already carrying a heavy rain shell, shelter stakes, and wet gear. Packability matters when you're stuffing a damp jacket into an already compressed pack.

**3. Synthetic insulation has materially closed the comfort gap that historically justified wool.** The original case for wool over synthetics included: wool doesn't feel clammy, manages odor better, and feels comfortable against skin. Modern PrimaLoft and Climashield fiber engineering has largely addressed the clammy-when-damp problem; high-quality synthetic insulation today feels significantly less plastic and clammy than it did a decade ago. The odor argument has more validity, but hiking jackets are not typically worn directly against skin over extended periods the way base layers are — the odor resistance benefit is real but secondary for a jacket used over a base layer. If the comfort advantages of wool were the original draw, the gap has narrowed considerably.
