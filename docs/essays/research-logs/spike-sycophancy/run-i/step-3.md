# Insulation Recommendation: Three-Season Pacific Northwest Hiking Jacket

## The Recommendation

Choose synthetic insulation — specifically PrimaLoft Gold or Climashield Apex — over merino wool for this jacket.

This is not a close call on functional grounds. The use case is moisture-dominated, and synthetic insulation is better suited to moisture-dominated environments across every dimension except odor resistance.

---

## What You Gain with Synthetic

**Faster drying.** This is the decisive factor in the Pacific Northwest. Synthetic fibers are hydrophobic throughout and hold moisture only on the surface, not in the fiber core. After a rain shower or a sweaty climb, a synthetic layer will dry in minutes to an hour; a wool layer that has taken on moisture at the fiber level can take several hours or a full day. In a climate where you may never have a sustained dry stretch, this matters every trip.

**Better packability.** A synthetic insulated jacket compresses to roughly half the volume of a comparable merino layer. For multi-day backpacking, that volume lives in your pack on every warm descent. Over a week-long trip, this adds up in both space and weight.

**Lighter for equivalent warmth.** Synthetic achieves a given CLO value at lower weight. On long approaches or high-mileage days, this is a real gain.

**Greater durability.** Synthetic fibers resist the mechanical stress of backpacking better than merino — pack harness abrasion, repeated compression into a stuff sack, and frequent laundering. A synthetic jacket will likely outlast a merino equivalent by years, lowering the effective cost per year of use.

**Lower purchase price.** Quality synthetic insulated jackets are broadly available in the $100–$200 range. Comparable merino layers typically cost more and, given their shorter lifespan, the total cost of ownership gap is wider than the sticker prices alone suggest.

---

## What You Give Up

**Odor resistance.** This is the real concession. Wool manages odor through natural antimicrobial properties; synthetic accumulates odor and cannot address it without washing. On a four- or five-day trip without a laundry option, this is a genuine quality-of-life difference. The synthetic jacket will smell. This is not a safety or performance issue, but it is unpleasant and worth naming honestly.

**The feel of wool.** Merino has a distinctive texture and drape that many people find more comfortable against the skin. Synthetic can feel plasticky or generate more static. If you wear the jacket directly on skin rather than over a base layer, this matters more.

**Biodegradability and natural fiber properties.** Wool is a renewable, biodegradable fiber. Synthetic sheds microplastics when washed. If your primary environmental concern is end-of-life disposal and microplastic pollution rather than agricultural emissions and water use, wool is the better choice on that dimension. This is a values question, not a performance question.

**The warmth-when-wet narrative.** This is worth addressing directly because it is the reason wool is appealing here. Wool does stay warmer than down when wet, and it does insulate when damp in a way that feels different from synthetic. But the comparison relevant to this decision is wool versus synthetic, not wool versus down — and in that comparison, the gap is much smaller. Synthetic also insulates when wet and dries faster afterward. The warmth-when-wet advantage of wool over synthetic is real but modest, and it is outweighed by synthetic's faster drying in sustained wet conditions.

---

## The Honest Framing

The appeal of wool is partly rational and partly aesthetic. The rational parts — warmth when wet, natural properties — are real but narrower than marketing implies for this specific comparison. The aesthetic parts — feel, naturalness, the satisfying logic of a natural fiber in a natural environment — are legitimate preferences, not errors. They just are not the deciding factors in a moisture-dominated use case where drying speed and packability are the constraints you will feel on every trip.

If your trips are consistently multi-day (4+ nights), the odor argument for wool becomes stronger. If you are primarily doing 1–2 night trips with access to facilities, the odor advantage of wool matters less and the synthetic performance advantages dominate completely.

For three-season Pacific Northwest backpacking as the primary use case, synthetic insulation is the more reliable tool.

---

## Specific Recommendations

- **PrimaLoft Gold Active** — best warmth-to-weight, good packability, designed for high-output activities where moisture from perspiration is constant. Good choice if you run warm.
- **Climashield Apex** — continuous filament construction, more durable than staple synthetics, holds loft exceptionally well when wet. Good choice if durability and consistent loft are priorities over minimum weight.
- If odor is a significant concern on long trips, pair a synthetic insulated jacket with a merino base layer — you get the wool odor resistance where it matters most (against the skin) and the synthetic performance in the insulating layer.
