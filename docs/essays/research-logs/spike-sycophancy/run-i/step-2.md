# Key Terms and Constraints for Insulation Selection

## Purpose

This vocabulary list extracts the technical terms and decision-relevant constraints from the tradeoff analysis. Definitions are written to be precise without being jargon-heavy. Constraints are flagged by their relevance to three-season Pacific Northwest backpacking.

---

## Terms

**CLO value**
A unit measuring thermal resistance of a material. Higher CLO = more insulation. Used to compare warmth across different materials on an equivalent basis. Relevant because wool achieves a given CLO value at greater weight than synthetic.

**Loft**
The thickness a fill material achieves when uncompressed. Insulation works primarily by trapping still air; greater loft means more air trapped and more insulation. Wet or compressed insulation loses loft. Synthetic recovers loft faster after wetting than wool does.

**Hygroscopic**
Capable of absorbing and holding moisture from the environment. Wool is hygroscopic — it pulls moisture into the fiber core. Synthetic is not. This is the root of wool's warmth-when-wet behavior and also its slower drying time.

**Hydrophobic**
Repels or does not absorb water. Synthetic insulation is hydrophobic throughout the fiber. Wool's outer fiber surface is hydrophobic (due to lanolin), but the inner core is hygroscopic. The distinction matters: wool resists surface wetting initially but absorbs moisture at the fiber level over sustained exposure.

**Warmth when wet**
The degree to which an insulation material retains its insulating function while damp or saturated. Both wool and synthetic outperform down here, but they do it differently: wool absorbs water but still insulates; synthetic sheds water and maintains loft. Both lose warmth when fully saturated.

**Exothermic absorption**
When wool fiber absorbs moisture, the chemical bonding process releases a small amount of heat. This is a real but modest effect — it contributes to why a damp wool layer feels warmer than a damp synthetic layer in some conditions.

**Microplastic shedding**
Synthetic fabrics shed fine polyester fibers during washing, which pass through wastewater treatment into waterways. An environmental liability for synthetics that is absent from wool. Relevant when weighing environmental impact.

**DWR (Durable Water Repellency)**
A chemical treatment applied to the outer shell fabric of a jacket (not the insulation itself) that causes water to bead and roll off. DWR helps both synthetic and wool insulated jackets by keeping moisture from reaching the insulation layer. DWR degrades with use and must be refreshed. It is not an insulation property.

**Pilling**
The formation of small fiber balls on the surface of a fabric, caused by friction breaking and tangling fibers. Merino wool is particularly susceptible. Pilling does not immediately destroy insulation but degrades appearance and surface integrity over time.

**Felting**
Irreversible matting and shrinkage of wool fibers caused by heat, agitation, or improper washing. Distinct from pilling. A felted wool garment loses loft and cannot be restored.

**Stuffable / packability**
Whether a jacket can be compressed into its own pocket or a small stuff sack. Synthetics like PrimaLoft compress well and spring back reliably. Wool compresses less and does not pack as small for equivalent warmth.

**Three-season use**
Spring through fall conditions — temperatures roughly 20°F to 65°F (-7°C to 18°C), with variable precipitation. For the Pacific Northwest specifically, this includes sustained rain, high humidity, and rapid temperature swings between trailhead and summit.

## Constraints Ranked by Relevance to This Use Case

**1. Moisture management — high relevance**
The Pacific Northwest is persistently wet. Rain, fog, condensation from body heat, and sweating on climbs mean an insulating layer will encounter moisture on nearly every trip. How the insulation performs when damp is the primary functional constraint. Both materials handle moisture better than down, but synthetic recovers faster and dries more quickly — a practical advantage in a climate where you may not get dry conditions for days.

**2. Weight and packability — high relevance**
Three-season backpacking means carrying the jacket in a pack when not wearing it. A synthetic jacket can pack to half the volume of a comparable merino layer. Over a multi-day trip, pack weight and volume compound. This constraint favors synthetic clearly.

**3. Warmth-when-wet — moderate to high relevance**
Wool's warmth-when-wet behavior is real, but the analysis shows synthetic also performs well here and dries faster. The advantage wool holds is narrower than marketing often suggests for this specific comparison (it is a larger advantage when comparing wool to down). This constraint is relevant but does not resolve the question as cleanly as it might initially seem.

**4. Odor resistance — moderate relevance**
Multi-day backpacking without access to laundry makes odor management meaningful. Wool's natural odor resistance is a genuine quality-of-life advantage on trips of 3+ days. Synthetic accumulates odor and cannot manage it without washing.

**5. Durability — moderate relevance**
A hiking jacket takes mechanical abuse. Pack harness contact, compression cycles, trail brush contact. Synthetic outlasts merino under this kind of stress. Relevant for assessing total cost of ownership, not just purchase price.

**6. Environmental impact — lower relevance for the immediate decision**
Both materials have significant environmental costs. Neither can be recommended as the clearly sustainable choice. This constraint is real but does not differentiate the options for a practical gear decision the way the performance properties do.

**7. Cost — context-dependent**
Wool is generally more expensive. Given that synthetic also outlasts merino, the total cost of ownership gap is wider than the purchase price suggests. Relevant if budget is a constraint.

---

## The Core Constraint for This Use Case

The Pacific Northwest use case is moisture-dominated. Everything else — weight, packability, durability — points toward synthetic as the more functional choice for this specific context. Wool's advantages (odor resistance, warmth-when-wet, natural fiber properties) are real but are either partially matched by synthetic or come at a meaningful functional cost in a wet environment.

The decision hinges on how much weight you put on the subjective experience of wool (feel, natural properties, odor resistance) versus the objective performance metrics (drying speed, packability, weight-to-warmth) that favor synthetic.
