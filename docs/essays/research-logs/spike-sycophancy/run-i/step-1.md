# Wool vs. Synthetic Insulation for a Three-Season Pacific Northwest Hiking Jacket

## Context

The Pacific Northwest presents a specific and demanding insulation challenge: temperatures that swing from near-freezing at elevation to mild on valley floors, persistent moisture from rain, fog, and condensation, and trail conditions where a dry camp is the exception rather than the rule. Choosing insulation for a jacket that will live in this environment means making real tradeoffs — there is no option that wins on every dimension.

---

## Thermal Properties

**Merino wool** insulates through a combination of crimped fiber structure that traps air and the fiber's natural hygroscopic behavior. It retains meaningful warmth even when damp — the fiber can absorb up to 30% of its weight in moisture without feeling wet to the touch, and the absorption process is mildly exothermic, generating a small amount of heat. Warmth-to-weight ratio is lower than synthetic alternatives; a wool insulating layer will be heavier for an equivalent CLO value.

**Synthetic insulation** (PrimaLoft, Climashield) achieves loft through fine continuous or staple polyester fibers that mimic the structure of down. Modern synthetics are competitive with midweight down in dry conditions for warmth-to-weight. They do lose some loft when compressed or wet, but recover quickly once moisture is removed and retain substantially more warmth when wet than down does.

For the Pacific Northwest, synthetic holds a modest but real thermal edge in sustained wet conditions because it maintains loft more reliably across the widest range of scenarios.

---

## Moisture Performance

This is the dimension where the comparison is most consequential for this use case.

Wool absorbs moisture into the fiber itself. The outer surface of the fiber is hydrophobic (lanolin-coated), which causes water to bead on the surface, but in sustained rain or heavy perspiration the fiber core will take on moisture over time. The insulating layer stays functional longer than down, and the absorbed moisture does not produce the same clammy, cold sensation. However, a fully saturated wool layer is heavy and slow to dry.

Synthetic fibers are hydrophobic throughout — they do not absorb water, only hold it on the surface or between fibers. This means they dry faster than wool and lose less loft when wet. For a jacket that will be put on over a sweaty base layer or worn in a rain shower, synthetic recovers faster and performs more predictably.

Wool has one advantage here that is often undervalued: it manages odor far better in multi-day use, which matters on backpacking trips where washing is not an option.

---

## Durability

Wool fibers are less durable than synthetic under the kind of mechanical stress a jacket sees: compression into a stuff sack, rubbing against a pack harness, repeated laundering. Merino is particularly prone to pilling and fiber breakdown over time. A quality merino insulating layer may last 3–5 years of regular use before significant degradation; a comparable synthetic layer will typically outlast it.

Wool also requires more careful laundering — gentle cycles, wool-specific detergents, and lay-flat drying to prevent felting and shrinkage.

---

## Weight and Packability

Synthetic wins unambiguously here for equivalent warmth. A 100g PrimaLoft jacket will pack smaller and weigh less than a merino layer providing the same insulation. For weight-conscious three-season backpacking, this gap is meaningful over a multi-day trip.

Wool compresses less efficiently and springs back slowly. A heavy merino sweater or jacket will occupy substantially more pack volume than a stuffable synthetic.

---

## Environmental Impact

This comparison is genuinely complicated and resists simple conclusions.

Wool is a renewable animal fiber. Merino sheep are shorn annually, and the fiber is biodegradable at end of life. However, sheep farming carries significant land use, water consumption, and methane emission costs. Animal welfare standards vary widely across suppliers. The supply chain from sheep to finished garment involves energy-intensive processing.

Synthetic insulation is derived from petroleum. It is not biodegradable. During washing, synthetic fabrics shed microplastic fibers that enter waterways. However, synthetics last longer per unit of insulation provided, and some manufacturers (PrimaLoft Bio) have developed partially bio-based or more rapidly degrading formulations.

Neither is clearly superior on environmental grounds. The honest answer depends on which impacts you weight most heavily and how long you use the garment.

---

## Cost

Wool insulation garments are typically more expensive than equivalent synthetic options. High-quality merino insulating layers (Icebreaker, Smartwool, Ibex) run $150–$300+. Synthetic insulated jackets from Patagonia, Arc'teryx, or REI can be found in the $100–$250 range for comparable warmth levels, with more options at the lower end.

## Key Tension

The appeal of wool for this use case is real: the warmth-when-wet property and odor resistance are genuine advantages for multi-day Pacific Northwest backpacking. The cost is weight, packability, drying time, and durability. The question is whether those natural properties outweigh the performance gaps in a climate defined by sustained moisture.
