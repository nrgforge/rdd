# Key Terms and Constraints: Insulation Selection for Pacific Northwest Backpacking

*User context: leaning toward synthetic insulation for a hiking jacket, three-season backpacking in the Pacific Northwest.*

---

## Purpose

This vocabulary is derived from the tradeoff analysis in step-1.md. It defines the terms needed to reason clearly about insulation choice and identifies which constraints dominate the Pacific Northwest three-season use case.

---

## Vocabulary

**CLO value**
A unit measuring thermal resistance of a textile — the insulation needed to keep a resting person comfortable at 21°C (70°F) with normal air movement. Higher CLO = more insulation. Useful for comparing equivalent garment formats, but does not account for moisture degradation of loft or how the material behaves under load.

**Loft**
The thickness of an insulation layer, which directly determines how much dead air is trapped and therefore how warm the garment is. Loft can be temporarily reduced by compression (stuff sack, pack pressure) or moisture, and permanently reduced by mechanical degradation of fill fibers over time. Synthetic fill loses loft irreversibly with heavy use; merino fiber does not have an equivalent loft-loss failure mode.

**Hygroscopic absorption**
The capacity of a fiber to absorb moisture vapor into its core rather than letting it condense on the surface. Merino is highly hygroscopic — it absorbs up to ~30% of its weight in moisture before feeling wet to the touch. This is why merino stays comfortable during aerobic activity in damp conditions; it buffers perspiration vapor rather than letting it sit on the skin as liquid. Synthetic fill is not hygroscopic — it sheds water at the fiber surface, which is an advantage in direct rain but a disadvantage in sweat management.

**Hydrophobic**
Repelling water at the fiber surface, preventing absorption into the fiber core. PrimaLoft and Climashield fibers are hydrophobic by construction or DWR treatment. This property is what gives synthetic fill its wet-loft retention advantage over down in precipitation — the fill stays lofted because water doesn't penetrate the fiber. This property does not translate directly to managing perspiration vapor.

**DWR (Durable Water Repellency)**
A surface treatment applied to face fabrics (and sometimes fill fibers) that causes water to bead and roll off rather than soaking in. DWR degrades with use and washing. Most synthetic insulation jackets rely partly on face fabric DWR for moisture management. Once DWR is depleted, the face fabric can "wet out" and feel cold even if the fill itself is still performing. DWR must be periodically refreshed (heat or spray treatments).

**Wet-loft retention**
The percentage of original loft a fill maintains when saturated. Down loses 60–80% when fully wet. PrimaLoft Gold retains roughly 85–90% of its loft when wet (10–15% loss). Merino fiber doesn't have a directly equivalent loft-retention metric because its thermal insulation mechanism is different — the fiber absorbs moisture rather than repelling it, and maintains warmth through a different physical process (exothermic absorption + fiber crimp structure).

**Exothermic absorption**
The small amount of heat generated when merino fiber absorbs moisture vapor. This is a measurable but modest effect — it contributes to merino's "warm when wet" reputation but is not the primary mechanism. The larger effect is that merino stays thermally functional even damp because the fiber crimp structure remains intact when wet, unlike down fill.

**Continuous-filament insulation**
Synthetic fill made from long, unbroken fibers (e.g., Climashield Apex) rather than short-staple blown fill (e.g., standard PrimaLoft). Continuous-filament constructions resist compression degradation better than short-staple fills because the fibers don't migrate, clump, or separate over time. For durability under pack compression on a multi-day trip, continuous-filament is meaningfully better.

**Short-staple fill**
Synthetic insulation made from short individual fibers blown into baffles or channels (PrimaLoft standard). Packs smaller and feels softer initially, but fibers can clump, migrate, and lose loft faster under repeated compression than continuous-filament alternatives.

**Microplastic shedding**
The release of microscopic plastic fibers from synthetic textiles during washing and wear. Synthetic insulation jackets shed measurable quantities of microplastics per wash cycle. These particles pass through most wastewater treatment systems and enter aquatic ecosystems. PrimaLoft Bio uses partially bio-based fibers intended to biodegrade faster, but shedding during the garment's use life is not eliminated.

**Mulesing**
A surgical procedure used on some Merino sheep to prevent flystrike. It is controversial from an animal welfare standpoint. Third-party certification programs (ZQ Merino, Responsible Wool Standard) audit against mulesing, but compliance varies. Relevant for buyers who weight supply chain ethics in purchasing decisions.

**Layering system**
The functional organization of garments into base layer (moisture management next to skin), mid layer (insulation), and outer layer (weather protection). The insulation layer in a proper three-season kit rarely encounters direct precipitation because the hardshell or softshell is worn over it in rain. This context changes the moisture performance calculus: the relevant moisture source for the insulation layer is primarily perspiration from the inside, not rain from the outside.

**Shoulder season**
The transitional periods at the edges of the main hiking season — spring and fall in the Pacific Northwest, characterized by variable temperatures, frequent rain, wet snow at elevation, and rapid weather changes within a single day. Three-season Pacific Northwest use is heavily shoulder-season use, meaning the jacket must handle wide temperature ranges and frequent transitions between wet and dry conditions.

## Constraints Ranked by Relevance to This Use Case

**Tier 1 — Dominant constraints (drive the decision):**

- **Moisture management under a layering system.** In the Pacific Northwest, the jacket is rarely the outermost layer in rain. Perspiration management (hygroscopic buffering) and rapid reset after light exposure matter more than raw wet-loft retention in direct rain.
- **Multi-day odor resistance.** Three-season backpacking trips in this region are typically 3–7 days. Synthetic's odor accumulation is a real comfort and social penalty on extended trips.
- **Reliability across temperature transitions.** Pacific Northwest shoulder-season days require a jacket that performs from cold-static rest to aerobic effort and back. Both materials handle this, but through different mechanisms.

**Tier 2 — Meaningful but not decisive:**

- **Durability over the garment's service life.** Relevant for regular-use backpackers; continuous-filament synthetic narrows the gap with merino on this axis.
- **Pack weight and volume.** Synthetic edges merino, but the difference is small enough that it doesn't dominate unless the user is aggressively cutting base weight.
- **Environmental impact.** Merino has a clear advantage on microplastics; synthetic with recycled content reduces but doesn't eliminate the gap.

**Tier 3 — Real but secondary:**

- **Cost.** Price bands overlap at comparable quality tiers. Not a differentiator here.
- **Raw CLO performance in dry conditions.** The Pacific Northwest rarely presents the dry cold that maximizes synthetic fill's comparative advantage.

## Strongest Case Against My Stated Preference

The user is leaning synthetic. From the vocabulary above, the strongest counterarguments are:

**1. The dominant constraint favors merino, not synthetic.**
Once a layering system is properly understood, the tier-1 moisture constraint is perspiration management, not rain resistance. Merino's hygroscopic buffering is mechanically better suited to this than a hydrophobic synthetic fill. The vocabulary distinction between hygroscopic and hydrophobic is not semantic — it maps directly to which constraint the material is optimized for. Synthetic is optimized for the wrong moisture source for this use case.

**2. The durability vocabulary reveals a qualitative difference in failure modes.**
Synthetic fill's degradation is progressive and invisible until the jacket is noticeably cold. Merino's wear manifests as visible fabric damage that can be repaired or monitored. "Loft loss over 3–4 years" is a more insidious failure mode than "a hole at the elbow" because it degrades safety margin silently. For a garment used in unpredictable Pacific Northwest shoulder-season conditions, the failure mode matters.

**3. The layering system framing dissolves the headline moisture argument for synthetic.**
The vocabulary entry on "layering system" is the most consequential framing shift here. The user's stated preference rests on moisture performance and durability — but the moisture argument is constructed around direct rain exposure, which is not the dominant challenge when a shell is worn over the insulation. Once this is clear, the case for synthetic rests primarily on packability and drying time, which are tier-2 constraints, not tier-1. The preference is built on a strong argument for the wrong scenario.
