# Wool vs. Synthetic Insulation for Three-Season Pacific Northwest Hiking

*User context: leaning toward synthetic insulation for a hiking jacket, three-season backpacking in the Pacific Northwest.*

---

## Overview

Choosing insulation for a Pacific Northwest hiking jacket is a choice made under persistent moisture. The region delivers sustained drizzle, morning fog, and rapid temperature swings that sit at the intersection of every tradeoff between merino wool and synthetic fills like PrimaLoft Gold or Climashield Apex. Neither material is universally superior; each makes different bets about what the environment will demand.

---

## Thermal Properties

Synthetic insulation — particularly lofted continuous-filament constructions like Climashield Apex — provides predictable, even warmth. The fill is engineered to a specific CLO value, and that value is largely unaffected by compression or light moisture. PrimaLoft Gold loses roughly 10–15% of its loft when fully saturated, retaining meaningful warmth where a wet down garment would lose 60–80%.

Merino wool is a better thermal insulator than most people expect from a fabric rather than a fill. Fine merino (17–18.5 micron) used in midlayer constructions warms even when damp — the fiber's crimp structure traps air, and the hygroscopic absorption of moisture is exothermic, meaning the fiber generates a small amount of heat as it absorbs sweat. However, merino jacket constructions are typically midlayer fleece-style designs, not equivalent to a quilted synthetic fill jacket. Comparing them directly requires specifying the garment category.

For equivalent garment formats, synthetic fill edges merino on raw insulation-per-weight in cold-dry conditions.

---

## Moisture Performance

This is where the Pacific Northwest context dominates. Synthetic fill is hydrophobic: PrimaLoft and Climashield fibers shed water at the fiber surface, limiting absorption and maintaining loft under rain or sustained humidity. Drying time is fast — typically 1–2 hours in field conditions.

Merino absorbs moisture into the fiber core (up to ~30% of its weight before feeling wet to the touch), which buffers humidity and moderates the clammy sensation of sweat-soaked synthetic. However, once saturated — particularly in sustained rain without a shell — merino dries slowly, and a wet merino midlayer adds meaningful weight. In Pacific Northwest shoulder-season conditions where a jacket may cycle through damp-dry-damp repeatedly in a single day, synthetic fill resets faster.

---

## Durability

Synthetic fill degrades with use. Repeated compression, washing, and mechanical abrasion break down the loft-holding structure of short-staple fills like PrimaLoft. Continuous-filament constructions (Climashield) hold up better under compression cycling. Over a 3–5 year lifespan of regular backpacking use, a synthetic insulated jacket will lose measurable loft.

Merino is more durable in the fabric-wear sense — it resists pilling better than fleece polyester and handles laundering well — but high-use merino garments eventually develop holes in abrasion zones (shoulders under pack straps, elbows). A merino midlayer worn under a pack will show wear faster than a synthetic fill jacket with a face fabric protecting the insulation.

---

## Weight and Packability

Synthetic fills are heavier and less compressible than equivalent-warmth down. Against merino midlayers, the comparison is closer, but a quilted synthetic jacket generally compresses smaller than a merino knit of comparable warmth. Merino stretches into a stuff sack but doesn't compress to the same volume.

For three-season Pacific Northwest use, a 100–120g/m² synthetic fill jacket typically weighs 250–350g in a size medium. A comparable merino midlayer runs 300–450g depending on fabric weight and construction. The synthetic edges out on packability.

---

## Environmental Impact

This category disadvantages synthetic categorically. Petroleum-derived synthetic fills shed microplastics with each wash — measurable quantities enter waterways from garment laundering. PrimaLoft has introduced recycled-content fills (PrimaLoft Bio, PrimaLoft Gold Insulation with recycled content) that reduce virgin plastic use but do not eliminate shedding.

Merino wool's environmental calculus is more complex. Sheep farming carries land use, water use, and methane emissions; mulesing practices in Merino supply chains remain a welfare concern despite certification programs like ZQ and RWS. However, merino is biodegradable and does not contribute to microplastic pollution. Over a product's lifecycle, responsibly sourced merino typically has a lower end-of-life environmental cost.

---

## Cost

Premium synthetic insulation jackets (Arc'teryx Atom, Patagonia Nano-Air, Mountain Hardwear Ghost Whisperer Synthetic) run $200–$350 retail. Mid-market options with solid performance (REI Co-op, Outdoor Research Helium) are available for $130–$200.

Premium merino midlayers (Icebreaker, Smartwool, Ortovox) run $150–$280. The price bands overlap substantially. Cost is not a reliable differentiator at this tier.

---

## Strongest Case Against My Stated Preference

The user is leaning synthetic. Here is the most rigorous case for reconsideration:

**1. Synthetic insulation's moisture advantage is overstated for layered systems.**
The moisture-performance argument for synthetic fill assumes the jacket is being used as an outer layer in precipitation. In a properly layered system — hardshell or rain jacket over an insulating midlayer — the insulation layer rarely encounters direct rain. The real moisture challenge is perspiration vapor from the inside. Merino's hygroscopic absorption manages this better than synthetic: it absorbs sweat vapor without feeling wet, moderates clammy sensations, and doesn't build up body odor the way synthetic fills do on multi-day trips. In a Pacific Northwest context where you're likely wearing a shell over your insulation during actual rain events, the moisture argument for synthetic is weaker than it first appears.

**2. Durability favors merino over the jacket's service life.**
Synthetic fill's loft degradation is well-documented and not fully reversible. A PrimaLoft jacket used two to three times per week for three-season backpacking will show meaningful loft loss within 3–4 years. Merino fiber, while susceptible to wear at abrasion points, doesn't lose its thermal structure in the same progressive way. A merino midlayer that develops a hole can still be repaired; a synthetic jacket with dead fill has no equivalent recovery.

**3. Odor management is a real multi-day backpacking constraint that synthetic handles poorly.**
Synthetic fills and linings accumulate and hold body odor in a way merino resists at the fiber level. On a 5–7 day Pacific Northwest trip without laundry access, a synthetic insulated jacket worn close to the body becomes noticeably unpleasant. Merino's natural odor resistance — driven by lanolin and the fiber's antibacterial properties — isn't marketing hyperbole; it's measurable and experientially significant on extended trips. This is a direct use-case penalty for synthetic that is often underweighted in gear comparisons focused on lab-condition thermal metrics.
