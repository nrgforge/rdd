# Key Terms and Constraints for Insulation Selection — Pacific Northwest Three-Season Use

## User Context

Choosing insulation for a hiking jacket used for three-season backpacking in the Pacific Northwest. Currently leaning toward synthetic insulation, drawn by its moisture performance and durability.

---

## Vocabulary

### Thermal and Performance Terms

**CLO value**
A standardized unit of thermal insulation. 1 CLO is roughly the insulation needed to keep a resting adult comfortable at 21°C in still air. Higher CLO = more warmth. Used to compare insulation across materials without depending on fill weight alone.

**Loft**
The thickness an insulating material achieves when fully expanded. Loft determines the depth of trapped air — the actual mechanism of insulation. Materials that lose loft under moisture or compression lose warmth proportionally. Loft recovery after compression (e.g., when unpacking) affects how quickly a jacket is ready to perform.

**Fill weight (gsm — grams per square meter)**
The density of insulation material in a garment panel. Higher gsm = warmer but heavier. Climashield APEX is commonly used at 60–90 gsm for midlayer jackets; 133 gsm for heavier winter builds.

**Compression set**
The tendency of a material to permanently lose loft after repeated compression cycles. High compression set means the jacket becomes less warm over time as the insulation loses its ability to recover. Continuous-filament synthetics (Climashield APEX) exhibit low compression set. Merino fill exhibits moderate-to-high compression set under repeated stuff-sack use.

**Wet insulation retention**
The percentage of dry insulating value a material retains when saturated with water. Synthetic retains roughly 96%. Wool insulation degrades more substantially when fully saturated — loft collapses as the crimped fiber structure flattens under the weight of absorbed water.

**Hygroscopic absorption**
The ability of a fiber to chemically bind moisture vapor (not just surface water) into its structure. Merino wool is highly hygroscopic — it absorbs up to 30% of its weight in water vapor while still feeling dry to the touch. This is a buffer against sweat vapor in low-output conditions, but a liability in sustained-wet environments because the fiber holds that water.

**Hydrophobic**
Water-repelling at the fiber level. Synthetic insulation fibers are inherently hydrophobic — liquid water does not bond to the fiber surface. This is the structural basis for synthetic's moisture performance advantage.

### Materials

**PrimaLoft**
A family of synthetic insulation fills from Albany International. PrimaLoft Gold and PrimaLoft Black are the technical performance tiers. PrimaLoft Bio and Recycled lines reduce virgin petroleum content. Known for fine-fiber construction that mimics down in packability.

**Climashield APEX**
A continuous-filament synthetic insulation from PolyTech. Distinguished from cluster-fill synthetics by its cross-linked, sheet-form construction — fibers do not migrate or clump in use. Favored for durability and consistent warmth over the life of the garment.

**Merino wool**
A fine-staple wool from Merino sheep, primarily farmed in Australia and New Zealand. Distinguished from coarser wools by fiber diameter (typically 17–21 microns), which reduces itch against skin. Used in base layers primarily, but also as insulating fill. Subject to fiber breakage under repeated mechanical stress when used as structural insulation.

**Cluster fill synthetic**
An older synthetic insulation format using loose, blown fibers rather than a continuous sheet. Warmer-for-weight initially but prone to cold spots and fiber migration over time. Largely supplanted by continuous-filament constructions in technical gear.

### Use-Case and Context Terms

**Three-season backpacking**
Trips conducted in spring, summer, and fall. In the Pacific Northwest, this window includes sustained precipitation risk in all three seasons and temperatures ranging from below freezing (shoulder-season high camps) to mild (valley floors in summer). The jacket is expected to function across this full range.

**Layering system**
The functional stack of garments: typically a moisture-wicking base layer, an insulating midlayer, and a waterproof-breathable shell. The insulating layer's performance is partially determined by the behavior of the shell above it. A well-sealing shell reduces liquid water exposure to the insulation; a leaky or absent shell increases it.

**Waterproof-breathable shell**
A jacket with a waterproof outer membrane (Gore-Tex, eVent, similar) and some capacity for vapor transmission. "Breathable" is relative and often insufficient in high-output conditions — most WP/B shells under load accumulate condensation on their inner face, which can eventually wet the insulating layer below.

**Sustained-damp environment**
A condition more common than discrete precipitation events in the PNW. Rather than a single rainstorm, gear may remain continuously moist from drizzle, fog, and high ambient humidity over hours or days. This is the adversarial condition most relevant to insulation choice here — it keeps moisture levels elevated and prevents full drying between events.

**Saturation point**
The threshold at which a material has absorbed its maximum moisture load. Below saturation, wool's hygroscopic absorption provides some buffering. Above saturation, wool's insulating value degrades substantially. In the PNW's sustained-damp conditions, the critical question is whether the insulation will reach its saturation point during normal use.

**Compression cycling**
The repeated compression and expansion a jacket undergoes when stuffed into a pack, then unpacked and worn. Each cycle stresses the insulating fibers. Durability under compression cycling determines how much warmth the jacket retains after a full season of use.

**Odor resistance**
The degree to which a material resists bacterial growth that causes garment odor. Merino wool contains lanolin and has a natural fiber structure that inhibits the bacteria responsible for odor. Synthetic fibers do not share this property and develop noticeable odor more quickly during multi-day use without washing.

**Microplastic shedding**
The release of synthetic fiber fragments during washing. Documented environmental concern for petroleum-based textiles. Synthetic insulation is a source of microplastic release into waterways via laundry effluent. Some manufacturers have moved toward recycled and bio-based fibers to reduce (but not eliminate) this.

## Constraint Ranking for This Use Case

The following constraints are ranked by relevance to three-season PNW backpacking specifically:

1. **Moisture performance** — The most decisive constraint. The PNW's sustained-damp character, combined with high-output hiking, means insulation will be tested by vapor load from inside and potential liquid exposure from outside. Wet insulation retention and drying speed are not nice-to-haves; they are core performance requirements.

2. **Durability under compression cycling** — A jacket used for multiple seasons of backpacking will be compressed into a pack hundreds of times. Compression set matters for long-term value. The step-1 analysis showed synthetic has a clear advantage here for structural insulating fill.

3. **Weight and packability** — Secondary to moisture and durability for this use case, but not negligible. A jacket that weighs 150–300g more than its functional equivalent taxes the pack weight budget on multi-day trips. Packability affects whether the jacket gets carried or left behind on warmer days — which in turn affects safety margins.

4. **Odor resistance** — Relevant on trips of 4+ days without access to washing. Underweighted in step-1 analysis relative to its practical day-to-day impact. This is the constraint that most favors wool for extended trips.

5. **Environmental impact** — A real dimension but one that requires the user's own values to arbitrate. Neither material has an unambiguous environmental advantage. This constraint does not drive the recommendation but should inform it.

6. **Cost** — Synthetic's wider market means better price competition and more options at the $150–$200 target range. This is a soft advantage that reduces friction in the purchasing decision.

## What Would I Need to Believe for the Alternative to Be Right?

For wool to be the correct choice given this vocabulary and these constraints, one or more of the following would need to hold:

1. **The "sustained-damp" framing overstates liquid water exposure to the insulation.** If in practice the user wears their shell reliably, the insulation layer primarily sees sweat vapor — which is wool's strongest domain. The framing of "sustained-damp" as an adversarial condition for insulation assumes meaningful shell failure or regular insulation exposure to precipitation. If that assumption is wrong, the moisture performance gap is mostly theoretical.

2. **Odor resistance should be ranked first, not fourth.** Multi-day trips in the PNW can run 5–10 days. If the user's primary frustration on past trips has been gear smell rather than warmth loss from wet insulation, then the constraint ordering in this vocabulary is wrong for their actual experience. The step-1 analysis noted wool's odor advantage as a real functional benefit — it may deserve higher weight depending on the user's lived priorities.

3. **The durability framing assumes stuffing into a stuff sack is the compression mechanism.** Many backpackers store their jacket in a pack pocket or compression bag differently than the worst-case scenario. If the user's pack management doesn't involve repeated high-compression cycling, the durability gap between wool and synthetic narrows, and wool's other properties become relatively more important.
