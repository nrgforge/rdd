# Wool vs. Synthetic Insulation for Three-Season Hiking in the Pacific Northwest

## User Context

Choosing insulation for a hiking jacket used for three-season backpacking in the Pacific Northwest. Currently leaning toward synthetic insulation, drawn by its moisture performance and durability.

---

## Thermal Properties

Both materials trap air to generate warmth, but they do so through different mechanisms and behave differently under stress.

**Synthetic (PrimaLoft, Climashield):** Performance is measured in grams per square meter (gsm) or CLO value. Modern synthetic fills like PrimaLoft Gold and Climashield APEX are engineered for consistent loft. Crucially, they retain roughly 96% of their insulating value when wet — the fibers don't absorb water, so the air pockets remain intact. Warmth-to-weight ratios have improved dramatically in the last decade but still trail top-tier down.

**Wool (merino):** Wool insulates through a combination of crimped fiber structure and the hygroscopic nature of the fiber itself. Merino can absorb up to 30% of its weight in moisture vapor while still feeling dry and continuing to generate some heat (the absorption process is mildly exothermic). However, once fully saturated, wool loses meaningful loft and its insulating value degrades significantly — more so than synthetic. The recovery time after a soaking is long.

**PNW relevance:** The Pacific Northwest is defined by persistent, low-level precipitation — drizzle, fog, and shoulder-season sleet rather than single hard downpours. Gear stays damp for extended periods. Synthetic holds its advantage more decisively in sustained-damp conditions than in brief rain events.

---

## Moisture Performance

This is the most outcome-relevant dimension for the stated use case.

**Synthetic:** Fibers are hydrophobic by design. Water passes through or beads off rather than bonding to the fiber. Drying time is fast — often under an hour of body heat and air movement. In a wet camp environment, this is a material advantage.

**Wool:** The hygroscopic absorption that buffers initial moisture also means wool holds water. A saturated wool insulating layer can weigh significantly more than its dry weight and will take hours or overnight to dry fully, even in a heated tent. Wool handles sweat vapor well during low-output activity, but high-output hiking generates moisture faster than wool can buffer it.

**Note on layering system:** Most PNW backpackers pair a waterproof shell over their insulating layer. In that configuration, the insulation primarily sees sweat vapor from the inside and incidental moisture when the shell is removed or the zipper is open. This context slightly narrows the gap between the two materials in real-world use.

---

## Durability

**Synthetic:** Modern continuous-filament constructions (Climashield APEX) are highly durable — they don't migrate, clump, or develop cold spots the way cluster-fill synthetics once did. The fibers are engineered to resist compression set. A well-constructed synthetic jacket should maintain most of its loft through hundreds of compression cycles over five or more years.

**Wool:** Merino is the softest of the wool types but also the most prone to fiber breakage under repeated mechanical stress. Used as a structural insulating fill (rather than a base layer), merino compressed repeatedly in a stuff sack degrades faster than synthetic fills. Felting is also a risk with heat and agitation in washing. Wool used as a woven outer layer performs better in abrasion tests than merino fill.

---

## Weight and Packability

**Synthetic:** Competitive in weight per CLO at mid-range warmth levels. Climashield APEX (60–90 gsm) packs to roughly the size of a softball for a midlayer jacket. It does not compress to the extreme compressibility of high-fill-power down, but it rebounds reliably and doesn't require careful packing.

**Wool:** Merino insulation jackets are typically heavier per unit of warmth than synthetic equivalents. A wool-insulated jacket serving the same temperature range will add 150–300g compared to a synthetic alternative. Packability is fair — wool doesn't compress as small as synthetic and rebounds more slowly.

---

## Environmental Impact

This dimension involves genuine tradeoffs without a clean winner.

**Synthetic:** Derived from petroleum. Manufacturing is energy-intensive. Microplastic shedding during washing is a documented concern. However, synthetic insulation lasts longer in use, and some manufacturers (PrimaLoft) have introduced recycled and bio-based fiber lines that meaningfully reduce virgin plastic content.

**Wool:** A renewable, biodegradable fiber. Wool garments biodegrade at end of life. However, the full environmental picture includes land use for sheep grazing, water consumption, and methane emissions from livestock. Merino farming is concentrated in regions with documented land degradation pressures. "Natural" does not straightforwardly mean "lower impact."

For buyers prioritizing biodegradability and non-petroleum-origin, wool has a real argument. For buyers prioritizing longevity and recycled-content availability, synthetic is competitive.

---

## Cost

**Synthetic insulation jackets** span a wide range — from $80 budget options to $300+ for technical constructions. Mid-range options from Arc'teryx, Patagonia, and REI Co-op offer reliable performance at $150–$200.

**Wool-insulated jackets** (distinct from merino base layers) occupy a narrower, generally more expensive niche. Brands like Ibex, Icebreaker, and Finisterre offer wool-insulated pieces at $200–$350+. The category is smaller, so competitive pricing pressure is lower.

## What Would I Need to Believe for the Alternative to Be Right?

For wool insulation to be the better choice for this use case, the following would need to be true:

1. **The shell system is highly reliable and worn consistently.** If the user never removes their waterproof shell during precipitation and the shell performs without failure, the insulating layer rarely encounters liquid water. In that scenario, wool's moisture absorption becomes less relevant and its vapor-buffering during aerobic output becomes more advantageous. The argument for wool depends substantially on the assumption that the insulation stays dry.

2. **Odor management is a high priority over multi-day trips.** Merino wool has genuine antimicrobial properties that synthetic fibers lack. On a five-day trip without resupply, a synthetic midlayer will develop noticeable odor faster than wool. If the user finds this meaningfully affects comfort, focus, or morale — and values not carrying extra clothing — wool's odor resistance is a real functional advantage that the above analysis underweights.

3. **Long-term environmental impact is weighted heavily.** If the user is making purchasing decisions primarily on ecological grounds and prioritizes biodegradability and non-petroleum origin over longevity and recycled-content availability, wool's profile is genuinely better on those specific axes. This requires believing that the end-of-life biodegradability and renewable sourcing outweigh the livestock-related land and emissions costs.

4. **The user's activity profile involves low-output movement.** Wool's hygroscopic buffering of sweat vapor is most effective during slow hiking, rest, or camp use — lower-intensity activities where moisture generation doesn't exceed the fiber's absorption capacity. If the primary use case is slow exploration, photography, or stationary belaying rather than sustained high-output hiking, the moisture performance gap narrows meaningfully.
