# Key Terms and Constraints: Insulation Selection for Pacific Northwest Backpacking

## Vocabulary

**Loft**
The thickness a fill achieves when fully expanded. Loft is the physical mechanism of insulation — trapped air is the actual insulator, and loft measures how much air the fill holds. Higher loft at a given weight means better warmth-to-weight ratio. Both materials lose loft when wet or compressed; synthetic recovers it faster and more completely after wetting.

**Warmth-to-weight ratio**
The amount of thermal insulation provided per gram of fill. Typically expressed relative to down (which sets the benchmark). PrimaLoft Gold approaches down's ratio; most synthetic fills trade some warmth-to-weight efficiency for moisture and durability advantages. Merino wool insulation is competitive in dry conditions but its effective warmth-to-weight degrades when wet.

**Hydrophobic**
Water-repelling. Synthetic polyester fills are inherently hydrophobic — water beads off and does not penetrate the fiber. This is distinct from DWR (Durable Water Repellency) treatments applied to shell fabrics. The hydrophobic nature of synthetic fill is a material property, not a coating, which is why it remains functional even after repeated washings.

**Moisture-active**
Wool's characteristic behavior of actively exchanging moisture with its environment. Wool fibers absorb water vapor and liquid into the cortex (inner fiber structure), which buffers humidity and generates a small amount of heat through adsorption. This is useful at the skin interface (base layers) but creates problems in insulation applications where fast drying and low absorption are priorities.

**Wicking vs. moisture transfer**
Wicking moves moisture along or through a material by capillary action without absorbing it. Synthetic fills are generally non-wicking but also non-absorbing — moisture passes through the fill system via the shell and liner rather than the fill itself. Merino wicks at the fiber level through the cortex absorption process. In a jacket-weight insulation context, the distinction matters because absorbed moisture requires energy (heat) to evaporate; wicked or repelled moisture does not.

**Continuous-filament construction**
A fill construction method (used by Climashield) in which fibers run the full length of the batt rather than being chopped into staple lengths. This eliminates fiber migration — the tendency of short fibers to shift and clump inside a jacket's baffles or channels over time. Continuous-filament synthetics maintain even coverage after heavy use and washing; staple-fill synthetics (including some PrimaLoft variants) can develop cold spots.

**Batt / sheet construction**
A method of forming insulation fill into flat, consistent sheets before incorporating into a garment. Common with Climashield. Batt constructions tend to be more dimensionally stable than loose fills, resisting both migration and clumping. Less packable than loose fill, but more predictably durable.

**Fill weight (g/m²)**
The density of insulation in a garment, expressed as grams per square meter. Common values for synthetic midlayers range from 40g to 133g. Higher fill weight means more warmth and more bulk. PrimaLoft Gold 60g is a typical three-season midlayer specification; 100g+ configurations are more appropriate for colder conditions.

**Felting**
Irreversible matting of wool fibers caused by heat, moisture, and mechanical agitation. Felting collapses the fiber crimp structure that provides wool's insulating loft, permanently reducing thermal performance. It's the mechanism that turns a wool sweater into a doll-sized sweater in a hot dryer. Synthetic fills cannot felt.

**Pilling**
Surface degradation in which short fibers form small balls (pills) on the fabric surface due to abrasion. Common in merino under pack straps and hip belt contact points. Pilling reduces the aesthetic quality of the garment and, in severe cases, weakens the fabric structure. Synthetic outer shells are generally more abrasion-resistant than exposed merino.

**DWR (Durable Water Repellency)**
A chemical treatment applied to outer shell fabrics (not the fill) that causes water to bead and roll off rather than saturating the face fabric. DWR wears off with use and washing but can be reactivated with heat (dryer or iron) and re-applied. Most quality synthetic insulated jackets include DWR treatment on the shell. Relevant to total jacket moisture performance but distinct from the fill's inherent properties.

**Microplastic shedding**
The release of microscopic synthetic fibers into wastewater during washing. A documented environmental concern with polyester-based fills. PrimaLoft Bio and Climashield recycled products partially address origin-of-material concerns but do not eliminate shedding. Washing bags designed to trap fibers (e.g., Guppyfriend) reduce but don't eliminate the problem.

**Odor resistance**
Wool's natural resistance to bacterial growth that causes odor. Lanolin and the protein structure of wool fibers inhibit the bacteria responsible for sweat odor. Synthetic fills (and synthetic fabrics generally) can harbor bacteria more readily, requiring more frequent washing. Relevant primarily for multi-day trips between wash opportunities.

## Constraints Ranked by Relevance to This Use Case

**High relevance**

1. **Moisture performance** — The Pacific Northwest's persistent rain, high humidity, and frequent precipitation make wet-condition insulation performance the dominant constraint. A fill that loses function when wet is a liability, not a tradeoff.

2. **Drying time** — Multi-day backpacking trips don't offer laundry breaks. A fill that takes hours rather than minutes to dry after getting wet creates compounding risk across a trip. Synthetic's fast-dry characteristic is operationally significant here.

3. **Durability under pack use** — Three-season backpacking means a pack is almost always present. Abrasion at hip belt, shoulder strap, and sternum contacts is a daily reality. Sensitivity to abrasion (pilling, matting) or washing errors (felting) are durability risks that matter over a multi-year jacket life.

**Moderate relevance**

4. **Packability** — A midlayer that packs into its own pocket or a stuff sack is useful when temperatures shift during a day. Synthetic is reasonably packable; merino insulation is less so. Not a disqualifying constraint for either, but relevant for a three-season jacket used across variable daily temperatures.

5. **Weight** — Backpacking prioritizes low weight, but a midlayer is not the heaviest item in the pack. A 50–100g difference between materials at this weight class is real but not decisive.

**Lower relevance**

6. **Odor resistance** — Meaningful for multi-week trips or minimalist packing. For typical three-season trips (3–7 days), both materials function acceptably between washes.

7. **Environmental impact** — A real consideration but difficult to optimize at point-of-purchase. Both materials have environmental costs; certified sourcing (recycled synthetic, RWS wool) reduces but doesn't eliminate them.

8. **Cost** — Budget matters, but the price differential between quality synthetic and merino is modest relative to total gear spend. Not a deciding factor unless budget is tightly constrained.
