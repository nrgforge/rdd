# Key Terms and Decision Constraints: Montessori vs. Reggio Emilia

## Vocabulary

**Prepared Environment**
The Montessori term for a classroom designed with intentional order, child-scaled furniture, and purpose-built materials arranged to invite self-directed work. The environment is a teaching tool, not just a backdrop. Its predictability is a feature: it scaffolds independence by making the next step visible and accessible without adult instruction.

**Guide (Montessori)**
The Montessori teacher role. Guides observe children, introduce materials at appropriate moments, and otherwise stay out of the way. The shift from "teacher" to "guide" reflects a genuine role change: the adult is not the primary source of instruction; the environment and the child's engagement with materials are.

**Progettazione**
The Reggio Emilia term for project-based learning. Translated loosely as "design" or "planning," it describes the emergent, long-arc investigations children undertake based on observed interests. It is not a curriculum unit imposed by a teacher; it unfolds in response to what children actually pursue. Projects may last days or months.

**Documentation (Reggio)**
The systematic capture — through photographs, transcripts, recordings, and display panels — of children's learning processes. Documentation serves two functions: it makes children's thinking visible to them and their community, and it provides teachers with material to interpret and plan next steps. In high-fidelity Reggio programs, documentation is continuous and parents are expected to engage with it.

**Implementation Fidelity**
The degree to which a program actually practices what its label claims. Both Montessori and Reggio suffer from low-fidelity implementations: programs that borrow the vocabulary without the substance. For Montessori, accreditation bodies (AMS — American Montessori Society; AMI — Association Montessori Internationale) provide partial quality signals. Reggio Emilia has no equivalent credentialing system, making fidelity harder to assess from the outside.

**The Hundred Languages of Children**
Loris Malaguzzi's core metaphor for the Reggio philosophy: children have many ways of knowing and expressing — drawing, building, dancing, storytelling, arguing — and a good environment honors all of them, not just verbal and academic competence. The metaphor is aspirational but also practically demanding; it implies teachers trained to recognize and respond to a wide range of expressive modalities.

**Executive Function**
A cluster of cognitive skills — working memory, cognitive flexibility, and inhibitory control — that govern self-directed behavior, attention switching, and impulse management. Executive function is a reliable predictor of academic and life outcomes. Montessori's research base most clearly demonstrates advantage in this domain.

**Mastery-Based Progression**
The Montessori principle that a child moves to the next material or concept when they have demonstrated genuine internalization of the current one, not because the calendar says it's time. This is the mechanism through which Montessori accommodates children who are developmentally ahead of or behind age peers.

**Community as Third Teacher**
The Reggio principle that the physical and social environment — including the parent community — is a co-educator alongside the child and the teacher. This is not a metaphor; it has operational implications. Programs built on this principle often structure parent participation into the learning process in ways that are philosophically non-negotiable.

**AMS / AMI Accreditation**
Quality assurance frameworks for Montessori programs. AMI (Association Montessori Internationale) represents stricter adherence to Montessori's original methods; AMS (American Montessori Society) is more widely distributed and permits some adaptations. Either accreditation is a meaningful signal that a program has met external standards for environment, materials, and teacher training.

## Constraints Most Relevant to This Family

**Schedule compatibility — high priority**
Two full-time working parents need a program with reliable full-day hours. Montessori full-day programs are common. Reggio programs are more variable; some urban implementations are half-day or operate on cooperative models that presuppose parent availability during the day. This constraint alone may narrow the Reggio options significantly.

**Parent involvement capacity — high priority**
Community-as-third-teacher is not optional in a high-fidelity Reggio program; it is structural. Parents are expected to engage with documentation, attend project showcases, and contribute to the learning community. A family that cannot sustainably meet this expectation should think carefully about whether a Reggio program will work as intended — and whether the gap between expectation and reality will register as failure.

**Quality signal availability — medium priority**
In an urban market with Montessori accreditation available, parents can use AMS/AMI status as a meaningful filter. For Reggio, there is no equivalent shortcut; evaluating a program requires classroom observation, conversation with teachers about documentation practices, and ideally speaking with families already enrolled. This raises the cost of finding a high-quality Reggio program.

**Implementation fidelity — medium priority**
A low-fidelity Montessori program (Montessori in name only) undermines the executive function and mastery-progression benefits that make the approach distinctive. Similarly, a Reggio-"inspired" program that borrows the vocabulary without the documentation practice or emergent curriculum is just a regular play-based preschool under a different brand. Fidelity matters for both; the tools for assessing it differ.

**Child's temperament — variable priority**
Neither approach is universally better for all children. Montessori's structured environment and individual work cycles suit children who are self-directed or benefit from clear limits. Reggio's collaborative, expressive model suits children who are socially motivated and comfortable with ambiguity. Without knowing the specific child's tendencies, temperament is a consideration to keep in mind during program visits rather than a decisive pre-filter.
