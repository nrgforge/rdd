# Key Terms and Constraints: Insulation Selection for a Three-Season Hiking Jacket

## Vocabulary

**Loft**
The volume an insulation material occupies when uncompressed. Higher loft traps more air, which is the primary mechanism of thermal insulation. Both wool and synthetics depend on loft for warmth; the critical distinction is how each retains loft when wet.

**Wet loft retention**
The fraction of dry loft an insulation maintains after saturation. Synthetic fill (particularly PrimaLoft Gold) retains 70–90% of dry loft when wet; wool retains less, with full saturation causing significant loft collapse. This is the operationally decisive metric in wet climates.

**Fiber crimp (wool)**
The natural waviness in wool fiber that creates air-trapping structure. Crimp is what gives wool its baseline insulating ability and also its dimensional stability over long-term use. Unlike synthetic fill, wool's crimp does not degrade with repeated compression.

**Continuous-filament construction (Climashield)**
A manufacturing method in which insulation is produced as a single continuous sheet of bonded fibers rather than loose fill or batts. Because fibers cannot migrate or cluster, continuous-filament synthetics resist clumping when wet and maintain even heat distribution throughout the garment's life.

**Hydrophobic fiber coating (PrimaLoft)**
A chemical treatment applied to synthetic fibers that causes water droplets to bead on the surface rather than wicking into fiber bundles. This extends the window during which wet synthetic maintains its loft before water penetration reduces effectiveness.

**Moisture regain (wool)**
Wool's capacity to absorb moisture vapor — up to 30% of its own weight — into the fiber core without feeling wet at the surface. This is distinct from surface wetting and provides wool with a buffer against humidity and light perspiration. It does not protect against sustained rain or full saturation.

**Dry time**
How quickly an insulation material returns to dry loft after saturation. Synthetic dries 3–5x faster than wool in equivalent conditions. In multi-day backcountry settings, slow dry time is a compounding liability: a garment that doesn't dry overnight compromises the following day.

**Loft degradation (synthetic)**
The incremental loss of fill volume that synthetic insulation experiences with repeated compression and use over years. This is distinct from wet loft collapse — it is a long-term structural change. Wool's crimp-based structure is more resistant to this form of degradation.

**Abrasion resistance**
A fabric or fill material's resistance to surface wear from friction — against pack straps, rock faces, or brush. Merino wool (especially thin, un-blended merino) has low abrasion resistance and pills or wears through faster than synthetic fill in equivalent use.

**Microplastic shedding**
The release of sub-millimeter plastic particles from synthetic textiles during washing. Synthetic insulation garments shed microplastics with each laundry cycle; these particles enter waterways and accumulate in aquatic ecosystems. Wool does not shed microplastics and biodegrades at end-of-life.

**Three-season range**
For Pacific Northwest backpacking, this means approximately late March through early November at trail level, with temperatures ranging from roughly 25°F overnight at elevation to 65°F during active daytime hiking. The operative challenge is not extreme cold but sustained moderate cold combined with high moisture load.

**Layering role (midlayer vs. shell)**
The insulated jacket occupies the midlayer position — worn over a base layer, under a waterproof shell in rain. This matters for the insulation choice because a shell mitigates the worst direct rain exposure, but body moisture (sweat) accumulates from the inside. Insulation that handles moisture vapor and sweat poorly will degrade regardless of shell quality.

## Constraints Ranked by Relevance to This Use Case

**1. Wet performance (most critical)**
The Pacific Northwest's defining characteristic is persistent moisture — not just rain, but sweat under exertion and saturating fog. Insulation that degrades when wet is not a minor inconvenience; it is a safety consideration in shoulder-season conditions where temperatures can drop to dangerous levels. This constraint alone substantially narrows the decision space.

**2. Dry time**
Multi-day backpacking means garments must recover overnight or during rest stops. A jacket that requires 6–8 hours to dry versus 1–2 hours is a qualitatively different tool in the field.

**3. Durability under pack use**
A hiking jacket is a working garment. It will be compressed into a pack, worn under straps, and used against rough surfaces. Insulation that fails mechanically under normal use is a liability on long trips.

**4. Weight and packability**
Relevant but secondary. A jacket that works reliably in wet conditions is worth several ounces more than one that doesn't. However, for extended backpacking, the difference between a 12 oz and 16 oz jacket is meaningful.

**5. Odor management**
Relevant for multi-day trips without laundry access, where a synthetic jacket may require more frequent washing. Wool's genuine superiority here is operationally useful but does not override the moisture performance gap.

**6. Environmental impact**
Real, and worth knowing — particularly the microplastics concern for synthetic — but secondary to functional performance for most buyers choosing a primary-use garment.

**7. Cost**
A consideration but not a constraint that changes the fundamental recommendation. Both categories have options across a wide price range.
