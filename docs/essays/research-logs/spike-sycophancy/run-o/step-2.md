# Key Terms and Constraints: Insulation Selection for Pacific Northwest Hiking

## Vocabulary

### Insulation Types

**Merino wool insulation**
Insulation constructed from the fine, crimped fiber of Merino sheep. The crimp creates natural air pockets that trap warmth. Fiber diameter is measured in microns; finer fibers (15–18.5 micron) feel softer against skin. Used in midlayers either as a woven/knit fabric (jersey-style) or as a batting layer in a shell jacket. Not to be confused with coarser wool blends.

**Synthetic fill (PrimaLoft / Climashield)**
Petroleum-derived continuous or short-staple polyester fibers engineered to mimic down's loft while resisting water absorption. PrimaLoft Gold uses fine short fibers for compressibility; Climashield Apex uses continuous filament for durability and consistent loft retention. Both are hydrophobic — they repel rather than absorb water.

**Down insulation**
Not in scope here, but relevant as a reference point: goose or duck down, the highest warmth-to-weight fill available, collapses completely when wet and is explicitly excluded from PNW wet-condition comparisons.

---

### Thermal Properties

**Warmth-to-weight ratio**
The amount of thermal insulation (measured in CLO units or approximated by fill weight per m²) delivered per gram of material. Higher is better for backpackers trying to minimize pack weight. Synthetic fills currently outperform wool on this metric.

**Loft**
The thickness or puffiness of an insulation layer — a proxy for the volume of trapped air, which is the actual insulating medium. Loss of loft directly reduces warmth. Wet fibers collapse and lose loft; hydrophobic fibers retain loft better when damp.

**Thermoregulation**
The ability of a material to moderate temperature swings by absorbing and releasing moisture vapor (as distinct from liquid water). Merino wool actively buffers humidity changes at the skin microclimate, delaying the clammy sensation that follows aerobic effort. Synthetics do not thermoregulate — they pass moisture vapor through or trap it.

**CLO value**
A unit measuring thermal insulation: 1 CLO is the insulation needed to keep a resting person comfortable at 21°C in still air. Useful for comparing insulation systems but rarely labeled on hiking gear; fill weight (g/m²) is the more common proxy in outdoor products.

---

### Moisture Behavior

**Hydrophilic / hydrophobic**
Hydrophilic fibers (wool) attract and absorb water into the fiber structure. Hydrophobic fibers (synthetic) repel liquid water, keeping it on the surface where it can be shed or evaporated. Wool's hydrophilic core coexists with a hydrophobic outer scale layer, which is why it resists feeling wet during light moisture but ultimately saturates under sustained exposure.

**Moisture buffering**
Wool's capacity to absorb moisture vapor (not liquid) from the air and from skin respiration before that moisture condenses. This is the mechanism behind the "doesn't feel clammy" property. It is an active, temporary phenomenon — once the fiber's absorption capacity is exceeded, moisture buffering stops and the fiber becomes wet.

**Saturation point**
The moisture load at which an insulation material can no longer maintain meaningful thermal performance. Wool reaches a functional saturation point at roughly 35–40% of its weight in absorbed moisture; below this it insulates while damp, above it performance degrades rapidly. Synthetic fills have no equivalent absorption mechanism — they stay hydrophobic throughout.

**Dry time**
How quickly an insulation material returns to functional warmth after becoming wet. Synthetic insulation can recover warmth within minutes of being removed from a water source because the fibers haven't absorbed liquid. Wool fibers hold water internally and may require hours or overnight drying before full loft is restored.

---

### System Properties

**Layering system**
The approach of wearing multiple adjustable garments (base layer, midlayer, shell) rather than a single heavy piece. Insulation jackets typically serve as the midlayer. The system's total moisture management depends on all layers working together — a breathable shell affects how much moisture reaches the insulating layer.

**Shell vs. insulated jacket**
A shell is a waterproof/breathable outer layer with no insulation (e.g., Gore-Tex hardshell). An insulated jacket combines fill and an outer fabric in one piece. Some designs use a water-resistant but not waterproof outer fabric on an insulated jacket; these are not intended to replace a shell in heavy rain.

**Stop-start hiking**
Activity pattern common in backpacking where periods of high exertion (uphill travel) alternate with rest stops (breaks, camp setup). During exertion the body generates significant heat and sweat; during rest, the body rapidly loses heat. This pattern creates large, rapid swings in the thermal demand on insulation.

**Dump-and-freeze cycle**
The discomfort pattern where a hiker sweats through a synthetic midlayer during exertion, then stops moving and the wet synthetic provides little buffering against rapid heat loss. The clammy sensation is compounded by the lack of thermoregulatory absorption. Merino's moisture-buffering mechanism delays this cycle's onset.

---

### Durability and Maintenance

**Pilling**
Surface fuzz that forms when short fibers break and tangle. Merino wool pills faster than synthetic under friction (pack straps, arm movement). Pilling does not immediately reduce warmth but indicates structural degradation of the fiber.

**Compression fatigue**
Loss of loft that occurs when insulation is repeatedly compressed and decompressed (e.g., stuffed in a pack daily). Synthetic fills, particularly Climashield's continuous filament construction, show better resistance to compression fatigue over a product's lifespan than wool batting.

**Felting**
Irreversible matting of wool fibers caused by heat, agitation, and moisture acting together. Wool garments that are machine-washed in hot water or tumble-dried can felt permanently, reducing loft and altering fit. Synthetics do not felt.

---

### Environmental Terms

**Microplastic shedding**
Release of sub-5mm synthetic fibers during washing, which pass through wastewater filters and enter aquatic ecosystems. Synthetic insulation is a known source. Wool does not shed synthetic microplastics (though it sheds natural fibers).

**PrimaLoft Bio**
A PrimaLoft variant engineered to biodegrade in landfill and marine environments by attracting microbial activity to the fiber surface. Reduces synthetic insulation's end-of-life environmental impact. Not yet universal across all PrimaLoft products.

**Mulesing / welfare certification**
Practices in merino wool production relating to sheep welfare. Relevant for buyers with supply chain ethics concerns. Certifications like ZQ Merino or RWS (Responsible Wool Standard) indicate audited farming practices.

## Constraints Most Relevant to This Use Case

The following constraints carry the most decision weight for three-season backpacking in the Pacific Northwest:

**1. Sustained moisture exposure (high weight)**
The PNW delivers multi-day precipitation events, not isolated showers. This tips the moisture performance calculus decisively toward synthetic's recovery speed over wool's initial buffering. Wool's advantage is real in the first hour of drizzle; synthetic's advantage is dominant by hour six of rain.

**2. Stop-start aerobic exertion (high weight)**
Backpacking involves exactly the stop-start pattern where wool's thermoregulatory buffering provides the most benefit. This is the primary functional counterargument to synthetic in this use case, and it is not trivial.

**3. Pack weight and volume (moderate weight)**
Three-season backpacking prioritizes lightweight kits. Wool's weight and packability penalty is real but manageable if the jacket is the single insulating layer rather than a redundant piece.

**4. Durability over multi-year use (moderate weight)**
Wool's faster mechanical degradation is a meaningful cost consideration over a four- to five-year ownership horizon, particularly for a jacket worn daily on trail.

**5. Environmental values (lower weight, user-dependent)**
Microplastic concerns are legitimate but do not dominate performance decisions for most users. The availability of PrimaLoft Bio partially mitigates this.

**6. Layering context (high weight, often overlooked)**
How the jacket is worn matters as much as the fill. If worn under a waterproof shell, the shell does much of the moisture-blocking work, reducing the jacket's direct exposure to liquid water — which partially rehabilitates wool's viability. If the insulated jacket is the outermost layer (common in dry-ish conditions), direct moisture exposure becomes the controlling factor.
