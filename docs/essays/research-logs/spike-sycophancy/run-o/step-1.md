# Wool vs. Synthetic Insulation for a Three-Season Hiking Jacket in the Pacific Northwest

## Context

Choosing insulation for a three-season hiking jacket in the Pacific Northwest means designing for a specific and demanding environment: persistent rain and drizzle from October through June, wide temperature swings between trailhead and ridgeline, and sustained aerobic effort that generates body heat and sweat. The two leading options are merino wool (typically Ibex, SmartWool, or Isobaa constructions) and synthetic fill (primarily PrimaLoft Gold/Bio or Climashield Apex).

---

## Thermal Properties

**Wool:** Merino fiber traps air in a crimped structure that provides meaningful warmth for its weight in dry conditions. The warmth-to-weight ratio is respectable but not exceptional — a 200g/m² merino midlayer will be warmer than a bare fleece but noticeably heavier and bulkier than an equivalent-warmth synthetic puffy. Wool also has a useful thermoregulatory quality: it absorbs moisture vapor before it condenses, buffering temperature swings during variable-output activities.

**Synthetic:** Modern synthetics — particularly PrimaLoft Gold and Climashield Apex — deliver superior warmth-to-weight ratios. A 60g/m² PrimaLoft fill will match or exceed the thermal performance of heavier wool constructions at roughly half the weight. Synthetics maintain loft (and thus warmth) more reliably under compression, which matters when a jacket is stuffed into a pack for hours.

**Edge:** Synthetic, by a meaningful margin on warmth-per-gram and warmth-under-compression.

---

## Moisture Performance

**Wool:** This is wool's most cited advantage, and it's real but often overstated. Merino can absorb up to 35% of its weight in moisture before feeling wet, and the fiber's hydrophilic core/hydrophobic surface chemistry allows it to continue insulating while damp. In the Pacific Northwest's persistent drizzle and high-humidity conditions, this property provides genuine functional benefit — a wool layer stays comfortable longer into a wet event. However, once wool is fully saturated (rain, stream crossings, sustained sweat), it loses substantial loft and becomes very slow to dry, significantly heavier, and potentially a heat sink rather than a heat source.

**Synthetic:** PrimaLoft and Climashield are hydrophobic by design — fibers don't absorb water, so insulation value drops less dramatically when wet and recovery time is fast. In a sustained downpour, a wet synthetic layer will regain warmth within minutes of moving into shelter; saturated wool may take hours or overnight to dry fully.

**Edge:** Nuanced. Wool performs better in light, intermittent moisture and high humidity. Synthetic performs better in sustained rain and after full saturation. For the Pacific Northwest, where "wet" often means truly wet, synthetic's recovery advantage is significant.

---

## Durability

**Wool:** Merino is the weaker fiber. It pills, abrades, and develops holes faster than synthetic under pack straps, repeated compression, and heavy use. A quality wool midlayer used three seasons annually might show significant wear in three to four years; comparable synthetic constructions often last five to seven years or more. Wool is also more sensitive to washing — hot water and agitation cause felting and shrinkage.

**Synthetic:** Synthetic fill and shell fabrics are more resistant to abrasion and compression fatigue. They tolerate machine washing without special handling. Over a product's lifespan, synthetics generally require less careful maintenance.

**Edge:** Synthetic, particularly for high-use applications.

---

## Weight and Packability

**Wool:** Heavier per unit of warmth than synthetic, and it compresses less efficiently. A packable wool jacket exists (some use thin batting constructions), but it will occupy more volume and add more grams than a comparable synthetic puffy. For ultralight or minimalist packing, wool is a meaningful penalty.

**Synthetic:** High-loft fills like PrimaLoft Gold compress into small stuffsacks and are substantially lighter per unit of warmth. Climashield Apex sacrifices some packability for durability and sustained loft but remains more packable than wool.

**Edge:** Synthetic, substantially.

---

## Environmental Impact

**Wool:** Natural fiber, biodegradable, renewable. Merino farming carries its own environmental costs — land use, water consumption, methane emissions from sheep — but the fiber itself doesn't shed microplastics and will decompose at end of life.

**Synthetic:** Petroleum-derived. PrimaLoft Bio (a newer variant) is designed to biodegrade in landfill and marine environments, which partially addresses this concern. Synthetic insulation sheds microplastic fibers during washing, which enter waterways. Conventional synthetics are not biodegradable.

**Edge:** Wool on fiber origin and end-of-life; synthetic increasingly competitive with Bio variants. Microplastic shedding from synthetics is a legitimate and unresolved concern for backcountry-oriented users.

---

## Cost

**Wool:** Jackets using quality merino insulation or thick wool batting typically run $200–$350 at retail. Wool as a raw material is expensive, and that cost propagates through finished goods.

**Synthetic:** A comparable synthetic insulated jacket runs $150–$280. The spread narrows at premium tiers (Arc'teryx Atom vs. Ibex wool jacket, for instance), but at mid-market price points synthetic is generally cheaper.

**Edge:** Synthetic, modestly.

## Key Tension for Pacific Northwest Three-Season Use

The Pacific Northwest doesn't give you polite, intermittent rain. It gives you days-long gray drizzle, morning dew condensation on vegetation that soaks pants and sleeves, and rivers crossings. Wool's moisture absorption advantage — the property that makes it feel comfortable in a humid gym or light urban rain — becomes a liability when a jacket sees sustained field use without the opportunity to dry overnight. The warmth-when-wet claim is more accurate at low saturation levels; fully wet wool is genuinely problematic.

That said, wool's thermoregulatory behavior during aerobic hiking is a real differentiator. The moisture-buffering effect reduces the clammy dump-and-freeze cycle that affects synthetic layers during stop-start hiking. This is not a minor comfort benefit — it reduces the need to actively layer up and down, which has real-world friction cost on trail.
