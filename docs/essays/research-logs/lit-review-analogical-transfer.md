### Synthesis

#### 13.1 — Primary Failure Modes in Analogical Transfer

The cognitive psychology literature converges on two distinct bottlenecks in analogical transfer: retrieval failure and mapping failure. These are separate mechanisms, and their interaction with the Issue #13 scenario has different implications.

**Retrieval failure (surface bias).** Gick and Holyoak's 1980 radiation-problem experiments established the baseline: without explicit cueing, only around 10% of participants spontaneously retrieve a structurally appropriate analog. When a source analog is given but differs in surface features from the target, transfer rates remain low (roughly 30%) even when the structural solution is present in the source. The mechanism is that long-term memory retrieval is primarily driven by surface feature overlap — shared objects, story elements, domain vocabulary — rather than relational structure. Keane (1987) replicated and extended this finding: people retrieve what is superficially similar, not what is structurally isomorphic.

This matters for Issue #13 in the inverse direction. The failure there was not failing to retrieve a source pattern — the source pattern was immediately and successfully retrieved, because the surface features of the new context matched those of the prior working case well. The retrieval bottleneck was crossed too smoothly. The problem was that retrieval success masked mapping-level discrepancies.

**Mapping failure (structural alignment gaps).** Gentner's 1983 structure-mapping theory provides the key construct: an analogy is valid when higher-order relational structure (causal chains, functional dependencies) is preserved across base and target, not when surface attributes (object names, domain vocabulary) are similar. Mere appearance matches — high attribute overlap, low relational overlap — feel like analogies but break when the relational structure is applied. The "systematicity principle" (Gentner 1983) predicts that people preferentially map connected relational systems; the corollary is that isolated attributes or first-order predicates that do not participate in higher-order relations are often over-weighted in initial assessment and under-scrutinized in transfer.

Holyoak and Thagard's (1989) multi-constraint theory adds pragmatic constraints to the picture: beyond structural and semantic similarity, the purpose for which an analogy is being used shapes what gets mapped. If a developer is in implementation mode with a working pattern in hand, pragmatic pressure points toward applying it, not questioning it.

**Negative transfer.** The literature's named failure mode is negative transfer: the application of a source solution to a structurally non-analogous target, where the solution principle conflicts with the target's actual structure and produces a worse outcome than a fresh start would have. Novick (1988) demonstrated that novices exhibit negative transfer at higher rates than experts because novices' representations are surface-feature-rich rather than relationally structured. Critically, Novick's Experiment 2 showed that structural features dominate only after a candidate analog has been pointed out — in spontaneous retrieval, surface features dominate even for experts.

**The Einstellung effect.** Luchins (1942) named the phenomenon: after repeated exposure to a problem-solution pairing, solvers apply the familiar procedure even when a simpler or more appropriate alternative exists. The effect is not mere habit — it is active suppression. Bilalic, McLeod and Gobet's 2008 chess study provided mechanistic evidence via eye-tracking: the moment an expert recognizes a familiar pattern, attentional resources are directed toward features consistent with that solution and away from features that would reveal a superior alternative. Critically, solvers in this study verbally reported they were actively searching for alternatives while their eye movements showed they were not. This is the attention-inhibition mechanism: the first-activated solution framework captures attention before deliberate search can proceed.

The 2010 review paper (Bilalic et al.) generalizes this to "a pervasive source of cognitive bias," and the mechanism maps directly to Issue #13: once the familiar BUILD pattern was recognized as applicable, attentional resources were directed toward executing it, and the features of the new context that violated a key assumption of the original were outside the attentional frame. The three review points (scenario writing, fitness criteria, code review) operated under the same activated frame and therefore asked the same implicit questions.

#### 13.1 — The Analogical Transfer vs. Anchoring Distinction (Pre-Research Methodological Question)

The pre-research question was whether the cognitive mechanism here is best described as analogical transfer or as anchoring/priming — and whether the literature distinguishes these.

The answer from the literature is: they are distinguishable but overlapping. Anchoring (Tversky and Kahneman 1974) is a numerical judgment heuristic in which insufficient adjustment from a starting value produces biased estimates. Analogical transfer involves structural mapping from a source domain to a target. The mechanisms are not identical. However, they share an important family resemblance: in both cases, a prior representation constrains subsequent reasoning by making some information more available and some information less visible.

Research on "analogy as relational priming" (see Holyoak 2012 review) suggests that simpler forms of relation-based transfer can be produced by priming mechanisms that do not require full structural mapping. This points toward a spectrum rather than a clean dichotomy. The Einstellung effect sits at the intersection: the familiar solution primes attentional allocation in a way that functions like anchoring (initial value narrows subsequent search) but operates through the pattern-recognition and schema-activation machinery of analogical retrieval rather than numerical adjustment.

For the X.1 provisional answer's claim that Track #13 (BUILD pattern application) and Track #16 (RESEARCH question formation) share a common mechanism: the literature supports this as a directionally accurate but not fully precise claim. Both involve existing-context gravity. But the Einstellung research suggests that at the BUILD end, the specific pathway is attention inhibition triggered by pattern recognition — a faster, more automatic suppression mechanism. At the RESEARCH end, the mechanism is more likely question-framing entrenchment, which operates at a slower deliberative level. These are empirically distinguishable if one looks for them: the BUILD case should show evidence of attentional suppression (features present in the artifact trail but not attended to), while the RESEARCH case should show evidence of question scope limitation (questions that were not formulated). Issue #13's framing of the failure — "the information needed to prevent it was present in the artifact trail throughout the cycle" — is consistent with the Einstellung attention-inhibition account rather than pure question-scope limitation. This is a meaningful distinction for intervention design, treated further in 13.2.

#### 13.2 — Mitigations: Evidence and Ecological Validity

**Analogical comparison (Gentner et al. 2003).** The most consistently supported mitigation in the laboratory is requiring explicit comparison between two cases rather than study of a single case. Gentner, Loewenstein and Thompson (2003) showed across three studies — including a live face-to-face negotiation exercise — that comparing two analogous cases increases schema abstraction and forward transfer. The operative mechanism is that comparison forces explicit mapping, which reveals structural alignment and misalignment that single-case study leaves implicit. For the Issue #13 scenario, this translates to: before applying the source pattern, surface an alternative case or a structurally different implementation of the same goal and compare them explicitly. The act of comparison forces the question the Einstellung effect suppresses.

The ecological validity of this finding is above average for the analogical transfer literature: the Experiment 3 result was obtained in a dynamic, face-to-face task rather than a constrained laboratory problem, suggesting the effect is not laboratory-only.

**Schema abstraction and labeling (Gick and Holyoak 1983).** Gick and Holyoak found that providing participants with a verbal label for the underlying principle (rather than relying on implicit induction from examples) improved transfer. In a development context this suggests that making the structural principle of a pattern explicit — not just the pattern's implementation — increases the probability that structural mismatches will be noticed. GoF (1994/1995) patterns include "Intent" and "Applicability" sections explicitly for this reason. The practical failure, as noted in the SE literature (Brown et al. 1998), is that these sections are rarely consulted when a developer already believes the pattern fits.

**Pre-mortem / prospective hindsight (Klein 2007).** Gary Klein's pre-mortem technique applies prospective hindsight: the team assumes the decision has already failed and generates plausible failure reasons. Research by Mitchell, Russo and Pennington (cited in Klein 2007) found a 30% improvement in failure-reason identification over standard assessment. For pattern reuse, a pre-mortem prompt would be: "Assume this reused pattern caused a regression that passed code review. What assumption of the original context doesn't hold here?" This is a forcing function that directly targets the Einstellung mechanism by mandating active search for disconfirming evidence.

**Checklist-based questioning.** The empirical evidence on code review checklists specifically is mixed: some studies find improvement in defect detection for inexperienced reviewers, others find no significant effect. The limitation is that generic checklists do not target assumption-level questions — they tend to address style, naming, and obvious error classes. A pattern-applicability checklist would need to be structured around the "Applicability" preconditions of the specific pattern being considered, which is highly context-specific and not supported by current static analysis tooling.

**Ecological validity gap.** The largest gap in the literature is between laboratory-demonstrated mitigations (analogical comparison, schema labeling) and practitioner deployment in naturalistic development settings. The closest bridge is Gentner et al.'s 2003 negotiation study and Klein's 2007 pre-mortem work, both of which show real-task benefits. There is no published empirical study that measures the effect of structured applicability-questioning prompts on pattern misuse rates in software development. This is a genuine research gap.

#### 13.2 — The Satisficing Alternative Explanation

The research methods reviewer's alternative hypothesis — that the failure is satisficing under cognitive load rather than analogical transfer per se — has partial support but does not fully account for the evidence from Issue #13.

Simon's (1956) satisficing model: under bounded rationality, decision-makers stop at the first option that meets a threshold, rather than optimizing. Sweller's (1988/1994) cognitive load theory predicts that when working memory is taxed (as it is in active implementation), schema-driven automatic processing dominates deliberate evaluation.

These mechanisms would predict that a developer reaches for the nearest available solution not because of analogical mapping but because the decision has no forcing function requiring comparison. This is a legitimate account. However, it describes a precondition — the absence of a stopping mechanism — rather than a mechanism that actively suppresses noticing. Bilalic et al.'s (2008, 2010) Einstellung evidence shows that familiar patterns do something stronger than merely being selected by default: they actively redirect attention away from features that would reveal their inapplicability. The Issue #13 failure, in which the relevant disconfirming information was present in the artifact trail but was not noticed across three review points, is more consistent with active attentional suppression than with simple satisficing. Satisficing would predict that additional review points would catch the issue (they provide stopping opportunities); the Einstellung account predicts that additional review under the same activated frame will not (attention is still directed away from the disconfirming features).

The two accounts are not mutually exclusive: satisficing may explain why the first application was not questioned, while Einstellung explains why the subsequent review points also missed it. The intervention implications differ: satisficing suggests that any forcing function to stop and compare would suffice, while Einstellung suggests the forcing function must specifically shift the attentional frame, not merely add time.

#### 13.3 — Software Engineering Literature: Named Failure Mode and the Bridge

**The SE literature's structural framing.** Brown et al. (1998) formalized anti-patterns as "a solution to a class of problem which may be commonly used but is likely to be ineffective or counterproductive" — explicitly, patterns applied in the wrong context. The SE antipatterns literature treats context mismatch taxonomically: it names and categorizes failure forms but does not model the cognitive mechanism that produces them. Brown et al. identify the "Lava Flow" and "Cut-and-Paste Programming" antipatterns, both of which involve reuse without re-evaluation of applicability, but the treatment is structural (what went wrong) rather than cognitive (why the mismatch was not detected).

GoF (1994/1995) built applicability conditions into the pattern format under the "Applicability" heading, documenting the preconditions under which each pattern is appropriate. This is exactly the information that would prevent negative transfer if it were routinely consulted. The practical failure, well-known in the patterns community, is that the format's conditions are used for learning (understanding what a pattern does) rather than for application checks (verifying the current context meets the conditions). The GoF format contains the right information but does not create a forcing function for consulting it.

**Sandi Metz's wrong-abstraction account (2016).** Metz articulates a development process in which copy-and-adapt creates "the wrong abstraction": an extracted function is parameterized over time to accommodate variation across contexts until its original structural coherence is lost. The causal chain is: two things look the same, they are merged under an abstraction, differences are handled with parameters, and the abstraction eventually has no coherent structural basis. This is a practitioner account of negative transfer accumulating over development cycles, and it maps closely to the cognitive literature's prediction: surface similarity drives early reuse decisions; structural mismatches are accommodated locally (parameters, conditionals) rather than recognized as fundamental.

**The bridge between cognitive and SE literatures.** This is where the research gap is most visible. The cognitive literature (Gentner 1983, Holyoak and Thagard 1995, Bilalic et al. 2008) explains the mechanism by which pattern misapplication happens: surface-driven retrieval, attention inhibition by first-activated solutions, and absence of spontaneous schema comparison. The SE literature (Brown et al. 1998, GoF 1994/1995, Metz 2016) catalogs the structural consequences: antipattern taxonomies, refactoring prescriptions, and architectural failure modes. The connection between the two — a cognitive account of why the SE-observed failure modes are persistent even among experienced practitioners — is not well-developed in either literature. Novick's (1988) expertise finding (experts show positive transfer where novices show negative transfer) might suggest that experienced developers would catch these mismatches, but Bilalic et al.'s (2008) chess finding directly contradicts this for the Einstellung effect: the effect was demonstrated in experts, not just novices, and was stronger for problems that bore surface similarity to familiar cases. Expert pattern recognition, which is a domain asset, becomes a liability precisely in the Issue #13 scenario — highly similar surface, violated deep assumption.

### Key Findings

- **Surface bias in retrieval is a documented failure mode, but it explains the wrong half of Issue #13.** The problem was not failure to retrieve an appropriate pattern — the pattern was retrieved too smoothly. The relevant failure mode is the Einstellung effect: once a familiar pattern is activated, attentional resources are directed toward execution and away from features that would reveal assumption violations. (Bilalic et al. 2008, 2010)

- **The Einstellung mechanism is attention-inhibitory, not merely preferential.** Eye-tracking evidence shows that experts who verbally report searching for alternatives are not actually attending to the features that would reveal them. This explains why three independent review points all missed the same mismatch: they operated under the same activated frame. (Bilalic et al. 2008)

- **Analogical transfer and anchoring/satisficing are distinguishable cognitive mechanisms, but overlapping in this scenario.** Satisficing (Simon 1956) accounts for the initial selection without comparison; Einstellung accounts for the failure of subsequent review. They are complementary, not competing, explanations. Intervention design must address both: a forcing function for comparison at first application, and a frame-shift mechanism for review stages.

- **Schema abstraction via comparison is the best-evidenced mitigation.** Requiring explicit comparison between the source case and one or more alternatives before applying a pattern triggers the structural mapping process that spontaneous reuse bypasses. This intervention has above-average ecological validity (Gentner et al. 2003). No equivalent study exists in software development settings.

- **Pre-mortem / prospective hindsight is a candidate forcing function with a different mechanism.** It does not require identifying a comparison case; it reframes the decision as already-failed and mandates generating failure reasons. The 30% improvement in failure identification (Mitchell et al., cited in Klein 2007) suggests a pattern-assumption-questioning prompt at the point of pattern application could be effective. No empirical evidence exists in SE contexts.

- **GoF's Applicability section contains the right information but creates no forcing function.** The format addresses the cognitive problem in principle but not in practice: practitioners consult it for learning, not for pre-application verification. (Gamma et al. 1994/1995; Brown et al. 1998)

- **The SE antipatterns literature treats misuse structurally, not cognitively.** Brown et al. (1998) and Metz (2016) describe what goes wrong; they do not model why trained practitioners reliably fail to catch it. The bridge between the Einstellung account and the SE antipatterns taxonomy is underdeveloped and represents the primary research contribution available to this track.

- **Expertise does not protect against Einstellung; it may amplify it in high-surface-similarity scenarios.** Novick (1988) shows experts transfer better structurally in low-surface-similarity cases. Bilalic et al. (2008) show experts are significantly impaired by Einstellung when a familiar pattern is surface-activated. The Issue #13 failure mode — familiar surface, violated assumption — is precisely the expert-vulnerable scenario.

- **The Track #13 / Track #16 common-mechanism hypothesis is directionally supported but should be refined.** Both involve existing-context gravity narrowing content selection. At the BUILD end (Track #13), the operative mechanism is attention inhibition (fast, automatic). At the RESEARCH end (Track #16), the mechanism is more likely question-scope entrenchment (slower, deliberative). These are distinguishable and require different intervention points, which the provisional X.1 answer correctly identifies. The mechanism distinction strengthens rather than undermines the hypothesis.

### Limitations

**No empirical study of pattern-applicability checking in software development.** The closest empirical analogues are laboratory studies (Gick and Holyoak 1983, Gentner et al. 2003) and naturalistic analogues (Klein 2007 pre-mortem). The direct translation to development workflows is inference, not evidence.

**Bilalic et al. (2008, 2010) findings are from chess.** Chess provides excellent experimental control (eye-tracking, clear optimal solutions), but the transfer to software development involves extrapolation. The mechanism — attentional inhibition by a first-activated schema — is plausible in development contexts and consistent with the Issue #13 phenomenology, but has not been studied in software engineering settings.

**The SE literature's treatment of misuse is taxonomic, not mechanistic.** Brown et al. (1998) and the broader antipatterns literature do not review cognitive science research. The synthesis between cognitive mechanism and SE observation in this literature review is the reviewer's inference, not a documented connection in either field.

**Satisficing vs. Einstellung is not empirically resolved for development contexts.** Both mechanisms are consistent with Issue #13. A study that could distinguish them would need to measure whether practitioners with additional time and prompting still miss assumption violations (Einstellung prediction) or whether the violations are caught when explicit comparison is required (satisficing prediction would be satisfied by either intervention).

**Ecological validity of analogical comparison mitigations.** Gentner et al. (2003) is the strongest study for real-task transfer improvement, but the negotiation context differs from implementation work. Development involves a partially automated execution environment (tests, CI) that provides delayed feedback — a structural difference that may either mitigate the problem (failures eventually surface) or amplify it (delayed feedback decouples the error from the decision point).

**Coverage gaps.** This review does not cover: formal methods and type systems as structural constraint enforcement mechanisms; peer code review process literature beyond checklist studies; decision support systems literature on assumption-surfacing in engineering contexts; or human factors / safety-critical systems literature on analogy-based failures in aviation and medicine, which likely contains relevant operational evidence.
