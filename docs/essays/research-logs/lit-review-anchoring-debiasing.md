### Synthesis

#### 16.1: The Mechanism Question — Is It Anchoring or Priming?

The research-methods reviewer's caution is well-founded. The literature distinguishes at least three mechanisms that could be operating in solution-anchoring, and they carry different implications for intervention.

**Anchoring-and-adjustment** (Tversky & Kahneman 1974; Epley & Gilovich 2006) applies most cleanly when the existing artifact serves as a *reference point* from which the researcher adjusts: "How much should we change from what we have?" Epley and Gilovich's critical 2006 finding is that this mechanism operates specifically when the anchor is *self-generated* — when the researcher has participated in creating the prior artifact. Adjustment from self-generated anchors terminates when a plausible value is reached, not when an accurate one is found. The practitioner who helped write the prior system design is in exactly the self-generated anchor condition. This is stronger than the experimenter-provided anchor condition studied in most anchoring research, where the mechanism is selective accessibility rather than insufficient adjustment.

**Selective accessibility** (Strack & Mussweiler 1997) applies when the existing artifact makes some solution concepts more readily available than others, without the researcher explicitly using the artifact as a numeric reference point. The comparison question in the standard anchoring paradigm — "is this higher or lower than X?" — activates information consistent with X, which then becomes the informational basis for the absolute judgment. In the Track #16 case, the implicit comparison question is "does this approach integrate with what exists?" — and the answer selectively activates artifact-consistent considerations. This mechanism produces anchoring effects even when the researcher explicitly tries to think freshly, because the activation is pre-semantic (Tulving & Schacter 1990) and precedes conscious deliberation.

**Priming/concept accessibility** (Bargh & Chartrand 1999; Higgins 1996) is a broader category. Higgins' applicability framework distinguishes accessibility (a concept is activated) from applicability (the concept has featural overlap with the target). Existing implementation artifacts prime certain concepts not just because they serve as anchors but because they make entire categories of solution — "distributed," "microservice," "event-driven," whatever the prior architecture embodies — more mentally available and applicable-feeling to the new problem.

**The practical distinction for Track #16:** If the mechanism is primarily anchoring-and-adjustment (the artifact as a reference point for how much to change), then interventions targeting *adjustment process* are most relevant — consider-the-opposite, consider-the-alternative. If the mechanism is primarily selective accessibility (the artifact making certain concepts more cognitively available), then the more powerful intervention is one that *re-populates the accessible information set* before the research question is formed — counterfactual mindset induction, constraint-removal framing, explicit generation of alternatives before the existing artifact is consulted. If the mechanism is priming operating at a pre-semantic level, the implication is harder: instruction-based interventions may be insufficient because the activation precedes and shapes what reaches deliberate attention.

**The likely answer:** All three mechanisms are probably operating simultaneously, with different interventions having leverage on different components. Existing implementation artifacts (a) serve as self-generated anchors (insufficient adjustment mechanism), (b) selectively activate consistent information (selective accessibility mechanism), and (c) prime entire solution categories at a pre-semantic level (priming mechanism). The critical point for intervention design is that the *pre-semantic priming component is not accessible to instruction*. This directly extends Essay 013's finding that sycophantic agreement (the hard dimension) is mechanistically entangled with genuine agreement at the prompt-interpretable level — here, prior-artifact influence is similarly entangled with legitimate use of prior knowledge.

**Functional fixedness** (Duncker 1945) adds a closely related construct from problem-solving psychology. Duncker showed that prior use of an object in a particular function prevents perceivers from seeing it as available for a different function — even when they are explicitly instructed to think differently. Jansson and Smith (1991) demonstrated the design equivalent: engineers shown a flawed example design continued to reproduce that example's problematic features even when explicitly told to avoid them. Chrysikou and Weisberg (2005) showed that fixation operates across four distinct dimensions (direct physical similarity, reproductive similarity, analogical similarity, reproduced flaws), and that verbal instructions to avoid specific features fail to eliminate fixation at the analogical level. This is the design research literature's operationalization of the same mechanism as selective accessibility: prior examples activate their features in ways that verbal instruction cannot reliably neutralize.

#### 16.2: Debiasing Interventions — What Works, and Does It Transfer?

**The intervention landscape**

The debiasing literature converges on a taxonomy. Larrick (2004) organizes interventions into technological strategies (quantitative models, checklists, decision aids that restructure the environment), motivational strategies (incentives, accountability), and cognitive strategies (education, consider-the-opposite, analogical reasoning training). Technological strategies have the strongest empirical record but require the bias to be characterized in advance so that the environmental design can counteract it. Cognitive strategies are more flexible but have weaker effect sizes and more contested transfer.

For the *selective accessibility* mechanism, the most mechanistically targeted intervention is consider-the-opposite / consider-the-alternative (Mussweiler, Strack & Pfeiffer 2000). The logic is precise: selective accessibility produces anchoring because anchor-consistent information becomes more accessible; generating reasons why the anchor is wrong re-populates the accessible information set with anchor-inconsistent information, counterbalancing the bias. Lord, Lepper & Preston's (1984) original consider-the-opposite work showed it reduces attitude polarization. Furnham and Boo's (2011) meta-review confirms this as the most reliably effective cognitive debiasing strategy for anchoring specifically.

The important caveat: Essay 013's spike experiment showed that adversarial framing — "argue the strongest case against my preference" — *overcorrects*, reversing the bias direction. The consider-the-opposite literature has a parallel finding. Wilson and Brekke's (1994) contamination-correction framework identifies three conditions for successful correction: the person must be *aware* the bias is operating, *motivated* to correct it, and *able* to determine the direction and magnitude. When any condition fails, overcorrection is as likely as correction. Adversarial framing produces overcorrection because it over-specifies the direction and magnitude of correction. The belief-mapping framing from the spike — "what would I need to believe for the alternative to be right?" — is more consistent with the correction conditions: it establishes awareness without mandating a direction of adjustment.

**Counterfactual mindset as an alternative**

Galinsky and Moskowitz (2000) demonstrated that inducing a counterfactual mindset — having people consider "what if things had gone differently?" — increases disconfirmatory hypothesis-testing on *unrelated subsequent tasks*. The mindset transfers across domains because it activates a style of processing (simulation of alternatives) rather than content. Galinsky et al. (2002) confirmed this extends to group decisions: groups primed with a counterfactual mindset searched for more disconfirmatory information. Roese (1997) showed the underlying mechanism is causal inference — counterfactuals highlight causes that could have been different, which broadens the space of alternatives perceived as possible.

For Track #16, the practical implication is that inducing a counterfactual mindset *before* research question formation — "imagine the existing implementation didn't exist; what problem would you be trying to solve?" — may broaden the accessible solution space at a level that precedes selective accessibility. This is a different intervention point than the consider-the-opposite debiasing: it operates on the *information-gathering phase* rather than on a specific estimate or judgment.

**Ecological validity — does any of this transfer?**

This is where the literature provides its most important and sobering contribution. Morewedge et al. (2015) demonstrated that one-shot training in debiasing (via game or video) reduced anchoring by 24-32% immediately and 19-24% at two months. This is encouraging evidence for training-based approaches. Morewedge et al.'s 2020 follow-up showed the training effect transfers to field settings — real-world decisions showed measurable bias reduction.

However, Nagtegaal et al. (2020) found that in public management naturalistic settings, motivational and accountability interventions had stronger effects than cognitive interventions. The Frontiers (2021) systematic literature study on retention and transfer of bias mitigation found that what transfers is highly task-dependent and that transfer to naturalistic settings is "barely known." The Lilienfeld, Ammirati & Landfield (2009) review concludes: "debiasing is one of the most relevant goals of modern psychology" and "cognitive biases are robust and debiasing is notoriously difficult."

The specific ecological validity concern for Track #16 is precisely the condition Essay 013's spike could not address: deep user engagement with substantial prior investment in existing artifacts. Morewedge's training effects were measured on participants with no particular emotional or professional investment in the anchor. The practitioner beginning a research phase with existing implementation artifacts has co-designed those artifacts, may have advocated for them, and has substantial sunk-cost engagement. The anchoring effects in this condition are closer to the Englich, Mussweiler & Strack (2006) judicial sentencing results — where expert judges with 10+ years of experience were still anchored by irrelevant, randomly generated numbers — than to the lab-condition undergraduates in most debiasing studies. The robustness of anchoring under expertise conditions is directly relevant: expertise does not protect against it.

**Are there interventions specifically validated for the question-formation stage?**

This is the critical gap. The vast majority of debiasing research targets *judgment and choice* stages — estimating a value, deciding between options, evaluating an argument. The question-formation stage — before the inquiry has begun, when the researcher is deciding what to ask — has received much less experimental attention.

The pre-registration movement in research methodology (Nosek et al. 2018; Booth et al. 2012) represents the most operationalized treatment of question-formation bias in naturalistic research. The core Registered Reports finding is striking: while 96% of traditional publications produce statistically significant results (indicative of post-hoc hypothesis laundering and publication bias), only 44% of Registered Reports yield significant results. The mechanism is exactly what Track #16 identifies: researchers who know their results before specifying their hypotheses unconsciously (and sometimes consciously) adjust their questions to match their findings. Pre-registration forces the question to be specified before the data is consulted — the research methodology equivalent of constraint-removal. The PRISMA protocol extension for search strategy (PRISMA-S) applies the same logic to literature search: the search strategy must be specified and peer-reviewed before it is executed, precisely to prevent the existing literature from shaping the search design.

The lesson for Track #16 is that the research methodology tradition has converged on a structural intervention for question-formation bias: *specify questions before consulting artifacts*. This is different from the cognitive debiasing approach of correcting biased questions after they have been formed. Whether a similar structural intervention is feasible within RDD's research phase — specifying questions before consulting the existing artifact corpus — is a design question the literature does not answer directly, but it points toward the same direction as the belief-mapping framing from Essay 013.

#### 16.3: Research Methodology Protections Against Prior-Work Influence

**Bracketing / epoché (qualitative methodology)**

Fischer (2009) and the qualitative methodology tradition offer *bracketing* — derived from Husserlian phenomenology's epoché — as the primary procedure for mitigating prior-knowledge influence on research question formation. Bracketing requires the researcher to systematically identify and suspend their existing beliefs, assumptions, and interpretive frameworks before data collection. In descriptive phenomenology, this is treated as a precondition for valid inquiry.

The operationalization challenges are substantial. Fischer's review notes that complete bracketing is acknowledged to be impossible in interpretive phenomenology — the researcher's assumptions inevitably shape what is perceived as meaningful data. The practical technique is reflexive journaling *before* question formation: the researcher writes out their prior assumptions, prior beliefs about the phenomenon, and prior experience with the domain. This externalizes the priors — making them visible and therefore available for suspension — rather than leaving them operating implicitly.

The connection to Track #16: bracketing is operationally analogous to the pre-research question-formation audit proposed in Essay 013's Research Methods Subagent concept. Before the research phase begins, explicitly inventory the existing artifact assumptions. The purpose is not to eliminate prior knowledge (impossible) but to make it visible so it can be deliberately suspended rather than unconsciously incorporated.

**Systematic review methodology (PRISMA/Cochrane)**

Systematic review methodology has the most operationalized treatment of the concern. The core protections are:

*Protocol-first design:* The research question, inclusion/exclusion criteria, search terms, and synthesis approach are all specified and registered before any evidence is consulted. PROSPERO registration creates a public commitment to the pre-specified question, making post-hoc question revision traceable. This is the structural intervention: the question is formed in the absence of the evidence that will answer it.

*Peer-reviewed search strategies:* PRISMA-S requires that search strategies be peer-reviewed by an information specialist before execution. The rationale is that search term selection is itself a site of bias — researchers unconsciously select terms that will retrieve evidence supporting their prior view. External review of the search strategy before execution provides structural resistance to this bias.

*Reporting bias taxonomy:* The Cochrane methodology distinguishes publication bias (studies not published based on results), outcome reporting bias (outcomes reported selectively based on results), and question framing bias (the research question framed to match available evidence). All three represent the same core dynamic: known results shaping what appears to be an antecedent question or design.

The direct application to Track #16: within RDD, the research phase currently forms its questions in the presence of the existing artifact corpus. The systematic review methodology suggests that a structurally sound research phase would specify its questions *before* consulting the artifacts and would treat the question specification as an artifact requiring external review. This is a structural intervention at the question-formation stage, not a cognitive intervention at the judgment stage.

**Peer review design (anchoring in evaluation, not question-formation)**

Stelmakh et al.'s (2023) large-scale RCT (1,544 papers, 2,797 reviewers) is the most rigorous test of anchoring in peer review. The specific result is a null: they found no evidence of herding — reviewer scores did not anchor to the first reviewer's score in structured discussion. This is an important contrast with the cognitive anchoring literature and with earlier peer review studies. The mechanism explanation is that structured discussion with pre-formed independent judgments provides natural protection: each reviewer forms their initial assessment in isolation before seeing others' scores.

This null result supports a design principle: *isolated, artifact-mediated evaluation* provides structural resistance that sequential conversation does not. Stelmakh et al.'s structured process prevented herding; unstructured sequential processes (where each reviewer sees prior scores before forming their own) produce it. Tomkins et al.'s (2017) PNAS study adds the complementary finding that single-blind review (reviewers know author identity) introduces prestige bias that double-blind review substantially reduces — again, isolation from identity information at the evaluation stage matters.

The application is not to question-formation (Stelmakh's null result covers the evaluation-discussion stage, not the question-formation stage), but it confirms the general principle: structural isolation of evaluative stages from prior-information contamination reduces bias at those stages. Applying this principle to question-formation means: the research question should be formed in a context structurally isolated from the existing artifact corpus.

### Key Findings

**Findings extending Essay 013:**

- **The mechanism is probably priming (selective accessibility) rather than pure anchoring-and-adjustment for externally produced artifacts; it is anchoring-and-adjustment for self-generated artifacts.** Epley & Gilovich's (2006) finding that self-generated anchors produce insufficient adjustment is directly applicable to practitioners who co-designed existing artifacts. The practitioner has a stronger anchoring exposure than the experimenter-provided anchor condition studied in most lab research (Epley & Gilovich 2001, 2006; Furnham & Boo 2011).

- **Pre-semantic priming (Tulving & Schacter 1990; Bargh & Chartrand 1999) is the component not accessible to instruction.** Existing artifacts prime solution concepts at a pre-semantic, pre-deliberate level. This is mechanistically parallel to Essay 013's finding that sycophantic agreement (the hard dimension) is entangled with genuine agreement at the prompt-interpretable level — in both cases, the operative influence precedes what prompt-level instructions can reach.

- **Design fixation (Jansson & Smith 1991; Chrysikou & Weisberg 2005; Duncker 1945) demonstrates that instruction-based intervention fails to override prior-example influence at the analogical level.** Even when participants are explicitly told to avoid features of a prior example, they continue to reproduce analogical similarities. This is the design research literature's operationalization of the same finding as the sycophancy decomposition in Essay 013: the mechanism the instructions are supposed to address is not where the bias is operating.

- **Ecological validity of debiasing is poor in high-investment naturalistic conditions.** Lab-demonstrated debiasing effects (Morewedge et al. 2015, 2020) do not have established transfer to conditions where the practitioner has co-produced and advocated for the anchoring artifact. Expert judicial anchoring (Englich, Mussweiler & Strack 2006) — judges anchored by irrelevant, randomly generated numbers despite 10+ years of experience — is the more accurate analogy to the Track #16 condition than standard lab anchoring studies. This directly addresses the ecological validity gap that Essay 013's spike (compressed 10-minute pipeline with scripted inputs) cannot close.

- **Consider-the-opposite is the most empirically supported cognitive debiasing strategy for selective accessibility specifically (Mussweiler et al. 2000; Furnham & Boo 2011), but adversarial framing overcorrects (Essay 013 spike; Wilson & Brekke 1994 correction conditions).** Belief-mapping framing avoids this by satisfying the Wilson & Brekke correction conditions (awareness + motivation + non-specified direction) rather than mandating a direction of adjustment.

- **Counterfactual mindset induction is a different intervention point from consider-the-opposite.** Galinsky & Moskowitz (2000) and Galinsky et al. (2002) show that counterfactual mindset broadens alternative consideration before specific judgments are formed, and transfers across domains. This is an intervention at the *information-gathering phase* (before the research question crystallizes) rather than the *judgment phase* (after an initial estimate has been formed). The literature does not show whether it is more or less effective than believe-mapping for the question-formation stage specifically — this is an empirical gap.

- **Pre-registration and Registered Reports (Nosek et al. 2018; Booth et al. 2012) are the most operationalized structural interventions for question-formation bias.** The 44% vs. 96% significant-result rate comparison is the strongest available evidence that specifying questions before consulting evidence structurally constrains bias at the question-formation stage. This is a structural intervention (question specified before artifacts consulted) rather than a cognitive debiasing intervention (question adjusted after artifacts consulted).

- **PRISMA-S peer-reviewed search strategies** provide structural resistance at the search design stage: external review of search terms before execution prevents the known literature from shaping the terms that will retrieve it. Applied to RDD: the research questions and evidence-gathering approach should be specified and reviewed before the existing artifact corpus is consulted, not after.

- **Stelmakh et al.'s (2023) null result for reviewer herding confirms that structured isolation protects evaluation stages.** The protection mechanism is independent pre-formed judgment before any exposure to other reviewers' scores. Applied to question-formation: forming the research question before exposure to the existing artifact corpus is structurally analogous. The question should be seeded from the underlying need, not from the current implementation.

**On the Track #13 comparison (X.1 provisional answer):**

The provisional X.1 answer hypothesizes that Tracks #13 and #16 share a common mechanism (existing-context gravity) but differ in intervention point (#16 at question-formation; #13 at solution-application). The literature provides qualified support:

- The *mechanism* is indeed the same family: selective accessibility / priming / functional fixedness all operate by making existing-context-consistent information more available than alternatives.

- The *intervention* literature does not provide strong evidence that interventions *differ* between question-formation and solution-application stages in the specific sense the provisional answer suggests. Mussweiler et al.'s (2000) consider-the-opposite works on judgment (solution-application stage). Pre-registration works on question-formation. Counterfactual mindset induction may work on both. But the evidence base for question-formation-specific interventions is substantially thinner than the evidence base for judgment-correction interventions. This is because most experimental debiasing research targets the judgment stage where anchoring is most measurable.

- What the literature does support is that the *structural intervention* approach (constrain the context before the question is formed) differs from the *cognitive debiasing* approach (correct a formed judgment after the fact). Both tracks may benefit from structural constraint, but the structural constraint is implemented at different points: Track #16 requires question-isolation before artifact consultation; Track #13 requires pattern-applicability verification before pattern-application. The provisional X.1 answer is partially supported — there is a different intervention *point* — but the *type* of intervention (structural isolation, counterfactual/belief-mapping framing) is probably the same.

### Limitations

**Question-formation stage gap:** The debiasing literature is substantially more developed for the judgment/choice stage than for the question-formation stage. Most experiments present participants with an anchor and measure how it affects subsequent estimation. There are almost no experiments testing how existing artifacts shape research question formation specifically, as opposed to downstream judgments. The pre-registration movement addresses this gap structurally rather than experimentally — it prevents the artifact from influencing the question by sequencing them, rather than measuring and correcting the influence.

**Ecological validity of high-investment anchoring:** No study in the reviewed literature directly tests debiasing effectiveness when the participant has co-produced the anchoring artifact. The Englich et al. (2006) judicial anchoring studies with experts are the closest analogue, showing anchoring is robust to expertise. Whether belief-mapping or counterfactual mindset induction is effective in this high-investment condition is untested.

**Priming vs. anchoring mechanism split in design contexts:** The distinction between anchoring-and-adjustment and selective accessibility has been tested primarily in numeric estimation tasks. Whether the mechanism split (self-generated → insufficient adjustment; experimenter-provided → selective accessibility) holds in design and research question formation contexts is not established. The design fixation literature (Jansson & Smith 1991; Chrysikou & Weisberg 2005) suggests that fixation effects in design are robust regardless of mechanism, but does not test which mechanism is operating.

**Counterfactual mindset ecological validity:** Galinsky & Moskowitz (2000) and Galinsky et al. (2002) demonstrated counterfactual mindset debiasing effects in lab settings. Transfer to naturalistic conditions — where the counterfactual mindset must be maintained while working with complex existing artifacts over extended time periods — is not established.

**Peer review research covers evaluation bias, not question-formation bias:** Stelmakh et al.'s (2023) null result and Tomkins et al.'s (2017) double-blind findings cover bias at the *evaluation* stage. The question of whether reviewer presuppositions shape the research questions they pose (as distinct from how they score papers) is not addressed in the empirical peer review literature.
