### Synthesis

#### Artifact-Mediated Collaboration: What Makes Artifacts "Mergeable"

The foundational insight runs from Star and Griesemer (1989) through Lee (2007) and Stahl (2006): artifacts serve as the *primary medium* through which people with different mental models, roles, and tool contexts build shared understanding. Star and Griesemer's boundary objects have two necessary properties — plasticity (they can be interpreted differently by each party) and robustness (they maintain enough common identity to coordinate across parties). This tension maps precisely onto the problem of two people working through separate AI intermediaries: each person's AI-assisted output is plastic relative to their own framing, but only becomes useful shared material when the artifact is robust enough to survive re-interpretation by the other person.

Lee (2007) refines this significantly. Where Star and Griesemer treated artifacts as stable facilitators of boundary-crossing, Lee's ethnographic study of museum exhibition design found that artifacts actively *negotiate and restructure* the boundaries themselves. She identified five types: self-explanation artifacts (carry their own interpretive context), inclusion artifacts (lower the threshold for others to participate), compilation artifacts (aggregate contributions without forcing premature synthesis), structuring artifacts (impose an organizing logic on subsequent work), and borrowed artifacts (repurpose existing forms to carry new meaning). For diverge-converge collaboration, the compilation artifact type is particularly relevant: it accumulates independent contributions before convergence without requiring anyone to have resolved the differences. This suggests that artifacts designed for parallel-work scenarios should favor compilation over structuring — they should be easy to append to before they are easy to synthesize from.

Stahl's (2006) treatment of the "joint problem space" in CSCL adds a critical epistemic dimension. Artifacts are not merely containers for pre-formed ideas; they are the site where group-level meaning is *constructed*. His analysis of collaborative knowledge building shows that inscriptions in a shared artifact (threaded contributions, typed thinking categories, modification histories) make the negotiation process visible and traceable. The artifact does not just record what was decided — it records *why* and by *what moves*. This has direct implications for AI-intermediated collaboration: when two people each produce AI-assisted drafts, the artifacts need to carry enough provenance that convergence is a genuine synthesis rather than overwriting. The practical design principle that emerges: artifacts intended for later merge must preserve the reasoning trace, not just the conclusion.

Clark and Brennan's (1991) grounding theory adds a cost dimension. Every act of establishing mutual understanding carries a cost that varies by communication medium. Their principle of least collaborative effort predicts that collaborators will choose grounding strategies proportional to the cost of the medium. For artifact-mediated asynchronous collaboration, this means artifacts bear a heavier cognitive burden: they must carry enough context that the recipient can re-establish common ground without the cheap backchannel cues (nods, "uh-huh") available in face-to-face work. This predicts that artifacts produced through individual AI assistants — where each assistant has a separate context window and separate conversation history — will tend to underspecify the reasoning that led to conclusions, creating expensive re-grounding costs at convergence.

Hutchins's (1995) distributed cognition framework offers a useful reframe: it is not the individual human or the individual AI that is the unit of cognition in collaborative work, but the entire system including people, artifacts, tools, and shared procedures. When two people each bring an AI assistant into collaborative work, the "cognitive system" has grown more complex — four participants rather than two — but the artifacts traversing the system remain the primary site where coordination happens. The ship navigation analogy is apt: a ship's navigation involves coordinating charts, instruments, and multiple crew members, with the chart as the persistent external representation that survives personnel changes. A shared document in collaborative AI-assisted work plays the same role.

#### Diverge-Converge Patterns: How Much Drift Is Productive

The double diamond model (Design Council, 2005; Bánáthy, 1996) is the most widely cited practitioner framework for structured diverge-converge work. Its core insight — that idea generation and idea selection must be separated to avoid premature convergence — has direct roots in Simon's problem-solving research and was formalized by Bánáthy. The transition between phases is what Kaner (1996) calls the "groan zone": the productive discomfort of holding multiple incompatible perspectives simultaneously before forcing convergence. The literature is consistent that this zone cannot be skipped; attempts to converge before sufficient divergence has occurred produce locally optimal but globally suboptimal outcomes.

What the practitioner literature lacks — and what the CSCW research partially fills — is a precise account of how much independent drift is productive before convergence becomes costly. Olson and Olson (2000) provide the most rigorous framework. Their "coupling of work" dimension distinguishes tightly coupled work (high ambiguity, constant negotiation, hard to partition) from loosely coupled work (well-defined interfaces, independently completable subtasks). Their core finding is that loosely coupled work can succeed asynchronously and at distance, while tightly coupled work degrades severely when removed from co-location and synchrony. For diverge-converge collaboration, this translates into a specific prescription: the divergence phase works best when the work has been *pre-partitioned into loosely coupled subtasks*, with convergence reserved for the junctions where tight coupling is unavoidable. Attempting to diverge on tightly coupled work — where each person's independent work depends on real-time information about the other's progress — produces expensive repair work at convergence.

The 2026 arXiv study (2603.12600) on individual vs shared AI configurations provides empirical evidence on this point from a different angle. In the individual-AI condition, students exhibited more off-task behavior (61% vs 36%) and more fragmented interaction. Crucially, individual AI access "distributed support across learners, producing more exploratory and evaluative cycles but also more fragmented interaction patterns, accompanied by increased teacher intervention to manage divergence." This is the experimental analog of independent drift becoming too wide: the productive exploration of individual AI use came at the cost of increased coordination overhead to reconcile divergent outputs. The shared AI condition produced more convergent, aligned behavior with less repair work — but at the cost of reduced individual exploration. Neither configuration is categorically superior; the choice should be made against the collaboration's goals.

Dix and Miles's (1992) early work on version control for asynchronous group work addressed this from a systems perspective: as the number of asynchronous authors increases, version management complexity grows non-linearly. Their formalization of the problem anticipates modern CRDT (Conflict-free Replicated Data Type) approaches, and their core finding holds: clean merges require that the divergent contributions operated on disjoint regions of the artifact. This is the technical formulation of "pre-partitioned into loosely coupled subtasks." Where two people independently edit the same conceptual territory, merge is always an interpretive act requiring human judgment; where they independently develop distinct sections, merge can be mechanical.

#### Collaborative Writing and Knowledge Work with Individual AI Tools

The research on individual AI tools in collaborative contexts is new enough that most published work is from 2024–2026 and much of it identifies gaps rather than solutions.

Dong et al. (2025) conducted the first study specifically examining collaborative document editing where multiple users work alongside multiple AI agents in a shared document. Their finding that no existing intelligent writing tool was designed for "multiple users plus AI" represents a genuine literature gap. Their study found that teams treated AI agent profiles as belonging to their creator — a form of AI-mediated authorship territoriality — but simultaneously used each other's agents as shared resources. This split between ownership norms and usage norms is structurally similar to the tension in open-source software where individual commits are attributed but the artifact is collectively owned. The design implication is that AI-intermediated collaborative writing needs explicit norms around agent portability and artifact stewardship.

The newsroom study (2025, arXiv:2509.10950) presents the most sobering findings for individual-AI collaborative work. In professional journalism contexts, individual GenAI use was widespread but invisible to colleagues. The result was a "suspicion chain" — editors could not tell whether a draft was AI-assisted, forcing extra scrutiny of all work. No formal mechanisms existed to track or coordinate AI usage across team members. Individual accountability concerns caused journalists to keep their AI use private rather than disclosing it. The asymmetric information problem (I know which parts of my work AI helped with; you do not) created coordination overhead that offset efficiency gains. The structural lesson for any diverge-converge workflow: when two people reconverge after independent AI-assisted work, the provenance of artifact sections must be legible to support trustworthy synthesis.

The GenAI mentor configurations study (2026, arXiv:2603.12600) is the clearest direct evidence on the individual-AI collaboration question. Its core finding is that individual AI access produces distributed, exploratory, evaluative cycles — valuable for divergence phases — while shared AI access produces synchronized, convergence-oriented collaboration. This suggests a possible design resolution for collaborative work using individual AI intermediaries: treat the divergence phase as the natural home of individual AI use (each person explores with their own assistant), and treat convergence as the phase where the individual AI sessions should be suspended in favor of a shared artifact synthesis mode. The individual AI assistants are tools for depth within a domain; they are poor tools for spanning across two people's epistemic standpoints simultaneously.

#### The Role of Uncaptured Discussion

The literature is consistent and pointed: informal, unrecorded communication provides value that formal systems systematically fail to provide, and attempts to capture it wholesale are both futile and counterproductive.

Nardi and Whittaker (2002) made the definitive case for face-to-face communication in distributed work. Their core argument, grounded in extensive field study, is that informal conversation is not a degraded form of formal communication but a qualitatively different activity that serves functions formal communication cannot: building trust, conveying nuance, recovering from misunderstandings through cheap repair, and creating the social substrate on which formal collaborative work depends. Face-to-face is not replaceable by richer videoconferencing; it is a different category of interaction.

The organizational knowledge management literature (Smith, 2001; document360.com synthesis) distinguishes tacit from explicit knowledge not as a spectrum but as a categorical difference. Tacit knowledge — embodied skill, contextual judgment, personal relationship awareness — is not merely *uncaptured* explicit knowledge waiting to be transcribed. It is knowledge that exists only in practice and in relationship. Informal conversation is the primary medium through which tacit knowledge influences collaborative work, not by making it explicit but by giving it behavioral expression. A CSCW system that attempts to record all conversation to make tacit knowledge explicit will either fail (the knowledge resists inscription) or succeed in the wrong way (the act of recording changes the communication, eliminating the informality that made it valuable).

The hallway conversations literature (conversational-leadership.net, 2023) adds the practical organizational argument: spontaneous serendipitous encounters drive cross-departmental problem-solving, creative ideation, and morale in ways that scheduled meetings cannot. Organizations that optimize away informal interaction in favor of fully-captured structured communication systematically destroy these serendipitous loops. The design implication for AI-intermediated collaborative work is that the system should not attempt to incorporate or transcribe the informal discussion that happens between two people working together — the side conversations, the clarifying questions, the half-formed thoughts. These should be allowed to remain as ephemeral social glue. The artifact is where decisions are recorded; the conversation is where they are made.

Importantly, the informal communication literature identifies *trust and psychological safety* as preconditions for productive informal exchange. This connects to the newsroom study's finding that individual AI use remained hidden partly for fear of judgment. Where psychological safety is low, the informal discussion that should accompany collaborative work gets suppressed — creating exactly the visibility problems that make convergence difficult. The "some discussion will happen without being captured and that is fine" principle requires a baseline of trust; without it, the undocumented discussion becomes not productive informality but invisible risk.

#### Natural Breakoff Points: When Paired Work Should Split

The pair programming literature offers the most operationally specific guidance on this question. Fowler and Hagemann's (2023) treatment of pair programming identifies a key heuristic: splitting is appropriate when (a) both participants understand the requirements clearly, (b) the tasks to be done are largely prescriptive, and (c) the pair has jointly planned the partition. The decision criterion is epistemic — once both people know enough that independent work will not drift into incompatibility, splitting is efficient. The inverse is equally instructive: when requirements are ambiguous or the approach is unclear, pairing is valuable precisely because it manages the interpretive negotiation in real time.

The Scrum literature's "converge-diverge pattern for refinement" (scrum.org) provides a complementary heuristic from agile practice: converge first to establish shared understanding of what must be built, then diverge to do the building independently, then converge again to integrate. The natural breakoff point is immediately after the shared planning that produces shared understanding — not during exploration, and not during integration.

The newsroom study's "suspicion chain" problem adds a negative signal for the breakoff decision: if the divergent work will produce outputs that the other person cannot evaluate without provenance information, splitting is premature. The breakoff point should be deferred until the artifact interface is well-defined enough that independently produced sections can be unambiguously attributed and evaluated. This is the same as Olson and Olson's loose coupling criterion, reframed: split when the interface between your respective work streams is clear enough to be independently executed without real-time coordination.

The EchoMind study (Chen et al., 2025) offers a different perspective on the breakoff problem. EchoMind's shared AI facilitator helped clarify discussion objectives and trace knowledge pathways in real-time group discussion. The implication is that breakoff is itself a convergent act — it requires shared understanding of what each person will independently work on. The tools that support this shared understanding (discussion maps, explicit issue structures, visible knowledge organization) are therefore preconditions for a clean split, not afterthoughts to the split.

### Key Findings

- **Artifacts for diverge-converge collaboration should be compilation-first, not structuring-first.** Lee (2007) shows that artifacts designed to accumulate contributions before synthesizing them are better suited to parallel work than artifacts that impose structure prematurely. Structure is a convergence operation; premature structuring defeats productive divergence.

- **Individual AI access promotes exploration; shared AI access promotes alignment.** The 2026 arXiv study on GenAI mentor configurations found that individual AI use produces more evaluative, divergent cycles while shared AI use produces more synchronized, convergent behavior. The implication for workflow design: individual AI intermediaries belong in the divergence phase; convergence requires a shared artifact mode.

- **Common ground in artifact-mediated asynchronous work must be over-specified relative to synchronous work.** Clark and Brennan (1991) show that the cost of grounding is medium-dependent. When AI assistants produce outputs without exposing their reasoning, re-establishing common ground at convergence becomes expensive. Artifacts must carry reasoning provenance, not just conclusions.

- **The individual AI "suspicion chain" is a real coordination failure mode in professional knowledge work.** The newsroom study (2025) found that invisible individual AI use creates asymmetric information that forces extra scrutiny, disproportionately burdens integrators, and suppresses disclosure through fear of judgment. Provenance transparency is not optional hygiene — it is load-bearing infrastructure for collaborative convergence.

- **Loosely coupled work can diverge; tightly coupled work cannot.** Olson and Olson (2000) establish that the precondition for successful asynchronous parallel work is that the work has been pre-partitioned at stable interfaces. Splitting before the partition is established produces expensive repair at convergence.

- **Natural breakoff points require shared understanding as a precondition, not a postcondition.** Both the pair programming literature (Fowler, 2023) and the EchoMind study (Chen et al., 2025) indicate that a clean split into independent streams requires that both people already share an understanding of what the split produces. The planning conversation is load-bearing; skipping it does not save time.

- **Informal uncaptured communication is not a deficiency of collaborative systems — it is a feature.** Nardi and Whittaker (2002), the tacit knowledge literature, and the organizational informal communication research converge on the view that informal, unrecorded conversation provides irreplaceable functions: trust-building, tacit knowledge expression, cheap repair, and creative serendipity. Systems should support rather than replace or capture this layer.

- **The key unsolved design problem is asymmetric AI mediation.** Where current CSCW infrastructure assumes roughly symmetric tooling (both people use the same editor, the same version control system), individual AI intermediaries introduce asymmetric context: each person's AI has a different conversation history, a different framing of the task, and potentially a different set of assumptions baked into its outputs. No existing CSCW system was designed for this configuration. The closest analogues — the newsroom study and the shared-vs-individual AI classroom study — both identify the coordination overhead as significant but uninvestigated at depth.

### Limitations

**Coverage gaps by angle:**

- *Diverge-converge timing and productive drift:* The quantitative question — how many hours of independent work before convergence overhead exceeds divergence benefit — has no direct answer in the literature. The CSCW literature addresses coupling topology (tight vs loose) but not timing or drift magnitude as a continuous variable.

- *Individual AI intermediaries in collaborative knowledge work:* The literature here is genuinely nascent. Most relevant work published before 2024 predates the widespread use of LLM assistants. The 2025–2026 studies found are predominantly in educational (classroom) or media (journalism) settings; there is no equivalent study of professional knowledge workers (e.g., researchers, analysts, engineers) each using their own LLM to co-produce research or design documents.

- *Merge quality as an outcome variable:* The collaborative writing literature addresses merge technically (CRDT, operational transformation) and socially (authorship norms, ownership) but not epistemically — whether merged artifacts actually contain better knowledge than un-merged independent outputs. The assumption that convergence produces better outcomes than continued parallel work is largely unexamined.

- *Informal communication under AI mediation:* The literature on informal communication values it in human-to-human terms. There is no equivalent literature on what happens to informal communication when each participant's working context is substantially shaped by private AI conversations. The question of whether AI-mediated private work changes the character of the subsequent informal discussion between people is entirely open.

- *Boundary objects and AI-generated artifacts:* Star and Griesemer's boundary object theory and Lee's boundary negotiating artifacts theory both assume human agents as the parties being coordinated. Whether AI-generated text inherits the properties of a boundary object — plastic yet robust, carrying enough interpretive frame to be reused across different contexts — is an open question with no literature addressing it directly.
