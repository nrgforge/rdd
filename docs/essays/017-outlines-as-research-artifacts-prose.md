# Outlines as Research Artifacts: A Susceptibility-Surface Argument

*2026-05-11*

## Abstract

RDD Cycle 018 investigated whether the RESEARCH phase's primary artifact should shift from the prose essay form instantiated sixteen times in the corpus to a structured outline carrying inline citations, comparing that hypothesis against three live alternatives: hybrid prose-with-structured-sections, annotated bibliography with synthesis notes, and audit-extended prose (Q1). It paired this question with a phase-by-phase audit of the methodology against the CRESS principles for context engineering (Gorman 2026a; Q2), tested whether the two threads co-illuminated (Q3), and ran an empirical corpus check on whether downstream phases substantively consume research-essay content (Q4). The investigation combined web fetch on CRESS source material, two thematic lit-reviews (AI-prose-critique landscape; minimal-surface artifact design across disciplines), a single validation spike comparing argument-audit fidelity on the same essay expressed in both forms, and methodology synthesis. The reframing finding is that the outline move is not principally justified as a response to the AI-prose critique — that critique applies categorically and audit-attestation does not resolve it (Cheong et al. 2025; Gallegos et al. 2026; Agarwal et al. 2025) — but rather by three independently-supported considerations: reduced surface for framing-adoption sycophancy, alignment with the CRESS Empirical and Small principles, and direct preservation of the structured content that downstream RDD phases already consume.

## 1. Motivation: the AI-prose-critique landscape and a content-surface hypothesis

The cycle was prompted by the user's growing engagement with mid-2026 commentary on AI-generated prose, including the CRESS framework (Gorman 2026a) and a wider discourse spanning peer-reviewed empirical work, journalism research, and practitioner essays. The user articulated a forming hypothesis in the constraint-removal step (ADR-082): a well-organized outline that provides sufficient information to be audited — and nothing else — should be *minimally susceptible* relative to a prose essay covering the same material. Susceptibility, in the framing the user offered, has two components: susceptibility to sycophancy and framing-adoption (the Cycle 013 line of work) and susceptibility to the AI-prose critique (the Cycle 018 entry concern). Less content surface, the hypothesis runs, leaves fewer sites at which either susceptibility can take hold.

The AI-prose-critique landscape (Loop B) decomposes into three complaint families of unequal force. Stylistic complaints — tone homogenization, register flattening, cultural erasure — are most-cited in mainstream discourse and rest on measurable findings: Agarwal et al. (CHI 2025) report that a classifier distinguishing Indian from American writing drops from 90.6% to 83.5% accuracy when participants write with AI assistance, scoped to a single cross-cultural writing study. Epistemic complaints are analytically more serious: Wright et al. (arXiv October 2025) demonstrate empirically across 155 topics and 12 countries that 27 LLMs tested are less epistemically diverse than basic web search; Dolezal et al. (arXiv April 2026) measure 33% higher semantic similarity in AI-generated web content at internet scale. Structural complaints appear in Gorman's CRESS framework and in the knowledge-collapse literature without being elaborated as a prose-vs-structured-form argument.

A structural gap in the literature reframes the cycle's central question: *no source surveyed distinguishes audited from unaudited AI prose*, which means the critique applies as written to both. Across all twelve sources reviewed, the critique treats "AI-generated content" as a category defined by generative process, not downstream verification. This is an absence-of-distinction across the surveyed corpus rather than positive evidence that audit-attestation has been tested and failed. Cheong et al. (CHIWORK 2025) find a persistent transparency penalty for disclosed AI use that survives attestation language like "reviewed by a human." Gallegos et al. (PNAS Nexus 2026) report that AI labeling produces no significant reduction in persuasive impact (P=0.91 comparing labeled to unlabeled content), scoped to controlled experimental conditions on persuasive writing rather than to long-form research artifacts. The stylistic and epistemic complaints "survive audit entirely" because they are distribution-level properties of the generation process, unaffected by document-level verification (Wright et al. 2025; Dolezal et al. 2026).

The implication is reorienting. The outline move *cannot* be primarily justified as a response to the AI-prose critique, because that critique applies whether the artifact is audited or not. Three other justifications survive scrutiny and become load-bearing: (a) a theoretically motivated but empirically untested reduction in surface area for sycophantic framing-adoption — less connective synthesis is hypothesized to leave fewer invisible premises (Cheng et al. ELEPHANT 2025; Cheng et al. Science 2026), but Loop B and Loop C both note explicitly that no 2025–2026 study directly tests prose vs. structured output as a susceptibility variable; (b) compatibility with CRESS-Empirical, since claims become individually attributable and each node carries its source (Gorman 2026b); and (c) reduced rhetorical-vs-logical conflation, an argument that emerges from the Loop C audit-extended-prose comparison and is treated in §3. These are sycophancy-resistance and epistemic-grounding considerations, not critique-response considerations. The three considerations have different epistemic statuses: (a) is theoretical inference; (b) draws on an explicit finding articulated in the CRESS literature; (c) is the essay's synthesis combining a Loop C observation (rhetorical and logical force are co-present in prose and not independently separable) with an RDD-context inference about AI-agent-produced headed prose. The cycle's central thesis stands on the first set rather than the second.

An alternative framing the evidence equally supports — and which this essay does not take — would treat the outline move as primarily a critique-response gesture and rely on the broad cultural force of the AI-prose discourse rather than its empirical content. That framing would generate stronger advocacy text but would be unsupported by the audited/unaudited finding above. The cycle's research-record stance prefers the narrower, evidence-grounded set of justifications.

## 2. CRESS and the phase-by-phase audit

The CRESS principles, articulated by Jason Gorman on the Codemanship blog (Gorman 2026a, dated 2026-05-04), describe properties of context that LLM coding tasks should satisfy: **C**urrent (up-to-date), **R**efutable (knowable when output diverges from intent), **E**mpirical (drawn from observed reality, not model output), **S**mall (minimum necessary information), and **S**pecific (narrowly scoped to a single problem). Gorman bases the framework on "three years of research and experimentation" via closed-loop experiments without published academic citations, and explicitly invites skepticism: "you shouldn't take my word for it. Test them for yourself." The scope of the framework as published is coding-task context engineering. A scope-transfer question therefore precedes its application to RDD's research artifacts: do the principles transfer when the "context" being engineered is a methodology artifact rather than a coding prompt?

The affirmative case for transfer rests on what CRESS principles describe at the abstraction level: properties of information-bearing context fed to LLM agents. RDD's phase artifacts (research essays, ADRs, system-design documents) function as context fed to downstream LLM agents (DECIDE, ARCHITECT, BUILD) within the methodology's own pipeline. The scope transfer is argued by analogy — context for coding tasks and context for methodology-internal agent consumption share the relevant property of being information that an LLM consumes to produce downstream output. The analogy is not validated by Gorman directly; it is the cycle's working hypothesis. A reader skeptical of the transfer can read the CRESS-thread findings as conditional on accepting that analogy.

Loop D maps each of RDD's nine phases (RESEARCH, DISCOVER, MODEL, DECIDE, ARCHITECT, BUILD, PLAY, SYNTHESIZE) against each CRESS principle using a four-level scoring (MAXIMAL/STRONG/PARTIAL/WEAK) drawn from each phase's documented mechanisms. The matrix surfaces three cross-cutting observations relevant to the artifact-form question.

First, **RESEARCH is the methodology's most consequential accidental S-Small divergence point**. DISCOVER and MODEL also score WEAK on S-Small in the matrix (accumulating across cycles, growing monotonically), but the principled-vs-accidental classification developed below distinguishes those phases (where accretion is principled or mixed) from RESEARCH, whose divergence is fully accidental and remediable by form change. Every other phase has either explicit size discipline (BUILD's tidying/YAGNI), structural constraints (DECIDE's per-ADR scope; ARCHITECT's per-module fitness), or convention-bounded brevity (PLAY's brief field notes; SYNTHESIZE's outline form). RESEARCH alone produces 5,000+ word artifacts as standard practice without the structural rationale the other accumulative phases carry. Second, `/rdd-synthesize` already operationalizes the proposed pattern: agent produces an outline, human writes the essay outside the pipeline. SYNTHESIZE scores STRONG on S-Small specifically because of this design choice. Third, E-Empirical and R-Refutable are *maximally* satisfied in BUILD (tests, compiler, type-checker as non-sycophantic feedback systems); other phases extend these principles via audit infrastructure — citation, argument, and framing audits in RESEARCH and SYNTHESIZE; conformance audit in MODEL; argument audit in DECIDE.

The principled-vs-accidental classification reveals that the RESEARCH essay-form divergence is *accidental*, not principled. No principled rationale for essay form over outline form appears in the artifact trail. SYNTHESIZE successfully operates with outline form. The user's practical experience that research essays are "not human-read" confirms the divergence has not been load-bearing for a human readership it lacks. By contrast, ADR variable length (per-decision scope) is principled, and the ARCHITECT system-design size issue was already classified as partial-accidental and remediated by ADR-084's Pattern B split. The RESEARCH essay length, in this classification, is a default that has not been re-examined rather than a deliberate trade-off.

The phase-by-phase audit returns to Q3. The cycle treated the two threads (artifact-form question; CRESS audit) as potentially convergent, with the convergence itself being a finding. The audit identifies the same phase from a principled direction that Cycle 018 identifies from a practical direction. The convergence is not coincidental: it is two independent paths reaching the same diagnosis. Thread 1 alone could be dismissed as user preference (the user doesn't like long essays); Thread 2 alone could be dismissed as CRESS-overreach (CRESS is scoped to coding context). Together, an independent principle-driven framework and an independent practice-driven observation reach the same diagnostic on the same phase. The convergence supports stronger conclusions than either thread would license alone — though the cycle-as-instance caveat in §8 still applies.

## 3. Prior art comparanda: structured medical abstracts, Cochrane, and ARA

Loop C surveyed eight prior-art categories under the question "where has minimum-sufficient-content with audit-attached been treated as a deliberate design pattern, and what can RDD learn from those experiences?" Each category was evaluated against three criteria: audit-fidelity, susceptibility-surface-minimization, and downstream-feed-forward preservation. The three most informative comparanda for the RDD context are structured medical abstracts, the Cochrane systematic-review hybrid, and the Agent-Native Research Artifact (ARA) preprint.

Structured medical abstracts (Haynes et al. 1990; Hartley 2004; Nakayama et al. 2005; Docherty & Smith 1999; PRISMA 2020) provide the clearest direct empirical support for susceptibility-surface-minimization through structural constraint. The Haynes et al. format — adopted by BMJ, JAMA, and Annals of Internal Medicine — requires explicit headings: Objective, Design, Setting, Patients, Interventions, Main Outcome Measures, Results, Conclusions. Hartley (2004) reports that structured abstracts show advantages in content, readability, recall, and retrieval relative to unstructured ones, scoped to medical journal abstracts indexed in MEDLINE; the specific composite phrasing varies across the surrounding structured-abstracts literature and is paraphrased here rather than presented as a verbatim quote. Docherty & Smith (1999) extend the analysis to Discussion sections and argue that mandatory subheadings prevent polemic by separating each analytical function into a designated section and force explicit treatment of limitations. The mechanism is direct: structure suppresses rhetorical packaging of vague claims because the field demands specificity at named sites.

The Cochrane systematic-review hybrid (Cochrane Handbook 2022; PRISMA 2020) emerges as the strongest overall comparandum across all three criteria. It layers audits: protocol pre-registration in PROSPERO; PRISMA reporting checklist; mandatory abstract headings; and GRADE evidence-quality ratings that attach to individual finding claims before synthesis prose is written. The hybrid preserves the synthesis depth that pure structured-abstract form cannot carry, while maintaining structural accountability at multiple points. Its limitation for RDD is scale: producing a Cochrane-quality review is a multi-year project, incompatible with RDD's cycle rhythm. The most transferable elements are conceptual: protocol pre-registration as pre-commitment audit; GRADE-like external evidence-quality ratings on individual claims before synthesis.

The Agent-Native Research Artifact (Liu et al. 2026, arXiv preprint) emerged as an unexpected limiting case. Liu et al. propose replacing prose papers with a four-layer machine-traversable structure (scientific logic, executable code, exploration graph, evidence-grounded claims) carrying a three-level "ARA Seal" verification credential. In empirical testing on a particular benchmark set in a single unreviewed preprint, **QA accuracy for downstream AI agents improved from 72.4% to 93.7% versus prose papers**; reproduction success improved from 57.4% to 64.4%. The figures are benchmark-dependent, and the analogy from the ARA's fully typed machine-traversable infrastructure to RDD's outline-with-inline-citations is qualified — the ARA result is directional evidence for structured claim-evidence separation aiding downstream agent consumption, not a direct measurement of the modest structural move RDD can make with existing infrastructure. This is the strongest empirical signal in the literature surveyed for outline-form artifacts serving downstream AI agents specifically. The scope qualifications: it is a single April 2026 preprint, not peer-reviewed; the infrastructure (ARA Compiler, Live Research Manager) does not exist in RDD's stack; the measurement is over a particular benchmark set rather than a general-purpose corpus.

The other Loop C categories rule themselves out or place subordinate to the three above. Pre-registration (Veldkamp et al. 2020; Chambers et al. 2022) is strong on audit-fidelity but architecturally mismatched: single-study pre-commitment, not multi-cycle synthesis. Formal specifications (TLA+, DITA; Lamport 2002; Newcombe et al. 2015) achieve maximal audit-fidelity within a bounded expressibility domain (system invariants) that does not include argument-quality or synthesis-judgment. ISDA contract drafting demonstrates the invariant-base + parameterized-elections architecture but requires that the option space be pre-enumerable, which is impossible for open-ended research synthesis. The annotated bibliography achieves per-citation audit-fidelity but lacks cross-source synthesis; outline-with-inline-citations is structurally superior because it combines atomic claim accountability with cross-source synthesis. Outline-vs-prose pedagogy (Booth, Colomb & Williams 2008; Dunleavy 2014; Sword 2012) treats outline as scaffolding rather than deliverable, except for Dunleavy's "storyboarding research" position; the deliverable case must therefore be made from downstream-consumer properties rather than from pedagogical convention.

A complication arises from the sycophancy literature that bears on outline form's susceptibility-reduction claim. Kim et al. (2025) report that citation-based rebuttals trigger the highest rate of regressive sycophancy in LLMs, as models over-weight authoritative-sounding argument structure even when conclusions contradict ground truth. The naive reading would be that a highly structured outline (with explicit CLAIM, EVIDENCE, WARRANT tags) could itself induce the maximum sycophancy by presenting an authoritative-looking framework. Loop C resolves the tension via an input/output distinction: Kim et al.'s effect operates at the *input* side (how a downstream agent receives an authoritative argument structure), while the artifact-design question the cycle pursues is about the *output* side (what surface area exists for framing adoption during artifact generation). Whether outline-structured inputs to downstream RDD agents (DECIDE, ARCHITECT) trigger Kim et al.-type over-weighting is an open empirical question the cycle does not resolve.

The Loop C observation against the strongest within-form competitor — audit-extended prose — is that rhetorical force and logical force are co-present in prose and not independently separable by structural headings: a well-written prose section under a mandatory heading can still embed unwarranted inferences in fluent prose. The RDD-context inference, built on that observation, is that **framing adoption can occur within a well-headed section without detection** when the artifact is produced by an AI agent whose prose discipline is the variable in question. Docherty & Smith's mandatory-headings-within-prose approach is a genuine competitor when prose discipline is high. In RDD's context, where the artifact is produced by an AI agent whose prose discipline is precisely the variable in question, the agent cannot be assumed to produce the well-structured, non-sycophantic prose that would make audit-extended prose effective. The observation is Loop C's; the inference combining that observation with the RDD-context AI-agent qualifier is this cycle's synthesis. The combined argument bears the load of the comparative case against simply "fixing the audit and keeping prose." It is the strongest single argument the Loop C survey returned for moving away from prose form rather than further investing in prose-audit machinery.

## 4. The audit-fidelity question: Spike S1

The primary falsifier the user identified for Q1 was audit fidelity: outlines might lose audit equivalence relative to prose. The user added an important qualification — the implication, if so, was that the *argument trail itself* would need to do more work, with the argument audit as the strengthening site. The question was consequential, amenable to tangible answer, and not resolvable by lit-review alone. Cycle 018 ran a single validation spike (ADR-087 decision: RUN) to produce direct evidence.

The spike's method: Subagent 1 produced a structural outline derivation of Essay 016 ("Initial Handoff and the Audience at the Margin") preserving argument-structure content — claims, warrants, evidence, scope qualifications, synthesis statements, calibration moves, meta-moves — in fresh context with no exposure to the prose audit. The outline ran to 516 lines (hierarchical-bullet line expansion) versus 188 prose lines. Subagent 2 (argument-auditor specialist, fresh context) ran a standard two-section audit (argument + framing) on the outline plus an additional section capturing outline-form audit experience. The comparison was against the prior argument-audit-017.md prose audit of the same essay.

The comparison summary indicates approximate audit-equivalence with directional gains and losses. The prose audit mapped 8 argument chains and 8 total issues (1 P1, 4 P2, 3 P3); the outline audit mapped 9 chains and 10 issues (1 P1, 4 P2, 5 P3). All eight prose issues transferred to the outline audit at equivalent or higher strength. One of those eight — a Decker citation bibliographic phantom — was *resolved* by the outline form itself when the citation node was made structurally explicit (so the resolution applies within the transferred eight, not as a separate item). The outline audit also surfaced two additional framing findings without prose equivalents (a missing Nguyen et al. 2025 citation, and an underweighted prior-familiarity intervention). The outline's ten-issue total comprises the eight transferred from prose plus those two new framing findings. The issue-count comparison is a *gain* story for outline form: more issues surfaced overall, one prose issue resolved by form change. The auditor's net qualitative assessment is a separate *loss* story focused on a specific dimension: outline form preserves approximately 80–85% of argument-audit fidelity relative to prose, with the loss concentrated in rhetorical-confidence calibration (differential-confidence signals that prose hedging carries implicitly but outline bullets do not). Both stories are true simultaneously — the issue-count gains and the calibration loss measure different things. The auditor calibrated the 80–85% figure explicitly: it is a qualitative assessment based on issue-by-issue comparison of one essay's two forms, not a statistical measurement over many essays; it is directionally informative, not precise; future cycles may calibrate further.

The qualitative findings beneath the percentage are more informative than the percentage itself. Outline form *gained* on three dimensions: (a) structural ordering of scope-qualification relative to claim was more visible — the P1 issue surfaced as a structural choice in outline form whereas it had been buried in paragraph flow in prose; (b) inferential chains were easier to map because each CLAIM bullet names a proposition and each EVIDENCE/SUPPORT bullet names its warrant; (c) internal consistency issues between sections were more visible, including a framing tension between §3's "primary mechanism" framing and §4.5's implicit prior-familiarity-as-dominant claim. Outline form *lost* on three dimensions: (a) rhetorical confidence calibration was harder to assess because in prose, hedging language ("suggests" vs. "establishes") signals differential confidence, while in outline form all CLAIM bullets carry the same visual weight regardless of underlying confidence level; (b) argumentative transitions and direction-of-travel between sections became invisible — prose has a rhetorical arc the auditor reads as logical movement, while outline collapses this to heading sequence; (c) implicit assumptions in prose framing were harder to surface from bullet labels.

The critical inference for Q1's sub-question: the argument-audit does **not** require extension to handle outline form. The P1 issue surfaced equivalently in both forms. The 80–85% preservation includes losses (calibration) that are partially offset by gains (structural visibility, framing). The differential-confidence-calibration loss is a real fidelity concern but does not require audit-extension. It requires *outline-production discipline* that uses different mechanisms — explicit CONFIDENCE-LEVEL tags, SCOPE-QUALIFICATION sub-bullets, CALIBRATION bullets — to communicate what prose hedging communicates implicitly. The falsifier sub-question premise ("outline needs stronger argument audit") is *not* established. The strengthening site is the outline-production discipline, not the audit machinery. Task R1 (prose-contribution analysis of Essays 013, 014, 016) reached the same conclusion via a different route: prose carries argument-structure content (transferable to outline form without semantic loss) and persuasive-flow content (not transferable, and not the same as argument structure); the audit's argument-trail parsing should function on outline form provided the outline-production discipline carries synthesis explicitly.

## 5. Citation-audit comparison: the cycle's own self-evidence

The cycle dogfooded its central question by producing both prose and outline forms of this research artifact in context-isolated subagents. Both forms were then submitted to independent citation-audit subagents, also run in isolated context, producing two separate audit reports (`citation-audit-018-prose.md` and `citation-audit-018-outline.md`). The audits' findings are themselves Cycle 018 data on the prose-vs-outline susceptibility question — an empirical signal the cycle produced about itself by the act of running both forms through the same audit machinery.

The empirical comparison is asymmetric. The prose-form audit identified twelve numbered issue entries across the three severity tiers — four P1 (must-fix) errors, seven P2 (should-fix) issues, and nine P3 (consider) items of which two were marked confirmed clean and seven were genuine P3 recommendations. The outline-form audit identified nine total citation issues — two P1 errors, two P2 issues, and five P3 issues. Two of the P1 errors appeared in both forms and trace to the underlying research log: a first-author error misattributing arXiv:2604.24658 to "Rahimi" rather than Jiachen Liu, and a misattribution of the BMJ paper at PMC1115625 to "Nakayama et al. (2004)" rather than to Docherty & Smith (1999). These shared errors are research-log-inherited; they show that the form change does not eliminate upstream citation-discipline problems that originate in the lit-review and synthesis loops feeding both forms.

The three P1 errors unique to the prose form bear closer attention. The first was a "Ripple et al. (2005)" attribution for a quote ("better content, readability, recall and retrieval") whose actual source is Hartley (2004), JMLA 92(3):368–371 — a reference that did not appear in the prose-form bibliography at all. The second was an author-initials error in the ELEPHANT entry (Yu T./Lee J. instead of Yu S./Lee C.). The third was a publication-year error placing the Cheng et al. Science paper in 2025 rather than 2026, along with a related author-order discrepancy. None of the three was inherited from the outline form, and none was inherited from the source research log in the same shape. The outline form's audit reported zero unique-to-outline P1 errors: the outline honestly self-flagged the only two uncertain citations it carried (Cheng et al. 2026 "content-selection mechanism" and Tsui et al. 2025), and the audit then resolved those flags — the first by confirming no such paper exists (removed from corpus), the second by identifying the correct single-author single-paper reference (Tsui 2025, arXiv:2507.02778).

The outline-form citation-auditor recorded a methodology observation directly addressing the audit-fidelity question Cycle 018 asks. The auditor identified four gains in citation auditability when working with outline form: every claim has an explicit provenance node, making claim-source alignment structurally unambiguous; orphan references surface immediately under grep; scope qualifications are structurally separated from claims; and calibration markers are explicit per bullet. One loss in auditability partially offsets the gains without reversing the net direction: quote accuracy is harder to verify because the boundary between paraphrase and direct quotation is less visible in bullet form than in prose, where block quotes and quoted phrases are typographically distinguished. The auditor's net assessment, verbatim: "Outline form increases citation auditability overall. The gains in claim-source separation and scope-qualification visibility outweigh the loss in quote-boundary clarity."

Several calibrations apply to the comparison. The evidence base is single-cycle and small — one prose form, one outline form, two citation audits — and the comparison is qualitative rather than statistical. The shared P1 errors are research-log-inherited and demonstrate that form change does not address upstream citation discipline. The unique-to-prose P1 errors suggest a different mechanism: the synthesizing-into-narrative step in prose production appears to require re-attribution moves (rewording bibliographic entries, restating attributions in flowing text) that the bullet form does not require to the same degree. Re-attribution at synthesis time creates opportunities for compounding or introducing errors that may not have been present in the source materials. Whether this mechanism generalizes across multiple cycles, or is specific to the particular sources Cycle 018 drew on, is an open empirical question that further cycles can address.

The implication for the cycle's central argument is that the dogfooded comparison supports — with explicit cycle-as-instance scope (ADR-087 §4) — the hypothesis that outline form has fewer citation errors AND increases citation auditability. The finding is research-not-validation: it is one cycle's data on its own central question, captured before subsequent work could obscure or lose it. The meta-quality of the finding is itself a methodology observation: producing both forms of the same artifact and submitting them to isolated audits generates evidence about the artifact-form choice that no single-form cycle could produce. The comparison machinery is reproducible across future cycles, which provides the path to whatever further calibration the question can support.

## 6. Downstream feed-forward: the corpus consumption check

Q4 introduced an empirical test the cycle could run cheaply: examine the existing 16-essay corpus and the downstream phase artifacts produced from it, characterizing whether downstream phases actually consume essay content substantively or merely refer to essays bibliographically. The minimal-surface argument relies on agent-facing consumption preserving feed-forward value; if downstream artifacts consume prose flow substantively, the minimal-surface hypothesis would need qualification for an agent-facing audience.

Task R2 used `grep` across the downstream phase artifacts in `docs/` and surfaced three distinct consumption modes. **Bibliographic provenance** (low information density) appears in roadmap.md (12 references), ORIENTATION.md (4), field-guide.md (2), and domain-model.md (cycle tags) — "Derived from Essay N" or "Background: Essay N" patterns establishing attribution chains. **Section-keyed warrants** (medium density) appear in system-design.agents.md, where every architectural driver has a Provenance line citing essay sections (e.g., "Essay 014 §6") as warrant for architectural decisions. **Substantive content reuse** (high density) appears in ADRs (96 essay references) and product-discovery.md (several substantive references), where downstream artifacts quote specific claims, framework names, synthesis statements, and concept introductions. The method scope is worth flagging: `grep -rEn 'Essay [0-9]+|essays/[0-9]+'` surfaces explicit essay-numbered references; implicit content reuse — where downstream artifacts adopt a framework concept or synthesis statement from an essay without citing it by essay number — is not captured by this method. The 96-reference figure is therefore a floor on substantive consumption, not a ceiling, and the three-mode taxonomy is grounded in explicit-citation patterns rather than the full consumption relationship.

The substantive-reuse mode decomposes into three element types: named claims ("Essay 002 identifies a structural blind spot"), framework introductions (Einstellung effect, AI Smell Taxonomy, Diátaxis, history pseudostate), and synthesis statements (e.g., ADR-014 cites Essay 003 §5's economic context *and* a reflection in 003 reflections §3 that sharpened the framing). All three element types are carriable in outline form *if the outline is structured to include them explicitly* as named bullets, framework anchors, and synthesis bullets. The risk surfaced by R2 is that an outline *might tend* to elide synthesis bullets in favor of bare claim-listing — but this is a discipline-of-production question for the outline form, not an inherent property of outline form.

One subtle observation R2 surfaced bears flagging: ADR-014 cites both Essay 003 §5 and a reflection in 003 reflections §3 that sharpened the framing. Reflections are inherently narrative ("I came to see X as Y") — first-person prose capturing conversational frame-shift. If the essay shifts to outline form, reflection content may be less natural to express in outline form than essay content. The outline migration question may not extend cleanly to reflections; the cycle's findings are scoped to the essay form, not to the reflections file.

The implication for Q4: downstream phases consume essays substantively (mode 3 is non-trivial — 96 ADR references is substantial), but **what they consume is structured content (claims, frameworks, synthesis statements), not prose flow**. This substantiates the minimal-surface hypothesis with a qualification: minimal-surface ≠ minimum-content. CRESS's S-Small specifies "no redundant background, irrelevant history, or over-verbose explanations" — synthesis statements are not redundant background. The outline must carry synthesis statements as explicit named bullets to preserve feed-forward value; bare claim-listing would degrade downstream consumption. The empirical corpus check supports the outline move provided outline-production discipline carries the structural content the downstream consumes.

## 7. Co-illumination and the central reframing

Q3 asked whether the two threads — artifact-form (Q1) and CRESS audit (Q2) — co-illuminated or were parallel investigations. The cycle treated convergence (or its absence) as itself a finding. The Loop D matrix and Loop A scope-transfer analysis return a strong answer: the threads **strongly co-illuminate**. CRESS S-Small bears directly on the artifact-form question; CRESS E-Empirical bears directly on the AI-prose-critique question; and the CRESS phase-by-phase audit independently identifies the RESEARCH essay as the only phase whose S-Small divergence is fully accidental and remediable by form change — distinct from DISCOVER, which Loop D classifies as mixed (the per-cycle update mode addresses part of DISCOVER's divergence, while MODEL's accretion is principled), and reaching the same diagnostic that Cycle 018's user-stated motivation reaches from practical experience.

The convergence is not coincidental. It is two independent paths reaching the same diagnosis. Thread 1 (user experience of essays as unread) is a practice-driven observation. Thread 2 (CRESS S-Small flagging a phase divergence) is a principle-driven framework finding. The cycle's central reframing follows from holding both: the outline move is justified not by the AI-prose-critique discourse (which applies categorically and is not resolved by audit-attestation, per Loop B), but by the joint force of three considerations — sycophancy-resistance via reduced framing-adoption surface (theoretically motivated but empirically untested, per Loop B and Loop C), CRESS-Empirical-and-Small alignment, and downstream-AI-consumption properties.

The synthesize-pattern-extension analogy carries weight in this reframing. SYNTHESIZE already operationalizes the proposed pattern (agent produces outline; human writes essay outside the pipeline) and scores STRONG on S-Small precisely because of this design choice. The principled-vs-accidental question for RESEARCH becomes pointed: why does RESEARCH diverge from SYNTHESIZE on this specific design choice? No principled rationale appears in the artifact trail. The divergence appears to be a historical accident — RESEARCH adopted essay form before SYNTHESIZE existed; the pattern SYNTHESIZE later operationalized was not retroactively applied upstream. Per Loop A, however, an audience-mismatch caveat survives the analogy: SYNTHESIZE feeds a human writer; RESEARCH feeds downstream agents. Minimal-surface arguments for human-facing artifacts do not automatically transfer to agent-facing ones. The Q4 corpus check (R2) addresses this directly and supports the transfer for RDD's specific downstream consumption pattern, with the discipline qualification stated above.

The Loop C observation — that rhetorical force and logical force are co-present in prose and not independently separable by structural headings — combined with the RDD-context inference that **framing adoption can therefore occur within a well-headed section without detection** when the producer is an AI agent whose prose discipline is the variable in question, bears the structural load of the comparative case against staying with prose. Docherty & Smith's structured-discussion approach is the strongest within-prose competitor; it succeeds when prose discipline is high; RDD's research artifact is produced by an AI agent whose prose discipline is precisely the variable in question. The mandatory-headings approach within prose addresses dimension (a) of "minimally susceptible" (sycophancy via framing-adoption) imperfectly and does not address dimension (b) (AI-prose-critique susceptibility) at all. The outline form addresses both: (a) via the discipline of explicit-claim-and-warrant outline production; (b) by sidestepping prose form entirely.

## 8. Implications, scope-of-claim, and open questions

The cycle's findings carry implications for downstream phases without recommending specific methodology changes (the latter is DECIDE's job). The most immediate implication is for RESEARCH itself: if the RESEARCH artifact form changes, the outline-production discipline becomes the load-bearing site. The outline must carry synthesis bullets, scope-qualification bullets, calibration bullets, framework references, and named meta-moves (PROVENANCE CORRECTION, SCOPE QUALIFICATION). A disciplined outline form is argument-equivalent to prose for audit purposes; an undisciplined outline form (bare claim-listing without warrants or synthesis) is argument-inferior to prose. The strengthening site is production discipline rather than audit extension.

For DISCOVER, MODEL, DECIDE, and ARCHITECT, the corpus-consumption findings (R2) are the most relevant: these phases consume claims, frameworks, and synthesis statements substantively. Any RESEARCH artifact-form change must preserve these consumption units. The 96 ADR references to essays are an empirical floor: a form that loses what those references actually carry would degrade downstream artifact quality. BUILD is largely insulated from the change since BUILD operates against scenarios, interaction specs, and the codebase rather than against essays directly. PLAY and SYNTHESIZE are unaffected by the RESEARCH form change in their own operation; SYNTHESIZE already uses outline form and may benefit from upstream alignment.

Scope-of-claim qualifications accumulate at several points. The Spike S1 80–85% audit-fidelity figure is the qualitative assessment of one auditor comparing one essay's two forms; it is directionally informative, not statistical. Loop B's audited/unaudited finding rests on the absence of the distinction in twelve sources surveyed, not on positive evidence that audit-attestation has been tested and failed. Loop C's "audit-extended prose cannot separate rhetorical from logical force" is theoretical (Docherty & Smith and the cycle's synthesis) rather than empirically measured for AI-agent-produced prose specifically. The Liu et al. ARA 72.4%→93.7% figure is a single April 2026 preprint, not peer-reviewed, on a particular benchmark set. The R2 corpus-consumption analysis is over one project's corpus (16 essays, this codebase's downstream artifacts), not generalizable to other RDD instantiations without re-running the check.

The cycle-as-instance reflection (ADR-087 §4 standing caveat) applies in full force here. This cycle's findings on outline-vs-prose are based on one cycle's evidence: one source essay derived to outline; one comparative audit; one corpus consumption check; one user's stated motivation. The cumulative-payoff structure of the methodology will be tested against the world over multiple subsequent cycles. The findings are research-not-validation until tested across additional research entries. The cycle treats the evidence as sufficient signal to inform DECIDE's deliberation, but not as statistical proof or as final settlement of the artifact-form question.

Several open questions remain. The dogfooding experiment — producing both essay and outline forms of this cycle's deliverable in parallel context-isolated subagents — generates evidence that §5 of this essay reports. The two forms have been independently citation-audited and argument-audited, and the comparison feeds Q1 directly. The outcome of that comparison is documented in §5 and is itself a finding for the cycle. A second open question: whether the outline-production discipline can reliably preserve synthesis statements without explicit scaffolding (template, checklist, or audit-time check for synthesis-bullet presence). A third: whether reflections, which R2 flagged as inherently narrative, should remain in prose form or be migrated. A fourth: whether the gate-prep "minimally susceptible" operationalization (sycophancy susceptibility + AI-prose-critique susceptibility) needs to be carried forward as a standing evaluation rubric for future research artifacts. These open questions are not blockers for DECIDE's deliberation; they are the natural follow-on work that an artifact-form decision generates.
