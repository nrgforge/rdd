# Reflections: The Outline as Exhibition

## The Feedback Loop Between Synthesis and Research

The essay treats synthesis as a terminal phase — the end of the RDD cycle. But the reflection surfaced something the essay only gestures at: synthesis is itself a mode of inquiry. Finding compelling ways to situate the work — choosing a form, an audience, a medium — is not merely packaging. It generates new questions that feed back into research. The act of trying to tell the story as an algorithm, or to a child, or as a gallery walk, may reveal gaps in understanding, unexplored connections, or latent tensions that the build phase never surfaced.

This suggests synthesis is not always terminal. It can be a re-entry point — a way of discovering what the next RDD cycle should investigate. The outline-as-exhibition is not just the end product; it is a diagnostic tool that reveals what the curator does not yet understand about their own collection.

## Discovery Comes From the Outside

The writer's background in ensemble-based theater — Viewpoints, Laban, devising practices with SITI Company, Double Edge Theatre, Frantic Assembly — illuminates why the curatorial and indeterminate framings resonate. In devising practice, the compelling material emerges not from planning but from physical and structural experimentation. "It's the act of doing something outside of your head that gets interesting results. The discovery always comes from the outside."

This is a stronger claim than the essay makes. The essay proposes that unexpected forms *can* generate content the writer wouldn't have found otherwise (Miller & Paola's hermit crab principle). The devising tradition says something more radical: that this is the *primary* mechanism of creative discovery. You don't think your way to the interesting structure and then execute it. You create unexpected structures — through constraint, juxtaposition, physical experimentation — and the interesting content reveals itself through the doing.

For synthesis, this means the framing conversation should not be purely discursive. The conversation itself could involve *trying on* forms — drafting the opening paragraph as a recipe, sketching the talk as an Ignite deck, arranging the artifacts as a gallery walk — not to commit to any of them but to see what each reveals. The form is a lens, and you don't know what you'll see through it until you look.

## Software as Wunderkammer

An unresolved question: "What kind of software can we make when we think about it this way?" The essay stays within the frame of writing and presenting. But the curatorial sensibility — juxtaposition, scale shifts, the shimmer, negative space — could inform software design itself, not just the communication of software decisions. What would a codebase look like that was designed for wonder? What would an API look like that created productive shimmering between what the user expects and what the system actually does? This is unexplored territory that sits at the intersection of the research-engineer-writer stakeholder's three identities.

## The Museum of Jurassic Technology as Lived Experience

The Museum of Jurassic Technology was not a research finding — it was a memory. The writer had visited the museum and recently read about it, and threw it into the conversation as an intuition. The fact that it integrated the research more coherently than any of the frameworks the research surfaced is itself an instance of the principle: the discovery came from outside the planned investigation. The most integrative concept was not found through systematic search but through the writer's own curatorial act of placing a personal reference next to the research material and seeing what meaning emerged from the juxtaposition.
